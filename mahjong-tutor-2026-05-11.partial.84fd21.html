Lesson 2
Game Flow
From the deal to declaring Mahjong — the sequence of every turn
The Two Phases of Play

American Mahjong has two distinct phases. Understanding this flow is essential before anything else.

PhaseWhat HappensWhen It Ends
1. The CharlestonPlayers pass unwanted tiles in a prescribed sequence to build better starting handsAfter mandatory first Charleston + optional second
2. Pick & DiscardPlayers take turns drawing from the wall and discarding; others may "call" discardsWhen a player declares Mahjong (winning hand)
A Turn in Detail
Turn Sequence (Pick & Discard Phase)
  1. Draw — Pick one tile from the wall (the face-down supply)
  2. Evaluate — Does this tile help your hand? Does it complete a winning hand?
  3. Declare Mahjong (if complete) — Expose your tiles and announce "Mahj!"
  4. Discard — Place one tile face-up in the center. Other players may now call it.
Calling Discards

After any player discards, other players may claim that tile — but only under these conditions:

Call TypeWhen AllowedEffect
MahjongThe discard completes your winning handYou win the game immediately
Kong / Pung / QuintDiscard completes a group in your hand (4 of a kind, 3 of a kind, 5 with jokers)You take the tile, expose the group, skip drawing, discard a tile
Important Rule — No Calls for Pairs or Chows Unlike Chinese/Hong Kong Mahjong, in American NMJL you cannot call a discarded tile to complete a pair or a chow (sequential run). You can only call for pungs, kongs, quints, or to win.
Joker Exchange

If an opponent has an exposed group (called pung, kong, etc.) containing a Joker, and you hold the real tile it represents, you may swap the Joker out on your turn before drawing. The Joker returns to you and can be used anywhere. This is called a Joker Exchange.

End of Game — No Winner

If all wall tiles are exhausted without anyone winning, the game is a "wall game" — no points change hands and East re-deals.

Lesson 4
The Charleston
The tile-passing phase unique to American Mahjong — your best opportunity to shape a winning hand
What is the Charleston?

The Charleston is a structured tile exchange that occurs before pick-and-discard begins. Players pass 3 tiles at a time in a specified direction. The entire first Charleston (3 passes) is mandatory. The second Charleston (3 more passes) is optional — any player can stop it before it starts, unless tiles have already been looked at.

The ROLLOR Mnemonic The pass directions follow: Right — Over — Left — Left — Over — Right
First Charleston: Right, Over, Left (mandatory)
Second Charleston: Left, Over, Right (optional, unanimous agreement required)
Charleston Pass Sequence
Pass #DirectionMandatory?Blind Pass Allowed?
1RightYesNo
2Over (across)YesNo
3LeftYesYes (3rd pass of 1st Charleston)
4LeftOptionalNo
5OverOptionalNo
6RightOptionalYes (6th pass)
7Courtesy PassOptionalNo — negotiated 1-3 tiles
The Blind Pass

During the 3rd pass (left, first Charleston) and the 6th pass (right, second Charleston), you may choose to "blind pass" some or all of the incoming tiles before looking at them.

When to Blind Pass Blind pass when your hand is strong and you do not want to disrupt it. Set aside 1-3 tiles you are certain you don't want, then slide the unseen incoming tiles (without looking) to the next player to make up the 3-tile total. This prevents you from accidentally becoming attached to good tiles you just received — which you would then have to give up anyway.
Courtesy Pass

After the second Charleston ends, the player across from you may offer a Courtesy Pass — an optional exchange of 1-3 tiles between just the two of you. It is entirely voluntary. If your opponent passed blind earlier, they likely have a strong hand; decline the Courtesy Pass to avoid helping them.

Scoring
How points are calculated, paid, and what makes some hands worth more
American NMJL Scoring Overview

Unlike Chinese Mahjong (where points are tallied tile by tile), American NMJL assigns fixed point values to each winning hand printed on the NMJL card. The winner collects from all players — amounts depend on how the win occurred.

ScenarioPayment Rule
Win on self-drawn tile (from the wall)All three other players pay the hand's full value
Win on a discardOnly the player who discarded pays — they pay double the hand's value
Win at end of Charleston"Earthly Hand" or "Heavenly Hand" — special rules apply (all pay double)
Wall game (no winner)No payment — push/redeal
Concealed vs. Exposed Hands A "concealed" hand means all tiles were drawn from the wall or received via Charleston — no tiles were claimed from discards (except the final winning tile). Some NMJL hands can only be won concealed and are worth more.

Lesson 6
Strategy Basics
The core decisions that determine whether you win or lose most games
The Three Fundamental Decisions
1. Hand Selection
Before the Charleston ends, commit to 2-3 candidate hands. After pass 3, commit to one. The fewer tiles you still need, the stronger your hand choice.
2. Tile Priority
Pairs (Joker-proof), Flowers, and tiles for your specific target hand take highest priority. Lone tiles that fit no candidate hand should be passed first.
3. Defensive Play
Watch opponents' discards. If someone passes a tile you need, they likely don't need it — and vice versa. Dangerous tiles to discard late-game: tiles no one has discarded yet.
Reading the Table

Because discards are visible, skilled players constantly gather information:

What You SeeWhat It Tells You
Opponent discards all Flowers earlyThey are not playing a Flower-heavy hand
Opponent calls a 6-Dot then discards all other DotsThey likely need 6-Dots in multiples — be careful with your 6-Dots
No one has discarded JokersEveryone is holding Jokers — Jokers are scarce this game
Many Wind tiles in discard pileWind-heavy NMJL hands are being avoided by most players
Switching Hands

The golden rule: switch your target hand by your 4th or 5th turn if you are still 4+ tiles away. After that, the wall is depleting too fast to change direction. Signs you should switch:

  • Key tiles you need are appearing in opponents' discards (so they exist, but others are drawing them)
  • You keep drawing tiles that fit a different hand pattern better
  • An opponent has called 2+ tiles (they may win soon — defensive urgency)
Joker Management
Golden Rules for Jokers (1) Never pass Jokers in the Charleston. (2) Use Jokers to complete pungs, kongs, or quints only — never "waste" a Joker on a pung if you might need it for a quint. (3) Exchange opponents' Jokers whenever you hold the real tile — this also disrupts their hand. (4) The fewer Jokers in your hand at game end, the more flexible your building was — experienced players often win with 1-2 Jokers.

Intermediate Play
Multi-hand tracking, probability thinking, and table dynamics
Tracking Multiple Candidates

Strong intermediate players maintain 2-3 "live" candidate hands mentally at all times until the mid-game, then pivot decisively. The framework:

StageHow Many CandidatesPriority Action
After deal (pre-Charleston)3-5 categoriesSort tiles, identify which NMJL sections fit
During Charleston2-3 specific handsPass tiles that fit no candidate hand
Turns 1-32 handsDraw toward both; start noting opponent discards
Turn 4-51 hand (committed)Pure defensive/offensive play toward that hand
Probability Basics
Key Numbers to Internalize There are 4 copies of every numbered tile (except Flowers and Jokers). If you need a specific tile and you have 0 copies, the tile can come from 4 locations. If you see 2 in the discard pile, only 2 remain in the wall or opponents' hands. This "live tile counting" is the foundation of intermediate probability thinking.

A quick mental model:

Copies seen in discardsCopies remainingDrawing probability (approx.)
0 discarded4 liveHigh — tile is in play somewhere
1 discarded3 liveGood
2 discarded2 liveModerate — consider alternative hands
3 discarded1 liveLow — this tile is almost gone
4 discarded0 liveZero — your hand relying on this tile is dead
Reading Opponents' Exposures

When an opponent calls a tile and exposes a group, you gain valuable information. Use it:

Exposed Pung of 4-Bam
They need tiles that go with 4-Bam — likely 2-Bam/6-Bam for runs, or more 4s. Stop discarding 2B, 4B, 6B.
Exposed Kong of Flowers
They are likely building a Flower-heavy hand. Flowers become extremely dangerous to discard.
Two Exposures by Same Player
They only need 1-2 more groups. Play defensively — prioritize safe discards over hand advancement.
The "Dead Hand" Awareness

If you realize mid-game that your hand is impossible to complete (tiles you need are all in discards), do not declare your hand dead. Continue playing, discarding safely (tiles that are already heavily discarded), and blocking opponents from winning.

Lesson 8
Advanced Tactics
Table control, bluffing through discards, Joker hunting, and winning with high-value hands
High-Value Hand Selection

Advanced players deliberately pursue harder hands when the tile distribution supports it. Key reasons to attempt a 50+ point hand:

  • You drew 2+ Jokers early
  • Your starting deal gives you 8+ tiles for the target hand
  • The table dynamic suggests opponents are fighting over common tiles (less competition for rarer tiles in your hard hand)
Controlling Your Discards
Misleading Opponents Your discard pile signals your hand strategy. Advanced players deliberately discard tiles that suggest a different hand from the one they're building. Example: if building a Bam-heavy hand, early discard a few Bam tiles to signal "I'm not in Bam" — then pivot back once you've received the key Bam tiles.

Equally important: track what tiles you haven't discarded. An opponent seeing you hold every tile of one suit knows you're building in that suit.

Joker Hunting

Actively seeking Joker exchanges from opponents' exposed hands is an advanced weapon:

ScenarioActionBenefit
Opponent has exposed Kong with JokerExchange with your real tile on your turnYou gain a Joker; opponent's group is unchanged but weaker
Multiple opponents have exposed JokersPrioritize exchanging the most dangerous opponent's JokerReduces the leading player's versatility
You hold a tile that matches multiple opponents' exposed groupsTarget the opponent with fewest tiles remaining to winDisrupts the most imminent threat
The "Safe Discard" System
Tier 1 — Safest
Tiles that have been discarded 3-4 times already. No one could be waiting for them. Discard these with confidence.
Tier 2 — Moderate
Tiles discarded 1-2 times. Probably safe but possible someone still needs one. Use if no Tier 1 options exist.
Tier 3 — Dangerous
Tiles not yet discarded (live tiles). Any of the 4 copies may be in an opponent's hand. Avoid late game unless absolutely necessary.
Tier 4 — Suicidal
Tiles that match an opponent's exposed group pattern. Directly feeds their winning hand. Never discard these if you can help it.
When to Play It Safe vs. Go for the Kill
SituationRecommended Approach
You are 1 tile from winning, no opponent has exposed groupsDiscard any tile — press for the win
You are 1 tile, one opponent also has 2 exposuresOnly discard safe tiles; avoid feeding opponent
You are 3+ tiles from winning, game is in late roundsShift entirely to defense — protect against giving others the win
Wall game seems likelyHold all dangerous tiles, accept the push
