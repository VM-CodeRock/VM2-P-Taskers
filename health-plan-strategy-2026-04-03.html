Overview

Executive Summary

Changeis Inc. is at a critical inflection point. With 185 employees, an aging workforce, rising Cigna fully-insured premiums, and a government contracting business model that demands both cost discipline and audit-proof compliance, the status quo carries compounding financial risk. This report synthesizes research on three alternative health plan structures — fully-insured (current), ICHRA, and self-funded — through the unique lens of a government contractor subject to FAR, DCAA, and SCA requirements.

Key Findings
  • Healthcare costs are accelerating. The KFF 2025 Employer Health Benefits Survey shows average family premiums reached $26,993 in 2025 (+6%), and Mercer projects a 6.7% increase in 2026 — the highest in 15 years. At that trajectory, doing nothing adds $330K+ in employer costs over two years.
  • Changeis is large enough to self-fund. At 185 employees, the firm sits squarely at the industry consensus threshold of 150–200 employees where self-funding becomes viable, with potential savings of $95K–$400K+ annually depending on claims experience.
  • ICHRA is growing fast in this segment. ICHRA adoption among employers with 100–199 employees grew 49% in 2025 (HRA Council 2025 Report), with a 92% renewal rate — signaling strong employer satisfaction.
  • The GovCon dimension changes everything. Self-funded plans trigger FAR 28.308 ACO approval, mandatory CAS 416 compliance, and complex SCA bona fide plan requirements that do not apply to fully-insured or ICHRA structures (FAR 31.205-19). Failure to comply renders all self-insurance costs retroactively unallowable.
  • A phased approach reduces risk. Starting with level-funded coverage to generate claims data, while simultaneously evaluating ICHRA for SCA-covered employees, provides the best risk-adjusted path to long-term savings.

Bottom line recommendation: Changeis should pursue a phased transition — beginning with claims data collection and level-funding evaluation immediately, targeting a hybrid model (self-funded or level-funded for professional staff, ICHRA for SCA-covered contract employees) for the January 2027 plan year. This approach maximizes savings potential while managing GovCon compliance risk.

Current Landscape and Why This Matters Now

Employer-sponsored health insurance costs have entered a period of sustained acceleration that particularly punishes mid-sized firms on fully-insured plans with aging workforces.

The cost trajectory is unsustainable. Per the KFF 2024 Employer Health Benefits Survey, average single premiums increased 6% and family premiums increased 7% in 2024, both outpacing wage growth (4.5%) and inflation (3.2%). The 2025 KFF survey shows family premiums at $26,993, and Mercer's National Survey projects the average total health benefit cost per employee will exceed $18,500 in 2026 — up from $17,496 in 2025.

An aging workforce is a cost multiplier. Per SSA actuarial research, employees aged 55–64 cost more than twice as much as those aged 25–34 in health benefits. The KFF 2024 survey confirms that firms with 35%+ of workers age 50+ face single premiums 5% above average ($9,171 vs. $8,738). For Changeis, with a workforce skewing older, this aging penalty compounds with each renewal cycle.

Prescription drug costs are surging. Mercer 2025 survey data shows prescription drug costs increased 9.4% in 2025 among large employers, with pharmacy costs now representing 20–30% of total healthcare spend. GLP-1 medications alone can cost $12,000–$16,000 per patient annually — and 49% of large employers now cover them.

185 employees is a decision inflection point. This size is large enough for meaningful actuarial credibility in claims experience, large enough for ICHRA TPAs to serve cost-effectively, and right at the threshold where 67% of covered workers nationally are already in self-funded plans. Remaining on a fully-insured plan at this size means paying for the carrier's profit margin, risk charge, and premium taxes — costs that self-funded and ICHRA alternatives can eliminate.

Section 2

Plan Type Fundamentals

Fully-Insured (Current State)

Changeis currently pays Cigna a fixed monthly premium per employee. Cigna bears all claims risk, processes claims, manages the PPO network, and handles most compliance. The employer's role is limited to enrollment administration and paying premiums.

What the employer is paying for:

  • Actual expected claims (~85% of premium, per KFF MLR data)
  • Carrier administrative costs (6–9% of premium)
  • Carrier profit and risk margin (5–7% of premium)
  • State premium taxes (2–3% of premium)
  • Broker commissions (typically embedded)

Estimated current Changeis employer cost: $2.1M–$2.7M annually (employer share after employee contributions), based on KFF 2024 benchmark data for a 185-employee firm with a mixed single/family enrollment.

Pros:

  • Simplicity and predictability — costs are known at start of year
  • Low compliance burden — carrier handles most ERISA, HIPAA, and ACA administration
  • Strongest SCA bona fide plan status — premium payments automatically satisfy 29 CFR § 4.171 irrevocable contribution requirement
  • Lowest DCAA audit risk — DCAA Chapter 34 treatment is straightforward
  • No FAR 28.308 ACO approval required

Cons:

  • No claims data access — Cigna controls the data, making it impossible to understand cost drivers or model alternatives
  • No cost control levers — plan design, formulary, and network are carrier-determined
  • Carrier profit is built in — approximately $255,000/year in carrier profit and overhead for 185 employees, per Health Tech Stack analysis
  • Renewal shock risk — after a high-claims year, renewals of 10–20% are common
  • Favorable claims experience accrues to the carrier, not the employer

ICHRA (Individual Coverage HRA)

ICHRA, created by federal regulations effective January 1, 2020, is a defined contribution health benefit. The employer sets a monthly allowance, and employees use it to purchase their own individual health insurance on the ACA marketplace or off-exchange.

How it works — four steps:

  1. Employer designs the plan: Sets allowance amounts by employee class, defines eligible expenses, selects a TPA
  2. Employee enrollment: Employees receive a mandatory 90-day advance notice (October 3 for January 1 start), then shop for individual coverage during Open Enrollment or a Special Enrollment Period
  3. Claims submission: Employees pay premiums and submit receipts or attestation to the TPA
  4. Reimbursement: TPA reimburses employees tax-free up to their allowance amount

Key structural features:

  • 11 permissible employee classes (full-time, part-time, salaried, hourly, geographic, etc.) with different allowances per class (Ameriflex)
  • No contribution limits — unlike QSEHRA ($6,450/year cap), ICHRA has no maximum (Take Command)
  • ACA mandate compliance through affordability safe harbors — 2026 threshold is 9.96% of income (Lift HCM)
  • Age-based allowance variation up to a 3:1 ratio from youngest to oldest (Remodel Health)

TPAs serving this segment:

  • Remodel Health — enterprise focus, HIPAA/HITRUST/SOC2 certified, direct carrier premium payment (Remodel Health)
  • Take Command Health — broad marketplace, licensed specialists, HRIS integrations (Take Command)
  • Venteur — SOC 2/FISMA/HIPAA certified, concierge enrollment (Remodel Health comparison)
  • Zorro — AI-powered plan recommendations, enterprise focus (Zorro)
  • Typical cost: $15–$30 PEPM in administration fees (~$44K–$67K/year for 185 employees)

Tax treatment: Employer contributions are fully tax-deductible and FICA-exempt (saving ~7.65% in payroll taxes). Employee reimbursements are income-tax-free and payroll-tax-free (RSM US).

Adoption stats: ICHRA adoption grew 49% among employers with 100–199 employees in 2025, with a 92% employer renewal rate (HRA Council 2025 Report). Approximately 500,000 to 1 million lives are now covered by ICHRA or QSEHRA nationally (Alegeus).

Case studies:

  • A 154-employee engineering firm across 3 states adopted ICHRA after "paralyzing" group rate increases and used savings to fund raises and additional benefits (ICHRA.com)
  • A 200-life employer facing a 20% medical increase restructured to ICHRA, achieving $500,000 in premium savings (BBG)
  • Rush Memorial Hospital (~400 employees) saved $2.25 million annually after switching to ICHRA through Zorro, with employees gaining access to 60 plans from 6 carriers (Zorro)

Self-Funded

In a self-funded plan, the employer assumes direct financial responsibility for paying employee healthcare claims. A TPA processes claims, a PPO network provides discounts, and stop-loss insurance caps catastrophic exposure.

How it works:

  • Employer establishes a claims funding account and deposits monthly contributions
  • TPA adjudicates and pays claims from the employer's account
  • Stop-loss insurance reimburses claims above set thresholds
  • Unused funds remain the employer's property

Stop-loss mechanics:

  • Specific (individual) stop-loss: Protects against catastrophic single-member claims. Typical attachment points for 185 employees: $50,000–$150,000 per individual. At a $100,000 deductible, 2025 premiums average $229.40 PEPM (IFEBP 2025 Stop-Loss Survey)
  • Aggregate stop-loss: Triggers when total plan claims exceed 125% of expected claims (the aggregate corridor), protecting against a systemically bad year
  • Stop-loss premiums increased 8.8–10% in 2025, with further increases projected for 2026 (IFEBP)

Financial model:

  • Total cost = actual claims (variable, 70–80% of spend) + fixed costs (TPA, stop-loss, network access, PBM, broker)
  • IBNR reserves (incurred but not reported claims) typically equal 2–3.5 months of expected claims (Investopedia)
  • Savings materialize through elimination of carrier profit (2–5%), state premium tax avoidance (2–3%), and claims data-driven plan optimization

Network access: Employers can rent Cigna's PPO network through a TPA or Cigna ASO arrangement, maintaining provider continuity (Memberly Benefits). Network access fees typically run $6–$35 PEPM.

Key stats:

  • 67% of covered workers are in self-funded plans nationally in 2025 (KFF 2025), up from 63% in 2024
  • Among mid-sized firms (10–199 workers), 27% are self-funded and 37% are level-funded — meaning 64% use non-traditional funding
  • Lockton actuaries estimate 3–5% savings from switching in year 1, with 10–25% savings over years 2–5 as claims data enables plan design optimization

Level-funded as a stepping stone: Level-funded plans bundle claims funding, stop-loss, and administration into a fixed monthly payment with a potential year-end surplus refund. Precision Benefits Group calls them "self-funding on training wheels." Per KFF 2025 data, 37% of covered workers at small-to-mid firms are on level-funded plans. Savings vs. fully-insured typically run 10–20% (Ethos Benefits).

Section 5 — Critical

The GovCon Dimension

This section is what makes health plan strategy fundamentally different for government contractors. Generic broker advice that ignores FAR compliance, DCAA audit exposure, and SCA obligations can lead to catastrophic cost disallowances.

FAR 31.205-6(m) — The Allowability Test

All health insurance costs — regardless of plan type — must satisfy three requirements to be allowable on government contracts (FAR 31.205-6(m)):

  1. Reasonable — comparable to benefits provided by employers in similar circumstances
  2. Required by law, agreement, or established policy — documented in writing, communicated to employees
  3. Not otherwise disallowed — must not be rendered unallowable by another FAR 31.2 provision

FAR 31.205-19 — Self-Insurance Triggers

FAR 31.205-19 creates dramatically different compliance obligations by plan type:

  • Fully-insured: Reasonable premiums, competitive quotes, government shares in dividends/credits. Straightforward.
  • Self-funded: CAS 416 compliance mandatory (even for non-CAS-covered contractors); self-insurance charges cannot exceed cost of comparable purchased insurance; requires FAR 28.308 ACO approval. Complex.
  • ICHRA: No specific FAR provision exists. Treated as fringe benefits under FAR 31.205-6(m). The employer is not retaining insurance risk, so FAR 31.205-19(c) self-insurance provisions should not apply.

FAR 28.308 — ACO Approval Requirement (Self-Funded Only)

This is the most consequential compliance obligation. If (a) 50% or more of self-insurance costs are allocable to negotiated government contracts AND (b) annual self-insurance costs are expected to be $200,000 or more, the contractor must submit a written self-insurance program to the ACO for approval before claiming costs (FAR 28.308).

A 185-person firm paying $6,000–$10,000/year per employee in health claims would easily exceed $200,000, making FAR 28.308 compliance mandatory. The submission requires 10 specific elements including loss history, reserve formula, and claims administration procedures.

Failure to obtain ACO approval renders self-insurance costs unallowable under FAR 31.205-19(c)(2).

For SCA-covered employees on FAA service contracts, fringe benefits must qualify as "bona fide" under 29 CFR § 4.171. The current SCA H&W rate (effective July 7, 2025) is $5.55/hour = $11,544/employee/year (Hunton Andrews Kurth).

Indirect Rate Impact

Health insurance is typically the single largest component of the fringe benefit pool (30–50% of total fringe costs). The plan structure determines fringe rate predictability, which directly affects proposal pricing competitiveness:

  • Fully-insured: HIGH predictability — fixed premiums create stable fringe rates, ideal for multi-year proposals
  • Self-funded: LOW-MODERATE predictability — claims volatility creates ±5–8% fringe rate swings year-to-year, complicating provisional billing rates and proposal pricing
  • ICHRA: HIGH predictability — employer-set allowances create fringe rate stability comparable to fully-insured, with a potentially lower cost base

Competitive proposal advantage: Predictable fringe rates enable more competitive bids. Self-funded fringe rate volatility can result in either underbilling (cash flow strain) or overbilling (refund obligations) on cost-reimbursable contracts (GovDash).

Section 7

Risk Analysis

Self-Funded Worst Cases

  • Catastrophic claimant: An $800K cancer claim with $75K specific stop-loss deductible costs the employer $75K in year 1, but the stop-loss carrier may laser that individual at $400K at renewal — creating $475K employer exposure over two years (Linkenheimer CPAs)
  • Multiple mid-tier claims: 12 employees each incurring $40K–$65K — none triggering specific stop-loss — can push aggregate claims $225K above expected, within the aggregate corridor the employer absorbs
  • Stop-loss non-renewal: After bad claims years, stop-loss carriers can decline renewal or increase rates 30–50% (Lockton Q4 2025)
  • GLP-1 drug adoption: 15% of employees (28 people) on GLP-1s = $336K–$448K/year in new pharmacy spend, potentially triggering aggregate stop-loss

ICHRA Risks

  • 90-day notice deadline: The October 3 notice deadline for January 1 start is firm — missing it delays implementation an entire year
  • Affordability calculation errors: Incorrect modeling can expose the employer to ACA penalties of $5,010 per employee in 2026
  • Individual market network quality: Many individual market plans are HMOs/EPOs with narrow networks; the Peterson-KFF ICHRA Brief cites network narrowness as the most commonly cited concern
  • Employee satisfaction: One case study documented satisfaction dropping from 95% to 89% due to poor shopping support
  • PTC displacement: Lower-income SCA employees offered an affordable ICHRA lose access to ACA premium tax credits

Fully-Insured Risks

  • Renewal shock: 10–20% increases after a high-claims year, locked in for the full plan year
  • No claims transparency: inability to understand or manage cost drivers
  • Inability to control pharmacy costs as GLP-1 and specialty drug utilization grows

GovCon-Specific Risks

  • DCAA questioned costs: Self-funded plans that fail CAS 416, cost cap, or ACO approval create retroactive disallowance exposure
  • SCA bona fide status: An unfunded self-insured plan not approved by DOL creates wage deficiency findings
  • Indirect rate volatility: Self-funded claims swings create fringe rate volatility that complicates proposal pricing and provisional billing

Section 9

Recommended Path Forward

Phase 0 — Intelligence Gathering (Now through May 2026)

  • Request 24–36 months of claims data from Cigna. This is the single most critical action. Under the Consolidated Appropriations Act (2021), plan sponsors are entitled to claims data. Request: aggregate claims by month, large claimant report (>$25K), diagnosis-level data, pharmacy spend by category, and loss ratio.
  • Engage SCA-specialist legal counsel for bona fide benefit analysis across all plan types, specifically for FAA contracts.
  • Map employee geographic distribution and audit individual market quality (carrier options, PPO availability) in each employee location.
  • Survey employees on current network/plan satisfaction, doctor preferences, and receptiveness to change.
  • Determine FAR vs. FAA AMS cost principles for each contract — confirm with the cognizant ACO.

Phase 1 — Analysis and Modeling (June–July 2026)

  • Actuarial analysis of claims data to determine loss ratio, high-cost claimant risk, and demographic cost drivers.
  • Model costs for all four options: fully-insured renewal, level-funded, self-funded, and ICHRA — including GovCon-specific compliance costs.
  • Assess DCAA/FAR allowability with government contracts counsel. For ICHRA, request an advance agreement under FAR 31.109. For self-funded, begin FAR 28.308 preparation.
  • Issue RFPs to ICHRA TPAs (Remodel Health, Take Command, Zorro, Venteur) if the ICHRA path is viable.
  • Issue RFPs to independent TPAs and stop-loss carriers if the self-funded path is viable.
  • Contact DOL WHD regarding SCA bona fide status for the chosen plan structure.

Phase 2 — Decision and Design (August 2026)

  • CEO decision based on analysis, modeling, and compliance assessment.
  • Plan document development with ERISA counsel.
  • Employee communication strategy development — this is critical for change management.
  • Finalize TPA selection and execute agreements.

Phase 3 — Implementation (September–December 2026)

  • If ICHRA: 90-day notice must be issued by October 3, 2026 for a January 1, 2027 start. Develop employee shopping support materials. Enrollment during November 1–December 15 Open Enrollment.
  • If self-funded: TPA setup, stop-loss binding, network lease agreement, HIPAA program establishment, FAR 28.308 submission to ACO. Budget for $800K–$1.2M in working capital.
  • If level-funded: Lower implementation bar — carrier handles most setup; focus on employee communication.
  • Open enrollment (all paths).
  • SCA compliance documentation filed with DOL WHD and contracting officer.

The Hybrid Option to Consider

A hybrid design leverages ICHRA's class rules to address the biggest pain points of each plan type:

  • ICHRA for SCA-covered hourly/service employees — simplifies SCA H&W compliance, provides geographic flexibility, offers predictable per-employee cost, and eliminates the complexity of DOL unfunded self-insured plan approval
  • Self-funded (or level-funded) for professional/exempt staff — maintains PPO network access critical for retention, provides claims data for cost management, and retains plan design control

This approach uses ICHRA's 11 permissible employee classes (salaried vs. hourly, geographic) to create distinct benefit structures. When offering both ICHRA and a group plan, the minimum class size for a 185-employee firm is 19 employees (10% of total) for "sensitive" classes.

Section 10

Key Questions to Ask Your Broker

These questions are designed to hold your broker accountable and surface information that may not be proactively shared:

  1. "Can you get me 24–36 months of claims data from Cigna, including diagnosis-level detail and large claimant reports?" If the broker cannot or will not, consider whether they are aligned with your interests.
  2. "What is our actual loss ratio on the Cigna plan?" This is the single most important number for the self-funding decision. A loss ratio below 80% means Cigna is profiting significantly from the Changeis account.
  3. "Have you modeled ICHRA specifically for a government contractor with SCA obligations?" Most brokers have limited ICHRA experience and zero GovCon expertise. If they cannot speak to SCA bona fide plan requirements, engage specialist counsel independently.
  4. "What are the specific stop-loss terms you would recommend, and have you obtained quotes from at least three stop-loss carriers?" Brokers sometimes steer clients to captive carriers. Insist on competitive stop-loss marketing.
  5. "What happens if one of our employees has an $800K claim — walk me through the cash flow impact month by month under each scenario." This tests whether the broker has done proper catastrophic scenario modeling.
  6. "How does FAR 28.308 ACO approval work, and what is your experience obtaining it for self-funded health plans?" If the broker's answer is blank, they are not qualified to advise a government contractor on self-funding.
  7. "For ICHRA, what is the individual market quality (number of carriers, PPO availability) in each zip code where our employees live?" This is the ICHRA feasibility test. If most employees are in thin markets with only HMO options, ICHRA is less attractive.
  8. "What commission or compensation do you receive from Cigna, and would that change if we moved to self-funded or ICHRA?" Transparency about broker incentives matters. Some brokers receive higher commissions from fully-insured carriers.
  9. "Have you considered a hybrid approach — ICHRA for one class of employees and a group plan for another?" Tests whether the broker understands ICHRA class rules and can design creative solutions.
  10. "What is the level-funded option from Cigna, and what claims data would we receive?" Cigna offers a level-funded product for groups of 25–999 employees — this may be the lowest-disruption path to generating claims data while preserving the Cigna network relationship.
  11. "What is the ICHRA TPA marketplace quality? Have you evaluated Remodel Health, Take Command, Zorro, and Venteur for our size and geographic footprint?" The ICHRA TPA selection is as important as the carrier selection in a traditional plan.
  12. "How would switching plan types affect our indirect rates and proposal competitiveness on FAA contracts?" This is the question that connects health plan strategy to revenue strategy.
  13. "What is the total embedded cost in our Cigna premium — carrier profit, risk charge, premium tax, and broker commission — that we could recapture by moving to an alternative?" Forces the broker to quantify the savings opportunity honestly.
  14. "For our aging workforce, have you modeled the age-rating impact on ICHRA allowances needed to maintain ACA affordability?" Critical for ensuring ICHRA allowances are sufficient for older employees in the individual market.
  15. "If we proceed with self-funded, can you coordinate the FAR 28.308 submission and CAS 416 methodology with our government contracts CPA?" Tests whether the broker can collaborate with GovCon advisors — essential for Changeis.
