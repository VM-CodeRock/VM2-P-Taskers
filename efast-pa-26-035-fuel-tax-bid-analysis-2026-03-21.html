Program Background

The FAA ARP Compliance and Management Analysis Division (ACO), Airports Financial Compliance Division (ACO-200), enforces statutory requirements of airport revenue use at federally obligated airports. Under the FAA Revenue Use Policy (79 FR 66282, published November 7, 2014), states and other affected jurisdictions that impose taxes on aviation fuel must submit action plans to FAA/ACO/ACO-200 demonstrating that fuel tax proceeds are used either for a State aviation program or for airport-related purposes.

This contract provides ACO-200 with the contractor support needed to administer, track, review, correspond, and report on this multi-jurisdictional fuel tax compliance program — spanning all 50 states and applicable territories. The program involves managing incoming action plans and corrective action plans from noncompliant jurisdictions, conducting compliance analysis, and maintaining stakeholder correspondence across a broad external audience of state and local governments.

BAM Functional Area Assessment

This opportunity is placed under the Business Administrative & Management (BAM) functional area. BAM tasks typically involve management analysis, administrative support, business process consulting, and program support — as opposed to technical IT or engineering work. Changeis's core expertise in program management, business process consulting, and compliance support maps directly to BAM.

However, Changeis is primarily known in the eFAST ecosystem as a technology and IT modernization firm. The BAM positioning means the winning proposal must deliberately frame Changeis's capabilities in business/management analytical terms, not technology terms. The proposal should lead with business process expertise, regulatory analysis, and compliance support — with technology capabilities presented as efficiency-enhancers, not the primary value proposition.

Domain Knowledge Gap: Aviation Fuel Tax Compliance
Aviation fuel tax compliance under the FAA Revenue Use Policy is a niche program area. The contractor must understand: (1) how states assess and collect aviation fuel taxes, (2) the statutory requirements of 49 U.S.C. § 47133 and related provisions, (3) how corrective action plan review works within ACO-200's enforcement framework, and (4) the escalation pathway for noncompliant jurisdictions. Changeis likely has ARP context from other eFAST work, but specific fuel tax program expertise may need to be obtained through teaming or key personnel with relevant backgrounds. This gap can be partially offset by a strong Technical Approach that demonstrates research rigor and a credible learning ramp.

Teaming Strategy Considerations

If Changeis identifies a gap in aviation fuel tax domain knowledge or economics expertise, a targeted teaming strategy could strengthen the proposal. However, per Attachment C Section 4.1, teaming arrangements are explicitly flagged as a performance risk by the Government. The following principles should guide any teaming decision:

  • Changeis as Prime is essential — the eFAST MOA holder must be the prime. Changeis should perform the majority (ideally 60%+) of the work by dollar value
  • Subcontractor for Junior Economist role only — if Changeis cannot source an in-house Junior Economist with aviation economics credentials, a targeted teaming arrangement for that LCAT may be defensible
  • No "spreadsheet teaming" — the Government assesses risk when prime workshare is significantly less than subcontractor workshare. Any teaming must be proportional
  • Subcontractor corporate experience is weighted equally — if a teaming partner brings relevant corporate experience (aviation fuel tax, ACO work), it can strengthen the Corporate Experience section
