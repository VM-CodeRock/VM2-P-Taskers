Solitude and Leadership

A leadership development brief for Changeis Inc., based on William Deresiewicz's West Point lecture

March 21, 2026 | Source: The American Scholar | Original: March 2010 | Leadership Brief

Executive Summary

Executive Summary

William Deresiewicz's 2010 lecture at West Point, published in The American Scholar, argues that the capacity for genuine leadership depends not on credentials, hierarchy-climbing, or multitasking, but on solitude — the disciplined practice of thinking for oneself. Against the grain of a culture saturated with social media, bureaucratic conformity, and distraction, Deresiewicz contends that leaders who cannot think independently will merely perpetuate what already exists. True leadership requires vision, moral courage, and self-knowledge, all of which are only developed through time spent alone with one's own thoughts.

For Changeis Inc. — a small federal IT consulting firm guiding FAA aviation modernization — this essay carries direct operational relevance. In federal contracting environments defined by bureaucracy, compliance rhythms, and agency-client deference, the leaders who create lasting value are those who think independently, ask the questions that clients have not thought to ask, and hold their convictions under institutional pressure. The principles in this lecture map directly onto the leadership challenges at Changeis, from project management to executive client engagement.

About the Author and Context

William Deresiewicz

William Deresiewicz is an American essayist and literary critic. He spent ten years as a professor and lecturer at Yale University before leaving academia. His 2014 book Excellent Sheep: The Miseducation of the American Elite and the Way to a Meaningful Life expanded on the arguments first developed in this lecture. He is a contributing editor of The American Scholar.

The West Point Lecture

In October 2009, Deresiewicz was invited to address the plebe class — the incoming freshman cohort — at the United States Military Academy at West Point, New York. The lecture was subsequently published in The American Scholar in March 2010 under the title "Solitude and Leadership." It has since become one of the most widely read and cited essays on leadership development in American public life, assigned at business schools, military academies, and executive education programs.

Why West Point

Deresiewicz chose his audience deliberately. West Point, like the Ivy League institutions where he had taught, trains people who will occupy positions of authority. By addressing cadets who were about to enter one of the largest, most hierarchical bureaucracies in the world — the United States Army — he was able to use their situation as a concrete case study for the broader question of how institutions shape, and often deform, leadership character. The lecture's direct address to military officers gives it a sharpness and urgency that general management writing rarely achieves.

Core Argument: Solitude as a Prerequisite for Leadership

Deresiewicz opens by acknowledging the apparent contradiction in his title. Solitude implies being alone; leadership implies commanding others. How can the two be related?

His answer proceeds in several linked steps:

  1. Most institutions reward conformity, not leadership. The people who rise to the top of bureaucracies are frequently not the most capable but the most adept at maneuvering — at pleasing superiors, following procedures, and avoiding risk. Deresiewicz calls this the "greasy pole" problem.
  2. True leadership requires independent thought. A leader who can only execute on pre-existing plans is a technician, not a leader. Vision — the capacity to identify new directions, ask new questions, and challenge the conventional wisdom — is what distinguishes leaders from high-performing followers.
  3. Independent thought requires solitude. You cannot think for yourself while marinating continuously in other people's opinions. The constant stimulation of social media, group conversation, and institutional noise forecloses the deep, patient, iterative thinking from which original ideas emerge.
  4. Solitude is therefore not optional for leaders — it is essential. "Solitude is the very essence of leadership," Deresiewicz concludes. "The position of the leader is ultimately an intensely solitary, even intensely lonely one. However many people you may consult, you are the one who has to make the hard decisions. And at such moments, all you really have is yourself."
Central Thesis

Solitude is not a retreat from leadership but the cognitive and moral precondition for it. A leader who has never learned to be alone with their own thoughts cannot think independently, cannot develop genuine convictions, and cannot exercise moral courage when institutions demand conformity.

Key Themes

1. The Bureaucracy Problem

Using Joseph Conrad's Heart of Darkness as a lens, Deresiewicz describes the defining pathology of institutional life: the people who rise to the top are often mediocre, distinguished only by their ability to navigate hierarchy. The manager of Conrad's Central Station — "commonplace in complexion, in features, in manners" — is a perfect portrait of someone who has mastered institutional politics without any substantive capacity to lead. Deresiewicz argues this pattern is universal: it applies equally to corporations, universities, government agencies, and the military.

2. Excellence Is Not Leadership

High achievement — perfect grades, impressive credentials, extracurricular abundance — does not produce leaders. It produces what Deresiewicz calls "excellent sheep": people who can clear any bar set before them but who cannot set bars for themselves. Leadership requires setting direction, not just executing on it. Yale and West Point alike, he argues, have confused the two.

3. Thinking for Yourself

The heart of leadership, Deresiewicz argues, is the capacity to form one's own views. This is harder than it sounds. Most people's first thoughts are inherited — they reflect what they have already heard, the conventional wisdom, the peer consensus. Original thinking requires sustained concentration: staying with a question long enough for the mind to make novel connections. This process cannot be rushed and cannot occur amid constant interruption.

4. Multitasking Destroys Thinking

Deresiewicz cites a Stanford research study finding that heavy multitaskers are worse at every cognitive task measured — including multitasking itself. They are more distractible, less organized, and less able to switch between tasks effectively. His conclusion: "Multitasking, in short, is not only not thinking, it impairs your ability to think." Concentration — giving sustained attention to one thing — is the mechanism by which real thinking occurs.

5. Moral Courage

Physical bravery, Deresiewicz notes, is something West Point cadets possess in abundance. What is harder and rarer is moral courage: the willingness to stand up for what one believes against institutional pressure, peer consensus, and the disapproval of superiors. He uses General David Petraeus as his primary example of a leader who possessed both kinds of courage — and who paid a professional price for it before being vindicated.

6. Concentration vs. Distraction

Deresiewicz treats social media, television, and the constant stream of digital information as forms of flight — ways of avoiding the difficult internal questions that genuine adulthood and genuine leadership require. "It seems to me that Facebook and Twitter and YouTube ... are all ultimately just an elaborate excuse to run away from yourself." The questions one avoids through distraction — about purpose, belief, identity, and ethics — are precisely the questions a leader must have answered before the moment of decision arrives.

7. Forms of Solitude

Deresiewicz broadens the definition of solitude beyond simple isolation. He identifies four forms:

  • Introspection — being alone with one's own thoughts, examining beliefs, values, and purposes
  • Concentrated work — focused manual or intellectual labor that engages the self fully (Marlow repairing his steamboat)
  • Sustained reading — especially of old books, which resist the conventional wisdom of the present moment
  • Deep friendship — long, uninterrupted conversation with one trusted person, a form of "thinking out loud" that mirrors internal dialogue

8. Self-Knowledge as Leadership Foundation

Deresiewicz argues that leaders must know who they are and what they believe before they face the situations that will test those beliefs. "You need to know, already, who you are and what you believe: not what the Army believes, not what your peers believe ... but what you believe." Waiting until a crisis to figure out one's convictions is like waiting until a firefight to learn to shoot.

Notable Examples and Anecdotes

Conrad's Heart of Darkness — The Manager of the Central Station

Deresiewicz devotes considerable attention to Conrad's description of the manager: "commonplace in complexion, in features, in manners, and in voice ... He had no genius for organizing, for initiative, or for order even ... He originated nothing, he could keep the routine going — that's all." Deresiewicz confesses that it took him ten readings of this passage to recognize it as a perfect portrait of a department chair he knew at Yale — and, he implies, of a type that recurs throughout every institution. The manager's characteristic weapon is unease: making subordinates feel they are doing something wrong without ever specifying what. This subtle coercion enforces conformity without requiring any articulable standard.

General David Petraeus

Deresiewicz holds up Petraeus as evidence that genuine leaders can rise through bureaucracy — but only by accepting personal cost. As commander of the 101st Airborne in Mosul in 2003, Petraeus developed the counterinsurgency approach that would later become official doctrine. At the time, it infuriated his superiors. He was reassigned to train the Iraqi army — widely seen as a career dead-end. He persisted. The lecture uses Petraeus to argue that moral courage, not political skill, is what ultimately distinguishes a leader from a maneuverer. Significantly, Petraeus holds a Princeton Ph.D., but Deresiewicz notes that advanced degrees do not make thinkers: what made Petraeus a thinker was the discipline of thinking for himself and the courage to act on it.

Marlow Repairing the Steamboat

Deresiewicz reads a passage from Heart of Darkness in which Marlow escapes the social chaos of the Central Station by throwing himself into repairing a damaged steamboat. This is offered as an illustration of solitude through concentrated work. "No influential friend would have served me better. She had given me a chance to come out a bit — to find out what I could do ... the chance to find yourself. Your own reality — for yourself, not for others — what no other man can ever know." The point is that solitary, focused labor — even physical labor — is a form of self-discovery that social interaction cannot replicate.

The Stanford Multitasking Study

Deresiewicz cites a Stanford research study whose findings surprised even its own investigators. The study compared high and low multitaskers on cognitive measures related to multitasking ability. High multitaskers performed worse on every dimension: they were more easily distracted, more mentally disorganized, and worse at switching between tasks. The finding is counterintuitive — the people who multitask most are least equipped to do it — and Deresiewicz uses it to anchor his argument that focused, sequential attention is cognitively superior to fragmented, parallel attention.

Thomas Mann, James Joyce, T.S. Eliot

Deresiewicz uses three literary examples to illustrate the relationship between slowness and excellence. Thomas Mann held that "a writer is someone for whom writing is more difficult than it is for other people." James Joyce wrote Ulysses at roughly 100 words per day over seven years. T.S. Eliot produced approximately 150 pages of poetry over 25 years — half a page per month. These figures are not offered as curiosities but as evidence of a principle: the best thinking proceeds slowly, iteratively, and through sustained engagement. Speed is a sign of superficiality, not competence.

The Hazing Scandal at Bahrain Naval Base

Near the end of the lecture, Deresiewicz poses a direct question to the cadets: what would they do if they witnessed something like the hazing scandal at the U.S. naval base in Bahrain, orchestrated by a senior noncommissioned officer? Would they have the courage to act? Would they even know what the right action was? He uses this as a concrete illustration of why self-knowledge and moral preparation must precede — not follow — the situations that demand them. The ethical emergency is the wrong moment to begin the work of developing ethical convictions.

Application to Changeis — Federal IT Consulting

Changeis operates in a federal contracting environment defined by bureaucratic structure, procurement regulations, agency hierarchy, and the compliance demands of aviation modernization programs. These conditions create specific leadership pressures that map directly onto Deresiewicz's analysis. The following recommendations are organized around the essay's core themes.

1. Distinguish Compliance from Leadership

Federal IT consulting rewards procedural competence: SOW adherence, deliverable cadence, CPARS performance scores, and agency satisfaction ratings. These are necessary but insufficient. Deresiewicz's core warning — that bureaucracies reward "keeping the routine going" rather than substantive leadership — applies with precision to the GS-driven FAA environment. Changeis leaders must cultivate the distinction internally: compliance is the floor, not the ceiling. The people at Changeis who create durable value are those who can see what the FAA needs before FAA program managers have articulated it.

Recommendation for Changeis

Establish an internal norm that separates "deliverable completion" from "strategic contribution." Project leads should be expected not only to execute task orders but to formulate, document, and advocate for program improvements that may exceed the scope of any individual contract.

2. Protect Time for Independent Thinking

Small consulting firms are especially vulnerable to the trap Deresiewicz identifies: constant responsiveness to client demands, team messages, and operational logistics crowds out the time required for genuine strategic thinking. If Changeis leaders are always reacting, they are never leading. Petraeus did not develop counterinsurgency doctrine during briefings; he developed it through sustained, patient analysis conducted with some insulation from institutional noise.

Recommendation for Changeis

Block dedicated thinking time — at minimum one to two hours per week, unscheduled and device-free — for senior staff and the President. This is not idle time; it is strategic infrastructure. Use it to examine program trajectories, client relationships, and firm direction without the pressure of immediacy.

3. Develop Moral Courage for Agency Pushback

Federal consulting firms face a structural incentive toward deference: the client pays, the client directs, and contract renewals depend on client satisfaction scores. But agencies that are navigating complex modernization programs (as the FAA is) sometimes pursue approaches that Changeis technical leadership can see are suboptimal. Deresiewicz's argument about moral courage applies here: a consultant who only confirms what the client wants to hear is not leading — they are enabling. The Petraeus model suggests that the credible, evidence-based challenge, delivered respectfully and grounded in technical expertise, is the mark of a trusted advisor rather than a vendor.

Recommendation for Changeis

Cultivate a firm culture that rewards constructive dissent. When technical leads identify risks or alternative approaches that differ from agency preference, create a structured channel — written technical memos, program risk registers, or executive briefings — for surfacing those views without jeopardizing the relationship. Document the recommendation and the rationale regardless of whether it is adopted.

4. Counter Multitasking Culture with Deep Work Practices

The Stanford study Deresiewicz cites has direct implications for how Changeis structures its technical work. Aviation modernization projects — involving software engineering, systems integration, regulatory compliance, and program management simultaneously — create natural pressure toward fragmented attention. The research suggests that this fragmentation degrades the quality of every individual task. Sustained, focused work on one problem produces better outputs than parallel attention split among many.

Recommendation for Changeis

Adopt deep work practices: limit meeting density, batch communications, and protect extended blocks of uninterrupted time for complex technical tasks. For senior engineers and architects working on FAA system design, even two to three hours of uninterrupted focus per day represents a measurable improvement in output quality compared to meeting-saturated schedules.

5. Invest in Reading and Intellectual Development

Deresiewicz argues that books — especially old ones — are valuable precisely because they pre-date the conventional wisdom of the present, and therefore challenge it. For Changeis, this principle extends to sustained engagement with work that exceeds the immediate demands of any contract: aviation system architecture literature, federal technology policy history, organizational theory, and leadership writing. Leaders who only read what their current task requires are perpetually reactive.

Recommendation for Changeis

Consider a lightweight internal reading or discussion practice — even quarterly — where leadership engages with material outside the immediate contract scope. Topics might include aviation modernization history, public sector transformation case studies, or leadership development literature. The goal is to maintain intellectual range that exceeds any single program.

6. Build Deep Mentorship, Not Broadcast Communication

Deresiewicz's expansion of "solitude" to include deep one-on-one friendship is particularly relevant for a small firm. He contrasts 968 Facebook friends who receive one-line messages with one or two genuine interlocutors with whom one can "unfold your soul." For Changeis, this maps onto the difference between broadcast team updates and genuine mentorship conversations. The junior staff members who will become the firm's future leaders need extended, honest dialogue — not just task assignments and performance reviews.

Recommendation for Changeis

Establish structured one-on-one mentoring between senior leadership and high-potential staff, distinct from project supervision. These conversations should explicitly address professional identity questions: what does the individual want to build, what do they believe about their field, what kind of leader do they want to become. This is the kind of "deep friendship" Deresiewicz identifies as a form of productive solitude.

7. Prepare for Hard Decisions in Advance

The lecture's most urgent practical point is that ethical and strategic preparation must precede the situations that require it. For Changeis, this means anticipating the difficult situations that federal contracting creates — a program office that pressures the firm to underreport risks, a scope creep situation that compromises deliverable quality, an internal personnel issue that intersects with client relationships — and having worked through those scenarios before they arrive. Leaders who have not done this work arrive at hard decisions empty-handed.

Recommendation for Changeis

Conduct periodic scenario planning exercises — even informally — in which leadership works through hypothetical hard decisions: how would we respond if a major client asked us to alter findings? What is our position if we identify a safety-relevant technical risk that falls outside our task order scope? Having pre-thought these scenarios is the equivalent of Deresiewicz's "practicing on the firing range before the firefight."
