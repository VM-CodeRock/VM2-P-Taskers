Table Setup

Four players sit at a square table, one per side. Each side corresponds to a Wind direction (East, South, West, North).

East is the dealer. Each game, East starts by rolling dice to determine the break point in the wall, then deals 13 tiles to each player (East gets 14 and discards first). The player to East's right becomes East next game if East loses; East keeps the seat on a win.

The wall is built by stacking tiles face-down in rows (each player builds a section). After the wall is built, tiles are dealt in groups of four using the dice-determined starting position.

Players organize their tiles on a rack (a small wooden ledge) so only they can see their tiles.

Game Flow

The Sequence of a Hand
PhaseWhat HappensDuration
1. Build the WallAll tiles shuffled face-down; each player builds a wall section of 19 stacks (2 tiles each)2-3 min
2. Break the WallEast rolls dice; the count determines where to break the wall and begin dealing30 sec
3. The DealStarting from the break point, 13 tiles to each player (East gets 14). Players rack tiles privately.1 min
4. The CharlestonTile-passing exchange: 3 passes right, across, left (required); then optional second round. (Lesson 4 covers this in depth.)2-5 min
5. Pick & DiscardEast discards first. Players draw from the wall or claim discards, then discard a tile.15-45 min

Pick & Discard in Detail

After the Charleston, play begins with East discarding a tile face-up in the center. Then, going counter-clockwise, each player either:

  1. Draws the top tile from the wall, then discards one tile; OR
  2. Claims the last discarded tile to complete a meld (pung, kong, or their completing tile for Mahjong).
Claiming a Discard: You can only claim the most recent discard. To claim it for a pung, kong, or quint, say "Take!" and expose that meld on your rack face-up. You then discard one tile. You cannot claim a discard to form a chow (run) in American Mahjong — only Mahjong itself can come from a chow-completing discard. To win, call "Mahj!" and reveal your entire hand.

Important: After a player claims a discard, play resumes from that player going counter-clockwise (the intervening players are skipped for that turn).

Joker swap: If you have a Joker in your hand and see one of your tiles used as a Joker substitute in an exposed meld on another player's rack, you can — on your turn — swap your natural tile for that Joker. This frees the Joker for your use.

The Charleston

The tile-passing phase unique to American Mahjong
What Is the Charleston?

The Charleston is a structured tile-passing phase that happens before regular play begins. It is exclusive to American Mahjong and gives players a chance to improve their starting hand by exchanging unwanted tiles. The mnemonic for the pass order is ROLLOR.

ROLLOR: Right, Over (across), Left, Left, Over, Right
First 3 passes = First Charleston (required)
Last 3 passes = Second Charleston (optional — requires unanimous agreement)

Pass 1 (First Charleston — Right): Pick 3 tiles you don't want. Pass them face-down to the player on your RIGHT (East). You will receive 3 tiles from the player on your LEFT (West).
Blind Pass Strategy

On the 3rd pass (First Charleston, Left) and the 6th pass (Second Charleston, Right), you may do a "blind pass." This means you pass some tiles from the incoming pass along without looking at them, mixing them with tiles from your own hand.

Why blind pass? If you have a strong hand forming and don't want to disrupt it, blind passing lets you fulfill your obligation to pass 3 tiles without revealing which tiles you value — and without looking at potentially distracting incoming tiles.

Rule: You cannot peek at the incoming tiles before deciding. Place your own chosen tiles face-down, then slide the unviewed incoming tiles alongside them to reach 3 total.

Never pass Jokers. Jokers are the most valuable tiles in American Mahjong. Passing one is essentially handing an opponent a wild card. Guard them at all costs.
The Courtesy Pass

After both Charlestons are complete, you and the player directly across from you may agree to exchange 1-3 tiles in an optional "courtesy pass." One player offers tiles; the other decides how many to accept. This is voluntary and either player can decline.

Scoring

Understanding point values, concealed hands, and the scoring calculator
How NMJL Scoring Works

In American Mahjong, point values are printed directly on the annual NMJL card. Each hand has a listed value. The winner collects that amount from each of the other three players (so a 25-point hand yields 75 total points from the table).

ScenarioWho Pays
You win on a tile drawn from the wall (self-draw)All 3 opponents each pay the full hand value
You win on a discard from an opponentOnly the player who discarded pays double; the other two pay single. (Varies by house rule — confirm with your group.)
Wall game (no winner)No points change hands
Concealed vs. Open: Some hands on the NMJL card are marked "Closed" — meaning all 14 tiles must come from the wall or the final Mahjong discard. Closed hands are often worth more and prevent you from claiming discards for pungs/kongs mid-game. "Open" hands allow you to claim discards throughout.

Strategy Basics

Hand selection, reading the table, and playing defense
The Three Pillars of Basic Strategy
1. Hand Selection

Pick 2-3 candidate hands from the NMJL card at the start. Narrow to one by the end of the Charleston. Your frontrunner should need the fewest additional tiles.

2. Reading the Table

Watch what opponents discard. If three 5-Bams have been discarded, don't build a hand requiring 5-Bams. The discard pile is your best intelligence source.

3. Defensive Discards

If your hand is unlikely to win, pivot to defense. Discard tiles that are "safe" — tiles already discarded by others or tiles that don't complete common NMJL hands.

Hand Selection Framework

When looking at your 13 starting tiles (before the Charleston), apply this framework:

  1. Survey your suits. Which suit appears most? That's likely your primary suit.
  2. Count pairs. Pairs are precious — they can't use Jokers and can't be claimed from discards (except to win). Protect pairs you have.
  3. Count Flowers. Three or four Flowers strongly suggest a Flowers-category hand.
  4. Identify Jokers. Each Joker dramatically improves any pung, kong, or quint hand. Note them but keep them flexible.
  5. Find category fit. Open the NMJL card. Look for a category (2468, 13579, Consecutive, Winds-Dragons, Quints, Flowers) that matches most of your tiles.
  6. Pick 2-3 hands. Select specific hands from your top 2 categories. Count how many tiles away you are from each.

Intermediate Play

Exposure logic, Joker etiquette, and reading opponents
Exposed vs. Concealed Melds

When you claim a discard, you must expose the resulting meld on your rack — face-up for all to see. This reveals strategic information. Key considerations:

  • Exposure signals your category. If you expose a pung of 4-Bam, opponents know you're in a Bam-heavy or even-number hand. They'll hold 4-Bams and 2-Bams tightly.
  • Exposure locks you in. Once exposed, you cannot change your hand. Plan carefully before claiming a discard.
  • Jokers in exposed melds. If you have a Joker in an exposed pung/kong, opponents can swap their natural tile for it (on their turn). This is legal and common.
  • Strategic concealment. Some hands benefit from staying concealed (no claims) — opponents can't read your hand and you're eligible for "closed" hand bonuses.
Joker Management
Jokers are the most powerful and most contested tiles in American Mahjong. The player who controls Jokers often controls the game.
SituationOptimal Play
You have a Joker earlyHold it. Don't commit it to a meld until you're sure of your hand. Flexibility is worth more than speed early on.
Opponent has a Joker in an exposed meldIf you hold the natural tile, plan the swap for your turn. This is a high-value play — you get a Joker and they lose one.
Multiple Jokers in handYou have a massive advantage. Build a high-point Quint hand if possible, as these can yield 35+ points.
You hold a Joker but are losing hopeA Joker is worth nothing at game end if you don't win. Consider pivoting to any achievable hand quickly, even if lower-value.
Reading Opponents — What to Watch
  1. Discard speed: A player who discards quickly has a clear plan. A player who hesitates is deciding between hands — likely flexible or close to Mahjong.
  2. Discard patterns: If someone discards all Bamboo tiles early, they're in Dots/Craks. Conversely, hoarding Wind tiles suggests a Winds-Dragons hand.
  3. Passing in the Charleston: Players often pass what they don't need. If someone passed 3 Dots to you, they're likely not in a Dots hand.
  4. Exposed melds: The most reliable signal. Two exposed melds means a player is 1-2 picks from Mahjong — increase defensive urgency.
  5. The blind pass signal: If a player blind-passed during the Charleston, they had a strong hand forming early.

Advanced Tactics

Statistical thinking, Joker-heavy strategies, and tournament mindset
Tile Probability Basics

With 152 tiles total, understanding availability dramatically improves decision-making:

Tile TypeTotal CopiesEffective Availability
Each numbered suit tile (e.g., 5-Bam)44 minus visible discards minus exposures
Each Wind tile4Same formula
Each Dragon4Same formula
Flower4 totalHighly contested; watch who has them
Joker8 totalHighest per-tile count; still rare
Key Principle: If you need a tile and 2+ copies have been discarded or are visible in others' melds, your probability drops steeply. At 3 gone, the odds are very poor. At 4 gone — abandon that tile requirement entirely.
Joker-Heavy Hand Strategy

When you hold 2+ Jokers, your strategic options expand dramatically. Prioritize:

  1. Quint hands (35+ pts): A Quint requires 4 natural + 1 Joker. With 2 Jokers, you can build two Quints or one Quint + one Kong. These are the highest-value hands on most NMJL cards.
  2. Concealed bonus hands: With Jokers, you may be able to build a concealed (closed) hand quickly since you're not relying on discards for pungs.
  3. Joker conservation: Do not expose Jokers unless necessary — opponents will plan Joker swaps against you.
  4. Speed: With 2+ Jokers, you should typically win within 5-8 turns of play. Prioritize completing the hand over optimizing the score.
The NMJL Card as a Strategic Tool

Advanced players memorize the current year's NMJL card. Key patterns to internalize:

  • High-frequency tiles: The card often requires specific "hot" tiles (e.g., the current year's digits). These are contested by many players — hold them or pass them strategically.
  • Closed hand count: Count how many closed hands are on the card this year. More closed hands = more players going concealed = fewer claims mid-game.
  • Category distribution: If 40% of hands use even numbers, many players will be collecting even tiles. Adjust your Charleston passes accordingly.
  • Joker-free hands: Some hands explicitly require no Jokers. These are rare but valuable if you're in a joker drought.

Video Implementation Plan

Platform comparison and executive recommendation for producing a video version of this curriculum
Overview

This section provides actionable recommendations for producing an 8-lesson video series based on this curriculum. The intended audience is Varun or a delegated production team. Three AI video platforms are evaluated, plus a fourth hybrid approach. A recommendation table concludes the section.

Context: The course requires clear tile visualization (animated/highlighted tiles), step-by-step hand walkthroughs, a presenter voice/avatar, and quiz prompts. Not all platforms handle tile animation equally — this shapes the recommendation.
Synthesia
Starter $22/mo · Creator $67/mo (annual)

Synthesia is an AI avatar video platform used by corporate training teams. It generates polished talking-head videos with AI presenters, supports slides as backgrounds, and allows text-to-video production from a script.

Strengths

  • 140+ AI avatars, many professional styles
  • Polished corporate presentation quality
  • Screen/slide backgrounds work for tile diagrams
  • Multilingual narration (useful if multilingual audiences)
  • Built-in script editor; fast turnaround
  • SCORM/LMS export on Business tier

Limitations

  • Avatar cannot manipulate tiles dynamically
  • No native animation engine — tile movements need external prep
  • Higher entry cost vs. HeyGen at the Creator tier
  • Less flexibility than Remotion/Manim for animated visuals
  • Videos feel "corporate" — may not suit casual learning aesthetic

Best for: Lessons 2 (Game Flow), 4 (Charleston), 6 (Strategy) — text-heavy, explainer content where an avatar narrator adds credibility. Pair with pre-made slide decks showing tile diagrams.

Pricing source: Colossyan comparison, verified March 2026 — Synthesia Starter $22/mo (annual), Creator $67/mo (annual).

HeyGen
Free (3 videos) · Creator $24/mo · Business $149/mo (annual)

HeyGen is a direct competitor to Synthesia, with a free tier that allows immediate testing. It differentiates on avatar customization, instant voice cloning, and a more accessible entry point.

Strengths

  • Free tier (3 videos/mo) — test before paying
  • Instant voice clone from a short sample
  • More affordable Creator plan than Synthesia
  • Photo-realistic avatar quality
  • API access for automation on higher tiers
  • Strong for custom brand avatar (Varun as avatar)

Limitations

  • Same core limitation: no dynamic tile animation
  • Business plan jumps sharply to $149/mo
  • Collaboration features weaker than Synthesia at scale
  • Watermark on free tier exports

Best for: Personal/custom avatar. HeyGen lets you clone your own likeness as the presenter — ideal if Varun wants to appear in the videos himself. Best for Lessons 1, 3, 5, 8 where a personal touch matters.

Differentiator vs. Synthesia: Free tier + voice cloning + lower Creator price. Synthesia wins on enterprise features and avatar library.

Pricing source: Colossyan comparison (March 2026), Argil.ai HeyGen pricing analysis (December 2025).

ElevenLabs
Free · Starter $5/mo · Creator $22/mo · Pro $99/mo

ElevenLabs is a voice-only AI platform — it produces the highest-quality AI narration available but does not generate video or avatar visuals. It would be paired with screen recordings, animations, or an editing tool.

Strengths

  • Best-in-class voice quality and naturalness
  • Cheapest path ($22/mo Creator) for professional narration
  • Voice cloning from short samples
  • 192 kbps audio on Creator+
  • Works with any video editor (Premiere, CapCut, DaVinci)
  • Best choice for screen-recorded tile walkthroughs

Limitations

  • No video output — requires separate video production workflow
  • No avatar — purely audio narration
  • Requires screen recording software + editing workflow
  • More manual production effort per episode

Best for: Lesson 3 (Hand Building), Lesson 7 (Intermediate), Lesson 9 (Sample Hands) — anywhere tile animation and screen recording is the primary visual. ElevenLabs narrates over the visuals you create.

Pricing source: PhotonPay ElevenLabs pricing guide (May 2026), Flexprice breakdown (January 2026).

Hybrid Approach (Recommended)
ElevenLabs $22/mo + Remotion/Manim (free/open source)

The most powerful approach for a tile-animation-heavy course: use ElevenLabs for voice narration + Remotion (React-based video) or Manim (Python animation library) for programmatic tile animation. This enables fully custom tile highlights, drag animations, and step-by-step hand walkthroughs that no avatar platform can match.

Strengths

  • Full tile animation control (highlight pungs, show discards, animate draws)
  • Lowest recurring cost ($22/mo ElevenLabs)
  • Infinite customization — exact brand colors, tile styles
  • Reusable code components across all 8 lessons
  • Can produce the exact same interactive content as this HTML tutor

Limitations

  • Requires developer/animator involvement (1-2 days per episode)
  • No talking-head avatar — voice-only presentation
  • Higher upfront time investment to build animation templates
  • Less polished than avatar videos for intro/outro segments

Workflow: (1) Write script for each lesson. (2) Record ElevenLabs narration. (3) Build Remotion/Manim tile animation components. (4) Composite in DaVinci Resolve or CapCut. (5) Publish to private YouTube or LMS. Total time per 5-min episode: ~4-6 hours with reusable templates.
