1. Executive Summary

Changeis is a mid-size federal consulting firm operating across DHS components (TSA, FEMA, USCG), FAA, and Army with five distinct service lines: federal program and proposal management, AI/automation, supply chain modernization, cyber (CMMC/NIST 800-171), and digital factory. The firm sits at an inflection point — active high-value pursuits (APEX, NSEMS, ATEPS-II) are maturing into award decisions, while the broader GovCon market is experiencing post-DOGE budget rationalization pressure.

This playbook delivers three outputs: (1) a Grow/Hold/Harvest strategic landscape mapping Changeis service lines and opportunities against market dynamics; (2) five corporate-level OKRs with SMART key results grounded in best-in-class GovCon practice; and (3) five Principal/CEO-level OKRs that cascade directly from corporate objectives. Together they form a ready-to-implement 2026 OKR system with a quarterly review cadence and RACI accountability structure.

Key Insight: Changeis's highest-leverage plays for 2026 are (a) converting APEX and NSEMS pipeline wins into a cyber/AI cross-sell wedge, and (b) using CMMC/NIST 800-171 advisory as a rapid-growth beachhead into DoD supply chain accounts — an underserved segment with near-zero incumbent competition at the $5M–$25M contract threshold.
2. Strategic Options Landscape — Grow / Hold / Harvest

Each Changeis service line and active pursuit is mapped against two axes: market tailwind strength (driven by federal budget priority, regulatory mandate, and competitive density) and Changeis positioning strength (existing past performance, incumbent relationships, and capture maturity). This yields a clear Grow/Hold/Harvest classification.

■ Grow

Invest & Accelerate

  • Cyber (CMMC/NIST 800-171) — DoD mandate driving 300,000+ contractor assessments; Changeis can command $1.5K–$3K/day advisory rates
  • AI/Automation — All six target agencies have active AI modernization budgets; strong APEX framework IP differentiator
  • APEX pursuit — High-confidence pursuit, strong agency relationship, AI delivery thesis aligned to solicitation
  • NSEMS pursuit — FEMA NSEMS is an undercompeted SB set-aside with Changeis having incumbent-adjacent knowledge
Rationale: High market pull + strong Changeis positioning. Resource investments here have 3–5× ROI horizon.
◆ Hold

Maintain & Selectively Invest

  • Federal Program/Proposal Mgmt — Stable demand; cash-generative but commoditizing; invest only in AI-augmented proposal tools to stay competitive
  • ATEPS-II IDIQ — $0 obligated; hold position, do not over-invest in task order pursuit without clear agency champion
  • Digital Factory — Emerging but capital-intensive; position for 2027 scale; run at breakeven in 2026
  • Army relationships — Maintain presence; watch for supply chain modernization RFIs as trigger for upgrade to Grow
Rationale: Adequate returns with moderate investment. Protects optionality without draining BD budget.
▲ Harvest

Extract Value / No-Bid

  • MAPS (no-bid confirmed) — Correct call; MAPS scope/margin did not justify pursuit cost. Harvest by redirecting proposal resources to APEX
  • Generic IT staffing — Low differentiation, margin pressure; allow natural attrition, do not recompete
  • Non-cyber compliance advisory — Outside CMMC lane; sunset or fold into cyber practice
Rationale: Declining returns; opportunity cost too high relative to Grow portfolio. Harvest cash flows only.

3. Best-in-Class OKR Patterns — GovCon & Consulting Examples

Five reference patterns drawn from GovCon firms, federal consulting analogues, and the authoritative OKR literature, each with a direct takeaway for Changeis:

Example 1 · Day & Zimmermann Government Services

Mission-Anchored OKR Cascade

D&Z GS President Doug Magee deployed OKRs anchored to the mission statement: "Build an organization that performs with purpose, adapts with agility, and delivers with integrity." Key results focused on talent retention rates, delivery outcome scores, and disciplined scaling metrics — not just revenue targets. Quarterly reviews tied OKR progress directly to headcount and BD investment decisions. Source: ExecutiveBiz

→ Changeis Takeaway: Mission statement + 3 KRs per objective is the right cadence. Don't exceed 5 OKRs at corporate level.
Example 2 · Booz Allen Hamilton (VoLT Strategy)

Technology-Growth OKR Integration

Booz Allen's VoLT (Velocity, Leadership, Technology) corporate strategy cascades into OKRs at every level. Technology investment OKRs are measured against new contract vehicle wins (e.g., new IDIQ vehicles per year) and AI/ML task order revenue as % of total. Performance reviews are quarterly with public heatmaps showing OKR maturity scores by business unit. Source: Booz Allen 2025 ERS Report

→ Changeis Takeaway: Use technology revenue % as a KR to force the AI/cyber service line transition — not just headcount.
Example 3 · BCG OKR Excellence Study (2024)

Outcome-Oriented Key Results

BCG's 2024 study found that the most common OKR failure mode in consulting firms is output-focused KRs ("launch X service") vs. outcome-focused KRs ("40% cost reduction vs. previous implementations"). BCG recommends a 3-KR limit per objective, an "outcome orientation" test for each KR, and big-room planning sessions every quarter to review and re-commit. Source: BCG, Apr 2024

→ Changeis Takeaway: Every KR below must pass the outcome test: it measures a change in behavior or result, not an activity completed.
Example 4 · GovCon BD Pipeline Best Practice

Pipeline Coverage Ratio as KR

Industry standard for established GovCon firms (7+ years): maintain a weighted pipeline of 3× annual revenue target, with coverage across ≥3 agencies. Pipeline velocity (days in capture stage) and Pwin accuracy (predicted vs. actual win rate) are the two most predictive KRs for growth outcomes. Monthly pipeline reviews with a 30/60/90-day cash flow projection complete the measurement system. Source: GovCon Giants

→ Changeis Takeaway: Build pipeline coverage ratio (3× revenue) and proposal win rate (target ≥35%) into corporate KRs explicitly.
Example 5 · CMMC Advisory Sector (2026)

Compliance-Driven Growth OKRs

DoD's 2026 CMMC rollout creates a finite, measurable runway: 300,000+ contractors need Level 2 assessments within 3 years. Leading advisory firms (e.g., RADICL, Coalfire) are setting OKRs around C3PAO partnership depth, SPRS score improvements for clients, and advisory client retention. Primes now require subcontractors to have SPRS scores ≥88 — creating a captive market for rapid compliance advisory. Source: RADICL 2026; SecurityMetrics

→ Changeis Takeaway: Set a KR on number of clients achieving SPRS ≥88 in 2026 — this is a measurable, outcome-based growth metric unique to the cyber practice.
4. Draft Corporate OKRs — FY2026 (Q3–Q4 Cycle)

Five corporate-level objectives with three SMART key results each. All KRs are outcome-based, time-bound, and measurable by observable data. Tracking cadence: monthly status, quarterly formal review.

Corporate OKR C-1 · Revenue Growth

Build a $15M+ Qualified Pipeline and Grow Awarded Revenue by 25% by December 31, 2026

  • KR1 Achieve a weighted BD pipeline value of ≥$15M across ≥3 federal agencies by September 30, 2026 (baseline: current pipeline snapshot as of June 1, 2026)
  • KR2 Improve overall proposal win rate from current baseline to ≥35% (measured by contract value awarded ÷ contract value bid) by December 31, 2026
  • KR3 Convert APEX and/or NSEMS pursuit into a signed contract award (base year + options) by December 31, 2026
Corporate OKR C-2 · Cyber Practice Launch

Establish Changeis as a Recognized CMMC/NIST 800-171 Advisory Provider with ≥$1M in Billable Revenue by Year-End 2026

  • KR1 Sign ≥1 C3PAO or RP (Registered Practitioner) partner agreement to enable CMMC Level 2 assessment services by July 31, 2026
  • KR2 Deliver SPRS score improvement engagements to ≥6 clients, with ≥4 achieving a final SPRS score of 88 or higher, by December 31, 2026
  • KR3 Generate ≥$1M in cyber advisory billings (CMMC gap analysis, NIST 800-171 implementation, POA&M management) by December 31, 2026
Corporate OKR C-3 · AI/Automation Differentiation

Make AI-Augmented Delivery Changeis's Measurable Competitive Differentiator on ≥50% of Active Engagements

  • KR1 Deploy at least 2 Changeis-owned AI automation tools (e.g., proposal AI, program reporting AI) into active client engagements by September 30, 2026
  • KR2 Achieve ≥25% reduction in proposal development cycle time (days from RFP receipt to compliant draft submission) compared to Q1 2026 baseline, measured across ≥5 proposals by December 31, 2026
  • KR3 Include AI/automation capability explicitly in 100% of proposals submitted after August 1, 2026, with ≥3 wins citing AI differentiation as evaluation factor
Corporate OKR C-4 · Talent & Delivery Excellence

Build a High-Retention, Credentialed Delivery Team That Earns a ≥4.2/5.0 CPARS Average Across All Active Contracts

  • KR1 Achieve ≥90% employee retention (voluntary turnover <10%) among billable delivery staff by December 31, 2026
  • KR2 Ensure ≥80% of billable staff hold at least one relevant certification (PMP, CMMC-AB RP, CompTIA Security+, AWS/Azure, or equivalent) by December 31, 2026
  • KR3 Receive ≥4.2/5.0 average across all submitted CPARS evaluations in FY2026 (collect and track all ratings; escalate any ≤3.5 immediately)
Corporate OKR C-5 · Operational & Financial Health

Achieve 15%+ Net Operating Margin and ≥3-Month Cash Reserve While Reducing Proposal B&P Cost Per Win

  • KR1 Maintain ≥15% net operating margin on a trailing 3-month basis through Q3 and Q4 2026 (monitor monthly via P&L dashboard)
  • KR2 Reduce bid-and-proposal (B&P) cost per awarded dollar by 20% vs. 2025 baseline by implementing AI-assisted proposal workflows and tighter no-bid discipline
  • KR3 Maintain ≥3 months of operating expenses in accessible cash/credit by December 31, 2026, with monthly cash flow projections reviewed at leadership team meeting
5. Principal / CEO-Level OKRs — Cascading from Corporate

Varun, as CEO and Principal, owns the outcomes that corporate OKRs depend on at the leadership layer. These OKRs cascade from the corporate objectives above — each Principal KR directly drives one or more corporate KRs.

Cascade Logic: Corporate OKRs set the organizational finish line. Principal OKRs answer "what must Varun personally do/own to make the corporate OKRs possible?" They are not a repetition of corporate KRs — they are the specific leadership behaviors and decisions only the CEO can make.
↳ Cascades from C-1 (Revenue Growth)
Principal OKR P-1 · Pipeline Ownership

Personally Own the Top 5 Pursuit Relationships and Drive APEX/NSEMS to Award-Ready Status

  • KR1 Conduct ≥2 in-person or video touchpoints per quarter with the senior government champion on each of APEX and NSEMS (minimum 4 touchpoints each by December 31, 2026)
  • KR2 Personally review and approve go/no-bid decision and win strategy for every pursuit ≥$2M — documented in capture plan — within 5 business days of RFP release
  • KR3 Lead ≥3 formal capture briefings with partner/teaming discussions for APEX and NSEMS by September 30, 2026, resulting in ≥2 signed teaming agreements
↳ Cascades from C-2 (Cyber Practice) + C-3 (AI Differentiation)
Principal OKR P-2 · Practice Line Leadership

Launch the Cyber and AI Practices with Named Leads, Defined Offerings, and First Revenue by Q3 2026

  • KR1 Hire or internally promote dedicated Practice Leads for (a) Cyber/CMMC and (b) AI/Automation by July 31, 2026; each lead has a defined 90-day ramp plan signed off by CEO
  • KR2 Publish a Changeis Cyber Practice one-pager and an AI Capabilities Sheet (capability statements, past performance, pricing model) for use in proposals by July 31, 2026
  • KR3 Close ≥1 new cyber advisory engagement and ≥1 new AI/automation engagement (each ≥$75K) by September 30, 2026, each sourced from Varun's direct network outreach
↳ Cascades from C-4 (Talent & Delivery Excellence)
Principal OKR P-3 · Culture & Team

Be the Culture Anchor — Ensure Every Team Member Knows the Strategy and Feels Invested in the Outcome

  • KR1 Host ≥1 all-hands strategy session per quarter (4 total in 2026) where corporate OKR status is shared transparently with all staff; measure attendance ≥85%
  • KR2 Complete 1:1 career development conversation with every direct report (≥quarterly), documented with a 90-day goal aligned to at least one corporate KR, by the end of each quarter
  • KR3 Achieve ≥4.0/5.0 on anonymous mid-year pulse survey question: "I understand how my work contributes to Changeis's 2026 strategy"
↳ Cascades from C-5 (Financial Health) + C-1 (Revenue)
Principal OKR P-4 · Strategic Partnerships & Teaming

Build a Partner Ecosystem of ≥3 Prime/Sub Relationships That Expand Changeis's Addressable Market by 40%

  • KR1 Sign ≥3 active teaming or subcontracting agreements with complementary firms (primes with Changeis as sub, or subs/JV for set-aside vehicles) by September 30, 2026
  • KR2 Attend or speak at ≥2 agency-specific industry days or small business forums per quarter (8 total in 2026) to build pipeline visibility beyond current relationships
  • KR3 Generate ≥$500K in awarded subcontract revenue through partner-sourced task orders on existing IDIQ vehicles (ATEPS-II or GSA schedules) by December 31, 2026
↳ Cascades from C-3 (AI) + C-5 (Ops) — CEO as Thought Leader
Principal OKR P-5 · Brand & Thought Leadership

Establish Varun as a Recognized GovCon AI/Cyber Voice — Generating ≥3 Inbound Qualified Leads per Quarter from Thought Leadership

  • KR1 Publish ≥1 substantive thought leadership article or white paper per month (LinkedIn, GovCon publication, or agency newsletter) on AI, CMMC, or digital transformation in government; 12 total by December 31, 2026
  • KR2 Secure ≥2 speaking engagements (panel, keynote, or workshop) at GovCon or DHS/FAA-adjacent events in 2026 (e.g., AFCEA, ACT-IAC, DHS Industry Day)
  • KR3 Attribute ≥3 qualified inbound leads per quarter (12 total) directly to thought leadership activity (track via "how did you hear about Changeis?" on first call)
6. Quarterly Review Cadence — Process Diagram

The Changeis OKR cycle runs on a quarterly heartbeat with four distinct phases. Each quarter is 13 weeks; the review rhythm below maps to that structure.

Week 1–2 of Quarter
Set & Align

CEO sets/revises corporate OKRs. Principals cascade their OKRs. All OKRs reviewed in all-hands session. Baseline data captured.

Week 4 (Monthly)
Monthly Check-In

30-min leadership standup. Traffic-light status on each KR (Red/Yellow/Green). Flag blockers. No OKR changes — only status updates.

Week 8 (Mid-Q)
Mid-Quarter Review

60-min deep dive. Score each KR 0.0–1.0. Re-forecast end-of-quarter outcomes. Reallocate BD resources if needed. Update pipeline.

Week 13 (End of Q)
Quarterly Close & Reset

Score all OKRs (0.0–1.0). Celebrate wins ≥0.7. Diagnose misses. Archive quarter. Set next quarter OKRs within 5 business days.

Scoring Convention (John Doerr / Google standard):

ScoreMeaningAction
0.7–1.0Successful — goal achieved or exceededCelebrate; raise bar for next quarter
0.4–0.6Partial progress — made meaningful movementDiagnose; keep if strategic; adjust KR if misspecified
0.0–0.3Failed or abandonedRoot cause analysis required before next quarter OKR set
Key Rule: A consistent score of 1.0 means the OKR was not ambitious enough. Target average scores of 0.7 at end of year — this indicates the right stretch level for a growth-stage GovCon firm.
