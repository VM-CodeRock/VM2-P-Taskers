Section 03 — Topic ARSS0009
ARSS0009

Future of Oversight

Technical POCs

Huasheng Li, Ph.D.
Huasheng.Li@faa.gov
Michael S. Bartron
Michael.S.Bartron@faa.gov
Research Domain
Aviation Safety Regulation & Oversight
AVS · Flight Standards (AFS) · Aircraft Certification (AIR)

ARSS0009 responds to a fundamental shift in aviation safety philosophy: moving from compliance-check-based inspection practices — developed over decades — toward a performance-based, proactive, data-driven oversight paradigm.

The FAA's 2024 SMS Regulatory Expansion extended mandatory Safety Management Systems (SMS) to Part 135 operators, Part 21 manufacturers, and §91.147 air tour operators — the most significant expansion since the original 2015 Part 5 rule. This creates an urgent need for FAA oversight systems that can assess whether organizations' SMS programs are actually working, not merely whether they check compliance boxes.

Compounding pressures include: ICAO Annex 19 Amendment 2 (applicable November 2026) enhancing SSP/SMS provisions; the Boeing 737 MAX and January 2025 DCA mid-air collision exposing systemic weaknesses in compliance-based oversight; FAA's first AI Safety Assurance Roadmap (August 2024); and the growing recognition that "Safety II" approaches must complement traditional "find and fix" (Safety I) models.

5 Research Areas (ARSS0009)
  • 1
    Traditional vs. SMS-Based Oversight: Real-world comparative studies examining whether operators with mature SMS programs demonstrate better safety outcomes than those under traditional compliance-based oversight. Quasi-experimental designs using phased regulatory rollout as natural experiment.
  • 2
    Safety Workforce of the Future: Characteristics, competencies, and training pathways required for a performance-based oversight workforce. Bridges from compliance-checking to analytical partnership — incorporating systems thinking, data literacy, and AI/ML literacy.
  • 3
    Measures of Effectiveness: Novel metrics for FAA's certification and surveillance roles. Develops leading indicator frameworks that can predict safety degradation before incidents occur, drawing on NRC Reactor Oversight Process PI methodology and ICAO Doc 9859 guidance.
  • 4
    Cross-Industry Comparison: Systematic lessons from financial (OCC risk-based supervision), healthcare (Just Culture / PSOs), maritime (ISM Code / Port State Control), nuclear (NRC ROP), and rail (FRA Risk Reduction Program) sectors.
  • 5
    Safety Risk Management Cooperation: Data and information sharing challenges under 14 CFR Part 5 §5.57 — new mandatory inter-entity hazard sharing requirement. Balancing confidentiality protections (49 U.S.C. §44735) with safety information dissemination needs, especially for newly covered small operators.
Section 04 — ARSS0009 Key Research Findings
Current FAA Oversight — DOT OIG Findings
Root Cause Analysis Failure
92%

In 171 of 185 sampled compliance cases, FAA inspectors accepted root cause analyses that did not identify the true root cause — often accepting "human error" rather than systemic causes. Source: DOT OIG American Airlines Audit (2021)

Inspector Staffing Model
46%

FY2019 actual hires were 46% below planned (326 vs. 605). DOT OIG found the staffing model "faulty, unreliable, and contained incomplete, inaccurate, and outdated data." Source: DOT OIG Inspector Staffing (2021)

ARIA Tool Limitations at DCA
874

MITRE's ARIA tool flagged 874 incidents at Reagan National Airport over 3 years before the January 2025 DCA mid-air collision — yet none were escalated as near-mid-air collision risks. Highlights limitations in current risk escalation processes. Source: Reason Foundation (August 2025)

SMS Evaluation Inconsistency
27x

Team inspections of SMS yielded 27 findings where individual inspector inspection found only 1 — a 27x ratio demonstrating critically inconsistent application of SMS evaluation methodology. Source: DOT OIG American Airlines Audit

EASA Performance-Based Regulation — Gold Standard
EASA Annual Safety Review 2024: In 2023, EASA achieved zero fatal accidents involving EASA Air Operator Certificate holders operating complex aircraft — an unprecedented result at 95% of pre-pandemic traffic levels. Key enabling factors include comprehensive risk management through mature SMS implementation and serious incident reduction from 63.5 to 33 cases annually. EASA defines Performance-Based Regulations as those where "implementing rules focus on desired, measurable outcomes, rather than on defining prescriptive means and conditions for achieving compliance." Source: EASA Annual Safety Review 2024

Transport Canada SMS Evaluation — 13 Key Findings (2021)

Transport Canada (TCCA) commissioned the most comprehensive empirical evaluation of SMS oversight implementation globally (2020–2021), producing 13 findings across what worked and what failed after a 6-year post-implementation period. The evaluation provides direct evidence for FAA as it navigates its own Part 135/21 SMS expansion.

What Worked: Non-punitive reporting (clearest success — increased report volumes, reduced fear); systematic hazard identification catching issues undetectable by other means; executive commitment in large organizations; improved industry-TC relations.

Critical Failures for FAA to Avoid: (1) No consistent understanding of SMS inspection procedures across regional inspector corps; (2) Guidance materials so unclear industry turned to consultants; (3) No data strategy to quantitatively measure SMS impact; (4) SMS was overwhelming for operators with 1–10 employees; (5) Industry perceived TC as "retreating" from oversight as SMS matured — a credibility risk.

Six Key Recommendations for FAA: Improve root cause analysis capacity; determine appropriateness of industry risk assignment practices; develop data strategy for quantitative SMS impact analysis; build continuous improvement capacity beyond minimums; ensure consistent inspector training; explore aggregated data-sharing for small operators.

Source: Transport Canada SMS Evaluation Report (February 2021)

NRC Reactor Oversight Process (ROP) — 25-Year Gold Standard
NRC Performance Indicators

The NRC's ROP (implemented 2000) is the most mature performance-based oversight system in U.S. regulated industries. PIs measured against color-coded thresholds (Green/White/Yellow/Red) with increasing regulatory scrutiny. Seven "cornerstones": Initiating Events, Mitigating Systems, Barrier Integrity, Occupational/Public Radiation Safety, Physical Security, Emergency Preparedness.

2026 NRC Reform — Aviation Parallel

ADVANCE Act (2024) directed NRC to revise the 25-year-old ROP. 2026 proposals: eliminate certain baseline inspection procedures; reduce inspection sampling by 38% (2,018 → 1,245 annual hours) while maintaining safety; expand PI use to replace targeted direct inspections. Directly parallels what FAA ARSS0009 seeks to accomplish. Source: ANS (February 2026)

Cross-Industry Lessons Summary

Five sectors (Finance, Healthcare, Maritime, Nuclear, Rail) share five common lessons: (1) Legal protection of voluntary safety info is essential; (2) Inspector/examiner training is the most common implementation failure; (3) Small organizations need scaled frameworks; (4) Data sharing is most valuable and most difficult; (5) Regulatory "retreat" is a real risk when performance-based trust is granted without adequate verification.

FAA AI Roadmap 2024 — 5 Action Areas

Published August 2024 by Associate Administrator David Boulter: (1) Collaboration with ICAO, NASA, DOD, NIST; (2) FAA Workforce Readiness — AI/ML Community of Practice launched; (3) Assure Safety of AI — adapt ARP4754A, DO-178C; (4) Use AI for Safety — ASIAS analytics, LLM compliance review; (5) Aviation Safety Research — aberrant behavior detection, runtime assurance. Source: FAA AI Roadmap v1

Section 05 — ARSS0009 Research Gaps (7 Identified)
  • GAP 1
    Empirical SMS Effectiveness Evidence
    No validated, statistically rigorous evidence comparing safety outcomes for operators under performance-based vs. compliance-based oversight in equivalent risk environments. Transport Canada's evaluation could not statistically attribute accident rate declines to SMS.
    Opportunity: Quasi-experimental designs using the phased Part 135 SMS rollout (2024–2027) as a natural experiment; matched-pair analysis of SMS-voluntary vs. compliance-only operators.
  • GAP 2
    Inspector Competency Assessment Tools (U.S. Context)
    SM ICG has developed international competency frameworks, but FAA has no validated instrument for assessing inspector readiness for SMS-based oversight. FAA's 7,017 AVS employees (average age 54) face a significant competency transformation gap.
    Opportunity: Develop and validate a U.S.-specific SMS inspector competency assessment tool, grounded in the SM ICG framework but adapted to FAA's SAS workflow.
  • GAP 3
    Novel Safety Performance Indicators for FAA Certification Role
    FAA's SAS Risk Profile uses compliance-based metrics; no validated leading indicator framework exists for FAA's certification role. ODA oversight gaps remain unaddressed.
    Opportunity: Develop a leading indicator framework for certification oversight, drawing on NRC's PI development methodology and ICAO Doc 9859. Identify indicators applicable to ODA (Organization Designation Authorization) oversight.
  • GAP 4
    Small Operator Data Sharing Architecture
    ASIAS serves Part 121 carriers well, but there is no equivalent aggregated data infrastructure for the newly covered Part 135 and Part 21 entities — many of which are small, lack safety analytics expertise, and generate insufficient individual data for trend analysis.
    Opportunity: Design a data sharing framework and governance model for small operators — potentially leveraging anonymization, cloud analytics, and trusted third-party intermediaries (analogous to Patient Safety Organizations in healthcare).
  • GAP 5
    AI/ML Tool Validation for Regulatory Use
    AI tools for safety risk identification (ARIA, NLP classifiers) are in use but lack validated performance metrics for regulatory decision-making contexts. The DCA collision exposed ARIA's limitations in systemic risk synthesis.
    Opportunity: Develop explainability and validation standards for AI tools used in FAA oversight decisions, addressing the "black box" problem — aligned with FAA AI Roadmap Action Area 3 (Assure Safety of AI).
  • GAP 6
    Cross-Industry Transfer Analysis
    Cross-sector analogies are frequently cited (NRC ROP, OCC examination, healthcare PSOs) but not systematically analyzed for transferability. Specific mechanisms — not just broad principles — need to be identified for FAA adaptation.
    Opportunity: Structured comparative analysis of NRC ROP, OCC risk-based supervision, and healthcare PSO models with explicit mechanism-level mapping to FAA regulatory context.
  • GAP 7
    Change Management Framework for FAA Workforce
    No published roadmap exists for transforming FAA's 7,000+ AVS workforce from compliance-checkers to performance-based oversight professionals — despite 32% of managers being retirement-eligible now and another 29% within 5 years.
    Opportunity: Evidence-based change management plan drawing on transformation case studies from NRC (ROP implementation), Transport Canada (Surveillance 2.0), and EASA (PBR implementation), with FAA-specific barriers and accelerators.
Section 06 — ARSS0009 Recommended White Paper Approach

Strategic White Paper Framing: ARSS0009

1
Lead with the SMS Expansion Urgency — Frame the white paper around the immediate, time-sensitive challenge: 2024 rule requires Part 135/21 SMS compliance by 2027, yet FAA has no validated tools to assess whether these new SMS programs are actually working. Position the research as operationally urgent, not academic.
2
Propose a Mixed-Methods Research Design — Combine quantitative quasi-experimental analysis (using the phased Part 135 rollout as a natural experiment) with qualitative field research (inspector interviews, operator case studies). Directly addresses Research Area 1 and provides evidence for Research Areas 2–5.
3
Leverage Transport Canada and NRC as Evidence Base — TC's 13-finding evaluation and NRC's 25-year ROP provide the richest empirical analogues. Position the research as extending these frameworks with U.S.-specific context, filling Gap 6 (cross-industry transfer) while directly informing all five research areas.
4
Integrate AI/ML as an Enabling Tool, Not a Topic — Position AI/ML capability (aligned with FAA's August 2024 AI Roadmap) as the mechanism enabling performance-based oversight — not as the research topic itself. Propose ASIAS integration, LLM-based compliance analysis, and predictive risk tools as outputs of the workforce transformation.
5
Demonstrate Deliverable Specificity — The white paper should propose concrete deliverables: (1) Inspector Competency Assessment Tool (filling Gap 2); (2) Leading Indicator Framework for certification oversight (Gap 3); (3) Small Operator Data Sharing Architecture Design (Gap 4). Each deliverable is practical, measurable, and directly implements FAA research priorities.
Section 07 — Topic ARAP0012
ARAP0012

Engineering Analysis of Field Monitoring and Instrumentation Data

Technical POC

Daniel Offenbacker
Daniel.I.Offenbacker@faa.gov
Funding
~$200,000–$250,000/year
Up to 3 years performance period
Research Domain
Airport Technology Research (ATR)
Pavement Engineering · Data Science

Since 1992 — over 30 years — the FAA has instrumented pavement sections at operational commercial airports across the United States to monitor structural responses and in-service performance. This program has directly supported the development and revision of three critical Advisory Circulars: AC 150/5320-6G (Pavement Design), AC 150/5370-10H (Construction Standards), and AC 150/5335-5D (ACR-PCR Strength Reporting).

Monitored data includes: static instrumentation responses (temperature, moisture, crack status — logged hourly); dynamic instrumentation responses (strains, deflections triggered by aircraft traffic); laboratory and field material characterization (resilient modulus, HWD/FWD tests); and Pavement Condition Index (PCI) assessments per ASTM D5340.

ARAP0012 calls for advancing this 30+ year dataset through modern AI/ML, innovative remote sensing technologies, digital twins, and edge computing — transforming a historically compliance-oriented program into a predictive pavement intelligence platform. Over $2.9 billion of the $4.3B in FY2020 AIP grants funded pavement projects (~68%).

6 Research Scope Areas (ARAP0012)
  • 1
    In-Situ Data for Pavement Design, Construction & Management: Improve airport pavement design and management using field instrumentation data from operational airports — NAPTF accelerated testing and PHL/other field sites. Refine FAARFIELD models and FAARFIELD 2.1 fatigue predictions using field observations.
  • 2
    Innovative Remote Monitoring Technologies: Research remote sensing approaches for pavement responses — including InSAR satellite settlement monitoring, ground-penetrating radar (GPR), LiDAR/photogrammetry, fiber optic distributed sensing (FBG/DFOS), and IoT wireless sensor networks.
  • 3
    AI/ML for Pavement Construction and Engineering: Apply machine learning, deep learning, and physics-guided AI to pavement distress classification (CNN/YOLO), PCI automation, remaining life prediction (LSTM), backcalculation of layer moduli (ANN), and maintenance scheduling optimization.
  • 4
    Pavement Instrumentation, Signal Processing & Data Analysis: Investigate sensor installation methods, wavelet-based signal processing for dynamic response data, DBSCAN-based data mining, multi-sensor data fusion, and automated quality assurance/validation pipelines.
  • 5
    Pavement Material Characterization: Characterize pavement materials via field samples or in-situ test data — dynamic backcalculation (DynaBAKFAA), moduli profiling, seasonal variation analysis, accelerated weathering characterization.
  • 6
    Remote Data Collection and Network Communications: Research LoRaWAN/LPWAN, 5G/private LTE, solar-powered sensor nodes, cybersecurity for airport OT/IoT systems, and edge computing for real-time on-site data processing without cloud dependency.
Section 08 — ARAP0012 Key Research Findings
NAPTF Capabilities — 9 Construction Cycles

The National Airport Pavement Test Facility (NAPTF) at the William J. Hughes Technical Center is a state-of-the-art full-scale accelerated pavement testing facility built in partnership with the Boeing Company (completed April 1999). The National Airport Pavement Test Vehicle (NAPTV) has two loading carriages, configurable for up to 10 wheels per carriage, loads up to 75,000 lbs per wheel, with controlled aircraft wander simulation.

9 Construction Cycles Completed (as of 2025): CC1 (validate computer-based design procedures; landing gear configuration data; CBR alpha factors) → CC3 (improve thickness design for 4-wheel and 6-wheel gear) → CC4 (unbonded concrete overlays) → CC5–CC8 (fatigue model/base thickness; geosynthetics; Phase 4 strength) → CC9 (flexible pavement; dynamic backcalculation validation; FAARFIELD fatigue model refinement). All databases publicly accessible.

Philadelphia International Airport (PHL) is the currently active field instrumentation site. Past sites include BOS, DIA, LGA, JFK, EWR, ATL, WWD, DTW, and CMH. Source: FAA NAPTF Overview | Field Instrumentation

ARAP0012 Recommended White Paper Approach

Strategic White Paper Framing: ARAP0012

1
Anchor on the 30-Year Data Asset — The NAPTF 9-construction-cycle dataset and PHL field data represent an irreplaceable multi-decade investment. Frame the white paper around unlocking this asset's value through modern AI/ML — particularly the physics-guided AI work (ICT/UIUC) that is already FAA-sponsored and ready for integration into FAARFIELD.
2
Target the TRL 6–8 Gap — The TRL matrix shows a clear research sweet spot: technologies like InSAR (TRL 7), FBG fiber optics (TRL 6–7), IoT networks (TRL 7–8), and LSTM prediction (TRL 6) are mature enough for airport-specific validation but need targeted research to bridge to TRL 9 deployment. This framing is compelling to FAA program managers who want applied, not theoretical, research.
3
Propose a Multi-Technology Integration Architecture — Rather than proposing a single technology, propose an integrated monitoring system that combines: (1) embedded IoT sensors (strain/temp/moisture) for real-time structural response; (2) InSAR satellite monitoring for settlement; (3) sUAS photogrammetry + CNN for periodic PCI assessment; (4) edge computing for on-site data fusion; (5) LSTM/digital twin for predictive maintenance scheduling.
4
Leverage the PHL Active Site — Philadelphia International Airport is the current active FAA field instrumentation site. Propose PHL as the validation platform for new monitoring technologies, creating direct continuity with FAA's existing program investment and enabling comparison with 30+ years of historical data from the same site.
5
Emphasize $2.9B AIP Relevance — Connect every proposed deliverable to AIP pavement funding optimization. Research enabling 40-year design life (vs. current 20-year) or AI-driven early distress detection (12-day lead time per IoT study) directly reduces AIP expenditures. Frame the ROI for the federal investment in pavement research.

Format Requirements

  • Maximum Length: 5 pages (excluding cover/references)
  • Font: Times New Roman, 12 point
  • File Format: Microsoft Word (.docx) or PDF
  • Margins: Standard 1-inch margins
  • Submission Window: Rolling through December 2, 2029
  • No Cost: No fee to submit white paper

Required Content Sections

  • Purpose: Clear statement of research objectives and alignment with BAA topic
  • Technical Approach: Methodology, technical plan, innovation
  • Team Qualifications: PI credentials, team expertise, past performance
  • Timeline: Period of performance, key milestones
  • Estimated Cost: Budget overview (not binding at white paper stage)

Submission Routing

  • Primary: Email to Technical POC (ARSS0009 or ARAP0012)
  • CC Required: 9-ACT-AAQ-600-BAA@FAA.gov
  • Subject Line: Include solicitation # and topic ID
  • Confirm Receipt: Request read receipt or follow-up confirmation

FAA Evaluation & Response

  • Response Time: ~60 calendar days from submission
  • Outcome A: FAA issues RFP inviting full proposal
  • Outcome B: FAA declines with brief explanation
  • Outcome C: FAA requests additional information
  • No Guarantee: White paper submission does not guarantee funding

Strategic Submission Tips
A
One topic per white paper. Do not combine ARSS0009 and ARAP0012 in a single submission — submit separate white papers to each TPOC, each precisely scoped to that topic's research areas.
B
Mirror the BAA language. Use the exact research area terminology from the BAA topic description in your white paper. FAA evaluators are matching your approach to their defined research priorities.
C
Demonstrate data access. For ARAP0012, reference specific FAA datasets (NAPTF construction cycles, PAVEAIR, PHL field data) to show familiarity with the existing FAA data infrastructure your research will build on.
D
Cost is not binding. At the white paper stage, provide a rough order-of-magnitude cost estimate. For ARAP0012, the stated funding range ($200K–$250K/year for up to 3 years = $600K–$750K total) provides a target budget envelope.
E
Submit early. BAA is open through December 2, 2029, but earlier submissions benefit from more available funding. FAA may also issue topic-specific closures if a topic is adequately funded.
