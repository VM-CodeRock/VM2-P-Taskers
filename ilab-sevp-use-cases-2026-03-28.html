What Is SEVP?

The Student and Exchange Visitor Program (SEVP) is a component of U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) under the Department of Homeland Security. SEVP acts as the federal nexus between the academic community and law enforcement: it certifies schools and programs authorized to enroll international students, manages the Student and Exchange Visitor Information System (SEVIS), and continuously monitors compliance with federal immigration regulations.

SEVP covers three nonimmigrant categories:

  • F visa — Academic students (F-1) and their dependents (F-2)
  • M visa — Vocational/non-academic students (M-1) and dependents (M-2)
  • J visa — Exchange visitors (managed jointly with the U.S. Department of State, though SEVIS records are maintained by ICE)

SEVP does not issue visas — that responsibility rests with State Department consulates — but a valid, accurate SEVIS record is a prerequisite for any F, M, or J visa issuance or maintenance of status inside the United States.

SEVIS: System Overview

SEVIS (Student and Exchange Visitor Information System) is the internet-based case management and compliance tracking platform that underpins all of SEVP's operations. It is a live, continuously updated database of every active F, M, and J record in the United States.

Function 1

Record Lifecycle Management

  • Creates and tracks student records from initial I-20/DS-2019 issuance through program completion or termination
  • Manages status transitions: Initial → Active → Inactive/Terminated/No Show
  • Tracks address, major, funding, enrollment, and employment authorization changes
Function 2

School Certification & Compliance

  • Administers Form I-17 school petitions (initial, updates, recertification every 2 years)
  • Hosts DSO verification and PDSO role management
  • Triggers out-of-cycle compliance reviews and site visits when anomalies detected
Function 3

Interagency Data Exchange

  • Feeds CBP port-of-entry inspection and I-94 record matching
  • Links to State Dept. visa issuance consular databases
  • Supports FBI, USCIS, and DHS Headquarters national security queries
Key operational reality: SEVP runs on a 12-month compliance cycle. Schools must file annual DSO verifications, respond to SEVP correspondence within tight deadlines (e.g., 15-day RFE windows), and submit complete evidence packages for every I-17 action. The high-volume, deadline-driven nature of this work is the core driver of iLAB opportunity.
What Is a Federal iLAB (Innovation Lab)?

A federal Innovation Lab (iLAB) is a structured, time-boxed organizational unit within — or adjacent to — a federal agency, designed to rapidly prototype, validate, and pilot emerging technology use cases before committing to full enterprise deployment. iLABs operate outside normal program office bureaucracy, enabling faster iteration cycles while maintaining compliance with federal IT and ATO requirements.

iLAB Characteristics

How Federal iLABs Work

  • Mission-driven: aligned to specific agency pain points, not technology novelty
  • Time-boxed: 30/60/90-day sprint cycles with defined success metrics
  • Cross-functional: product managers, engineers, data scientists, and mission operators co-located
  • FedRAMP-constrained: solutions operate within approved cloud boundaries (AWS GovCloud, Azure Government)
  • Human-in-the-loop: all AI outputs are advisory; adjudicators retain final authority per OMB M-25-21
iLAB Precedents

Federal Examples & Policy Drivers

  • DHS Procurement Innovation Lab (PIL) — Streamlined procurement processes using AI/automation
  • ICE Stella Chatbot — Internal generative AI tool for administrative and operational queries, led by ICE Chief Innovation & AI Officer
  • ICE AI Inventory 2025 — 51 active AI use cases at ICE, nearly doubling from 2024; compliance checks, tip processing, and document extraction are leading categories
  • OMB M-25-21 — Mandates federal agencies accelerate AI adoption with governance, innovation, and public trust guardrails
Policy anchor: Executive Order 14179 (Removing Barriers to American AI Leadership) + OMB M-25-21

AI / Automation Use Case Portfolio

The following six use cases represent a prioritized portfolio for the SEVP iLAB. Each is designed to be piloted within a 90-day sprint cycle, with a clear human-in-the-loop design compliant with OMB AI governance requirements.

Use Case 01 · Correspondence

Correspondence Copilot

Type: Generative AI / LLM Effort: Medium Impact: High Timeline: Sprint 1 (Days 1–30)

Problem: SEVP adjudicators draft hundreds of standardized correspondence items weekly — Request for Evidence (RFE) letters, approval notices, recertification acknowledgments, and denial notices. Each requires pulling regulatory cites, matching case facts, and adhering to approved language. This is high-effort, low-judgment work.

Solution: A retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) copilot trained on SEVP policy guidance, approved correspondence templates, and regulatory text (8 CFR Parts 214 and 214.3). The copilot ingests case data from SEVIS (school name, petition type, pending issues) and drafts a jurisdiction-appropriate response for adjudicator review and edit before sending.

Key Capabilities:

  • Template-aware drafting: selects the correct letter type and populates case-specific variables
  • Regulatory citation injection: automatically includes relevant CFR and SEVP policy guidance citations
  • Tone and clarity checking: flags passive voice, ambiguous deadlines, and missing required elements
  • Adjudicator edit + approval gate: no correspondence leaves without human review and digital signature
  • Audit log: every draft and final version is stored with adjudicator ID, timestamps, and diff

Estimated Impact: 50–65% reduction in per-correspondence drafting time; improved consistency in regulatory language across adjudicator teams; reduction in RFE back-and-forth cycles due to clearer initial correspondence.

Use Case 02 · Case Management

Case Triage Agent

Type: ML Classification / Rules Engine Effort: Medium Impact: High Timeline: Sprint 1 (Days 15–45)

Problem: Incoming SEVP work — I-17 petitions, site visit scheduling requests, RFE responses, annual verifications — arrives in flat queues with no automated prioritization. Adjudicators self-sort, leading to inconsistent SLA performance and risk that high-urgency cases (e.g., school facing imminent certification expiration, tip referral for fraud) sit buried in backlog.

Solution: A case triage agent that ingests incoming work items (from email intake, SEVIS event feeds, and field referrals), scores each item on multiple dimensions, and routes it to the appropriate team with a recommended priority tier.

Scoring Dimensions:

  • SLA urgency: days until regulatory deadline (e.g., 15-day RFE response window, 180-day recertification notice)
  • Risk signal: presence of fraud indicators, prior compliance history, tip referrals
  • Complexity: estimated adjudicator hours based on petition type and school profile
  • Volume impact: number of active student records affected if school loses certification

Output: A daily triage dashboard showing each adjudicator's prioritized queue with scores, deadline countdown, and recommended action. Supervisors can override scores; all overrides are logged for model retraining.

Estimated Impact: 30–40% reduction in SLA breaches; measurable improvement in high-risk case turnaround time; supervisor confidence in workload allocation decisions.

Use Case 03 · Compliance

Compliance Checklist Builder

Type: Rules Engine + LLM Verification Effort: Medium-High Impact: High Timeline: Sprint 2 (Days 30–60)

Problem: SEVP compliance reviewers must evaluate schools against 20+ regulatory requirements across record-keeping, reporting timeliness, DSO qualifications, program offerings, and physical campus verification. This process is currently manual, inconsistent across reviewers, and reactive — triggered only after anomalies surface in SEVIS.

Solution: An automated compliance checklist builder that generates a school-specific, regulation-anchored checklist for each review. The system pulls live SEVIS data for the school, compares it against regulatory requirements, auto-populates checklist items with pass/fail/needs-review status, and flags items requiring adjudicator judgment.

Checklist Coverage:

  • DSO count and qualification status vs. enrolled student population benchmarks
  • SEVIS registration timeliness rates (must register students within 30 days of term start)
  • I-17 currency — last update date, program-of-study accuracy, campus address verification
  • Annual DSO verification submission status and deadline compliance
  • OPT/CPT authorization patterns: anomaly detection for outlier authorization rates
  • Student-to-faculty ratios and enrollment caps vs. certified capacity

LLM Layer: For items that require document comparison (e.g., submitted accreditation letter vs. I-17 program listing), an LLM extracts key data points and flags discrepancies for human review — it never auto-approves or auto-denies.

Estimated Impact: Review preparation time reduced by 60%; compliance check consistency standardized across the adjudicator corps; proactive identification of at-risk schools before formal complaints are filed.

Use Case 04 · Document Processing

Document Intelligence Pipeline

Type: OCR + Structured Extraction (IDP) Effort: High Impact: Very High Timeline: Sprint 2–3 (Days 30–75)

Problem: Schools submitting I-17 petitions, recertification packages, and RFE responses send multi-document PDF packages containing accreditation letters, ownership disclosures, financial statements, lease agreements, course catalogs, and DSO credentials. SEVP adjudicators must manually review each document, extract key data points, and compare them to SEVIS records — a time-intensive, error-prone process.

Solution: An intelligent document processing (IDP) pipeline that ingests email submissions, classifies document types, extracts structured data fields using OCR and LLM-based extraction, and pre-populates a review summary for each submission. The pipeline outputs a structured evidence package linked to the school's SEVIS record.

Pipeline Stages:

  • Intake: Monitors SEVP submission mailboxes; classifies incoming attachments by document type (I-17 petition, accreditation letter, financial statement, DSO credential, etc.)
  • Extraction: OCR + field-level extraction for structured forms (school SEVIS ID, owner names, program CIP codes, campus addresses, financial figures); LLM-based extraction for unstructured documents (accreditation scope statements, ownership narratives)
  • Validation: Cross-references extracted data against existing SEVIS record for consistency; flags discrepancies (e.g., address in submitted lease vs. I-17 campus address)
  • Review Package: Generates a structured summary with confidence scores, discrepancy flags, and linked source documents for adjudicator review

Estimated Impact: 70–80% reduction in manual data extraction time per submission; error rate reduction through automated cross-reference; faster I-17 adjudication cycle times.

FedRAMP note: IDP pipeline should leverage FedRAMP-authorized services (e.g., AWS Textract on GovCloud, Azure AI Document Intelligence in Government regions) to maintain ATO boundary compliance.
Use Case 05 · Operations

Digital Twin Operations Dashboard

Type: Data Integration + Real-Time BI Effort: Medium Impact: High (Leadership) Timeline: Sprint 2 (Days 30–60)

Problem: SEVP program managers and ICE leadership currently lack a unified, real-time operational picture. Workload metrics, SLA performance, adjudicator capacity, school certification pipeline status, and risk indicators are siloed across SEVIS reports, email systems, and spreadsheet trackers. This makes resource allocation decisions reactive and data quality questionable.

Solution: A digital twin operations dashboard that synthesizes SEVIS data feeds, case management metrics, and workforce data into a live operational picture. The dashboard serves two tiers: an operational view for supervisors (daily queue status, SLA countdown, adjudicator workload) and an executive view for program leadership (certification pipeline health, compliance trends, geographic distribution of at-risk schools).

Dashboard Modules:

  • Pipeline Health: Number of I-17 petitions by stage (intake, under review, RFE issued, pending decision) with average age and SLA status
  • Workforce Capacity: Adjudicator workload distribution, availability, and throughput vs. targets
  • School Risk Register: Portfolio view of all 10,000+ certified schools with risk tier (derived from Use Case 06), certification expiry calendar, and open action items
  • Compliance Trend Analysis: Out-of-cycle review triggers over time; common deficiency patterns; geographic hotspots
  • SEVIS Anomaly Feed: Real-time alerts on SEVIS data anomalies (e.g., sudden enrollment spikes, mass status terminations, unusual OPT authorization rates)

Estimated Impact: Leadership can make data-driven resource allocation decisions; supervisors catch SLA breaches before they occur; program-wide compliance posture becomes visible and reportable to DHS headquarters.

Use Case 06 · Risk Management

Risk Assessment Workbench

Type: Predictive ML + Explainability Layer Effort: High Impact: Very High (National Security) Timeline: Sprint 3 (Days 60–90)

Problem: SEVP's out-of-cycle compliance reviews are triggered by SEVIS anomalies, tips from other agencies (FBI, USCIS, CBP), and complaints — a reactive posture that allows fraudulent "visa mill" operations to operate for extended periods before detection. With 10,000+ certified schools, manual proactive monitoring is impossible at scale.

Solution: A risk assessment workbench that generates a continuously updated risk score for every SEVP-certified school using a predictive ML model trained on historical compliance outcomes and current SEVIS operational data. The workbench provides explainable risk scores (not black-box outputs) with specific contributing factors, enabling adjudicators to prioritize which schools receive proactive review.

Risk Signal Inputs:

  • SEVIS data anomalies: enrollment velocity, status termination rates, OPT/CPT authorization patterns, DSO turnover frequency
  • Compliance history: prior RFEs, out-of-cycle reviews, warning letters, partial withdrawals
  • School profile: accreditation status, ownership structure (for-profit vs. non-profit), campus count, years certified
  • Geographic and sector patterns: regional clusters of compliance failures, sector-specific risk factors
  • Interagency referrals: tip volume and source agencies

Explainability Requirement: The workbench must surface the top 3–5 contributing factors for each school's risk score. This is non-negotiable for due process — adjudicators must be able to articulate the basis for any compliance review action independent of the model output.

Governance Design: Classified as a "rights-impacting" AI use case under OMB guidance; requires designated AI Responsible Official sign-off, independent bias testing across school type and geography, and quarterly model performance review.

Estimated Impact: Proactive identification of 20–30% more at-risk schools before formal complaints; more efficient deployment of SEVP field representative site visit resources; measurable reduction in visa mill detection lag time.
