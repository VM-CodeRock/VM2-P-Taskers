Amazon CEO Andy Jassy · 2025 Annual Letter · Published April 9, 2026
Context & Framing

Jassy opens with a deliberately non-linear career biography — "not exactly a straight line" — and uses The Beths album title Straight Line Was a Lie as a structural metaphor throughout the letter. The core thesis: transformational progress is never linear; the companies that thrive are those comfortable operating amid ambiguity, pursuing multiple parallel bets, and willing to tear down what's working to rebuild something better.

Financial snapshot: Amazon revenue grew 12% YoY to $717B in 2025. AWS reached a $142B run rate (20% YoY growth). Free cash flow dropped from $38B to $11B, driven by $50.7B increase in capex — almost entirely for AI infrastructure. Jassy is unambiguous: this is intentional, expected, and will pay off.

1. AI Perspectives

"Every customer experience will be reinvented by AI, and there will be a slew of new experiences only possible because of AI."
Andy Jassy · Amazon 2025 Letter · on AI's scope
What He's Saying About AI

AI as "A Once-in-a-Lifetime Opportunity"

Jassy makes six specific claims about AI that he calls "hard to debate":

  1. Fastest-adopted technology in history. ChatGPT hit 100M users in 2 months — 4× faster than TikTok. He analogizes AI to electricity: electricity took 40 years to reorganize every factory and home. "AI appears to be moving ten times faster."
  2. Amazon is in the middle of the land rush. AWS AI revenue run rate is over $15B in Q1 2026 — 260× larger than where AWS's total revenue was three years after its own launch.
  3. Customers choose AWS for AI due to breadth (SageMaker, Bedrock, Trainium, Strands, AgentCore, Kiro, Transform), security, and proximity of inference to existing data.
  4. Custom silicon changes the economics. Trainium2 delivered ~30% better price-performance vs. NVIDIA; Trainium3 (shipping 2026) is 30–40% better than Trainium2. AWS chips annual run rate is $20B+ and would be ~$50B if sold as a standalone business. "Expect tens of billions in capex savings per year."
  5. FCF is being sacrificed short-term for long-term gain. $200B capex in 2026 is not a hunch — substantial portions are already committed by customers including OpenAI ($100B+).
  6. The investment is non-negotiable. "We're not going to be conservative in how we play this — we're investing to be the meaningful leader."
"AWS could be growing even faster. AWS added 3.9 gigawatts of new power capacity in 2025… we still have capacity constraints that yield unserved demand."
Andy Jassy · on AWS capacity constraints

AI Reimagining Every Customer Experience

The Bedrock team rebuilt their entire inference engine ("Mantle") in 76 days using 6 engineers and their own agentic coding tool Kiro. Bedrock processed more tokens in Q1 2026 than all prior years combined. Jassy uses Alexa as the example of painful but necessary reinvention: "We had to completely rewire her brain." Customers now talk to Alexa twice as much, complete purchases 3× more, stream music 25% more, and use smart home features 50% more.

"The temptation is to just add a little AI to the existing experience. That's a start. But, the trick for leaders, ourselves included, is how to get organized and convicted about going back to the starting line and reimagining your experiences from a clean sheet of paper, assuming you were building with the new technology available."
Andy Jassy · on resisting "AI bolt-on" temptation

2. Organizational Future

Strategic Direction & Org Philosophy

Flattening for Speed — "World's Biggest Startup"

Jassy explicitly states that Amazon has been working to flatten its organization over the last year — not as a cost-cutting measure, but to improve decision-making speed and delivery. The framing is competitive: inflections "favor the bold and adaptable." The company must "move fast, have teammates that act like true owners, and be scrappy."

"At Amazon, we talk a lot about operating like the world's biggest startup. It's the primary reason we've worked to flatten our organization the last year, and we're pleased with the improved speed of decision-making and delivery."
Andy Jassy · on organizational flattening

Parallel Bets as a Core Principle

Jassy argues that the natural tendency to "focus on what looks most tidy" is a strategic mistake. Amazon deliberately pursues multiple delivery mechanisms simultaneously: Same-Day Fulfillment Centers (85 in the U.S.), Prime Air drones (30M customers by year-end), and Amazon Now ultra-fast delivery (25% month-over-month growth in India). These aren't redundant — they're complementary, each solving a different customer need and reinforcing the others.

Big Bets in Play

Bet 1

Robotics at Scale

1M+ robots in fulfillment centers. Still "early stages." Expect expanded form factors, grasping, intelligence, and potential third-party sales of robotics solutions.

Bet 2

Amazon Leo (Satellite)

200+ satellites in orbit. Low Earth orbit broadband launching mid-2026. Delta, JetBlue, AT&T, NASA already committed. Uniquely: seamlessly integrates with AWS for data, AI, analytics.

Bet 3

Grocery ($150B)

Amazon is now the #2 U.S. grocer. Same-Day perishable delivery in 2,300+ cities. Perishables sales grew 40× since early 2025. WFM accelerating with 100+ new stores coming.

Cultural Attributes for Navigating Inflections

Jassy closes the letter with a detailed framework for organizational culture under transformational conditions. The key attributes:

  • Right data, mechanisms, and "truth-tellers" to deeply question what's changed
  • Comfort operating with ambiguity
  • Willingness to invent and experiment — accepting failure as a cost of learning
  • Speed and owner-mentality ("act like true owners")
  • Willingness to "over-rotate" on big needle-movers rather than cut it fine
  • Readiness to "reimagine not only every customer experience, but also how you organize and get work done"
  • Comfort "being misunderstood for long periods of time"

3. Jobs & Workforce

How Jassy Views the Future of Work

Robotics + Jobs = Not a Zero Sum

Jassy's clearest workforce statement is about robotics: Amazon now has over one million robots in its fulfillment network, yet explicitly notes it "has done this while continuing to be one of the largest job creators in the country." The argument is both defensive and forward-looking: automation enables faster, cheaper, safer operations — and more humans are needed to manage, build, and deploy these systems.

"While the above progress is substantial, we're still in the early stages of how we'll leverage robotics. Expect us to keep innovating on form factors, use case diversity, agility, grasping, and intelligence."
Andy Jassy · on robotics as a continuing frontier

AI Agents as Knowledge Worker Multipliers

Jassy's agent ecosystem (Kiro for coding, Transform for migrations, Quick for daily knowledge worker tasks) is positioned as a productivity multiplier rather than a replacement narrative. The Bedrock team example is telling: six engineers with agentic tools did in 76 days what would previously have taken 40 people a year. That's not just efficiency — it's a signal about how Amazon conceptualizes its own workforce in an AI-native environment.

Change Management as an Organizational Imperative

"You have to be willing to reimagine not only every customer experience, but also how you organize and get work done. Challenging conventions that have existed a long time (and worked) is difficult. People sometimes call these 'change management' obstacles. That's true, but in times of transformational tumult, they're almost like reexamining your faith."
Andy Jassy · naming change management explicitly

This is one of the most direct CEO-level endorsements of the organizational change management discipline in recent memory from a major tech leader. Jassy is explicitly saying that the hardest part of AI transformation is not the technology — it is the human and organizational layer.

Workforce Reskilling — Implicit but Pervasive

While Jassy does not dedicate a section explicitly to reskilling, the letter is saturated with examples of teams being retrained, redirected, and reorganized: Alexa teams rebuilding from scratch; Bedrock teams spinning up new architectures; same-day fulfillment teams absorbing drone operations. The operational philosophy demands constant organizational learning.

JPMorganChase CEO Jamie Dimon · 2025 Annual Letter · Published April 6, 2026
Context & Framing

Dimon's letter is characteristically sweeping — covering geopolitics, banking regulation, AI, management philosophy, and national security. He opens by acknowledging America's 250th anniversary and JPMorgan's 227th. The core frame: JPMorganChase has generated record revenue for the eighth consecutive year ($185.6B, net income $57B, ROTCE 20%) — and yet the existential risks ahead are as serious as any in the firm's history.

Unlike Jassy's forward-looking optimism, Dimon writes with the tone of a general reviewing terrain for a campaign. He does not sugarcoat: geopolitics, sovereign debt, asset prices, trade realignment, and AI risks are all named with unusual specificity. His AI commentary is simultaneously more cautious about society-level disruption and more practically specific about JPMC's implementation approach.

1. AI Perspectives

"The importance of AI is real — and while I hesitate to use the word transformational — it is. The pace of adoption will likely be far faster than prior technological transformations, like electricity or the internet."
Jamie Dimon · on AI's transformational character
What He's Saying About AI

AI Is Transformational — Faster Than Any Prior Technology

Dimon's framing is notably more nuanced than the pure-bullish Jassy position. He "hesitates to use the word transformational" — then uses it. He makes the following substantive points:

  • AI deployment will accelerate faster than electricity or the internet, which took decades.
  • "We will deploy AI, as we deploy all technology, to do a better job for our customers (and employees)."
  • "AI will affect virtually every function, application and process in the company."
  • "In the long run, it will have a huge positive impact on productivity." He goes further: "I do not think it is an exaggeration to say that AI will cure some cancers, create new composites and reduce accidental deaths."
  • Key uncertainty: "We do not yet know exactly how AI will unfold." No single tool will dominate. Power consumption, cost, chip technology, and data center deployment will shift constantly.
  • "The investment in AI is not a speculative bubble; rather, it will deliver significant benefits."
"Eventually, AI will allow clients to predict cash flow needs and anticipate upcoming bills, doing their budgeting for them."
Jamie Dimon · on AI in consumer financial services

AI Risks Are Real — and Must Be Managed

Dimon is the more explicit of the two CEOs on AI risks:

  • Deepfakes and misinformation
  • Cybersecurity vulnerabilities
  • Agentic commerce risks — "risks associated with the misuse of customer data… likely to get far worse with AI and agentic commerce"
  • "The worst mistakes we can make are predictable: overreact at the first serious incident and regulate out important innovation or underreact and fail to learn from what went wrong."

JPMC's AI Implementation

Dimon describes a structured, company-wide approach to AI deployment:

  • AI is embedded in "product design and rollout" across all business lines
  • Companywide AI platforms built for reusability — "certain platforms need to be companywide and easily deployed"
  • Small, dedicated teams get "authorized with the decision-making ability to move and act like Navy SEALs"
  • Special Advisory Services now formally offers clients access to JPMC's own AI expertise — a direct revenue opportunity from internal AI capability
  • AI-powered Smart Cash (automatic cash management), best-in-class retail trade execution, fraud prevention capabilities being extended to third parties
"AI will also introduce serious new risks — from deepfakes and misinformation to cybersecurity vulnerabilities. These risks are real, but they are manageable if companies, regulators and governments prepare."
Jamie Dimon · on AI risk management

Second- and Third-Order AI Effects

Dimon makes a point rare among CEO letters: he calls for explicit monitoring of second- and third-order effects of AI on society — comparing these to how cars created suburbs, agriculture created cities, and the 1969 internet eventually created mobile phones and social media. This is an unusually sophisticated systemic framing that most AI commentary misses.

2. Organizational Future

JPMC's Strategic Direction

Small Teams, Maximum Speed — "Navy SEAL Model"

Dimon dedicates an entire management learning section to the imperative of small, dedicated, autonomous teams — a direct parallel to Jassy's "startup" framing, but expressed in military terms. The key insight: you cannot win at the detailed segment level with teams where a new initiative is "1% of a lot of people's jobs." You need a team 100% dedicated to the mission.

"The teams needed to tackle these challenges should be small and authorized with the decision-making ability to move and act like Navy SEALs or the Army's Delta Force. Finally, they need to be dedicated to the task at hand… when efforts are 1% of a lot of people's jobs, it will never get done."
Jamie Dimon · on small team organizational model

JPMC as a "Powerful Neural Network"

Dimon introduces a striking frame: JPMorganChase is itself a neural network — the value isn't just in the capital or the systems, but in the "intelligence of our people and their healthy connections." This is a direct reframe of human capital as investment rather than expense. Culture = competitive moat.

"In effect, with all of this 'culture building,' JPMorganChase is its own strong 'neural network' — powerful and healthy connections between our people. People usually look at investment as capital expenditures, but in many ways, our investment is in the intelligence of our people and their healthy connections."
Jamie Dimon · on organizational intelligence as investment

Key Growth Vectors & New Initiatives

Initiative 1

Security & Resiliency Initiative (SRI)

$1.5 trillion, 10-year plan to finance industries critical to national economic security and resiliency — aerospace, defense, advanced manufacturing. Directly aligned with federal government priorities.

Initiative 2

American Dream Initiative (ADI)

Firmwide multi-year effort targeting 10M small businesses, housing affordability, financial health, careers/skills, healthcare access. Six focus areas; starting in Alabama, Atlanta, LA, Philadelphia, San Francisco.

Initiative 3

Special Advisory Services

Formally connecting clients with JPMC's own AI, cybersecurity, digital assets, geopolitics, supply chain, and talent management experts. Monetizing internal expertise as a service — a notable BD innovation.

Initiative 4

Blockchain & Digital Assets

"A whole new set of competitors is emerging based on blockchain, including stablecoins, smart contracts and other forms of tokenization." JPMC is actively building blockchain capabilities.

Geographic Talent Reallocation

Dimon reveals a telling trend: JPMC has shrunk New York headcount from 30,000 to 24,000 over a decade while growing Texas headcount from 26,000 to 32,000. This is presented as a market-rational response to high taxes and costs — but it also signals that large organizations are actively redistributing their workforce geographic footprints, a change management challenge at scale.

3. Jobs & Workforce

Dimon's Direct Treatment of AI & Jobs
"AI will definitely eliminate some jobs, while it enhances others. Our firm will have definitive plans on how we can support and redeploy our affected workforce."
Jamie Dimon · the most direct jobs statement in the letter

The Honest Accounting

Dimon is among the most candid major CEOs on this topic. His five-part framework on AI and jobs:

  1. AI will eliminate some jobs. No equivocation. The firm has plans for redeployment.
  2. AI will enhance other jobs. Skills + tools = higher-productivity workers.
  3. AI will create new jobs — some visible now (cybersecurity, AI development), many not yet visible.
  4. Workforce adaptation may be slower than AI deployment. "There is a possibility that AI deployment will move faster than workforce adaptation to new job creation. In prior technological transformations, labor had time to adjust and retrain."
  5. Business and government have a shared responsibility to "properly incent retraining, income assistance, reskilling, early retirement and relocation for those whose job might be adversely impacted."
"There is a possibility that AI deployment will move faster than workforce adaptation to new job creation. In prior technological transformations, labor had time to adjust and retrain."
Jamie Dimon · on the timing risk of AI workforce disruption

The "Reduce the Workweek" Prediction

Dimon makes a bold long-term claim: "It will eventually reduce the workweek in the developed world. And people will live longer and safer." This frames AI not as a threat to employment but as a civilizational productivity dividend — though it implicitly acknowledges that the transition period may be extremely disruptive.

Workforce Development as Strategic Initiative

The American Dream Initiative dedicates an entire pillar to careers and skills: "Broaden access to skills-based training through policy and hiring opportunities." The SRI aligns with worker training in aerospace, defense, advanced manufacturing. JPMC explicitly partners with community colleges, nonprofits, and employers to build skills pipelines. This is not purely philanthropic — it is also a business investment in the talent pools that JPMC's corporate clients need.

"Jobs create dignity and self-worth — and attaining that first rung almost always leads to the second, which, in turn, fosters many positive social outcomes."
Jamie Dimon · on the social value of employment

The Most Important Convergence

The Convergence Signal

"The hardest part of AI transformation is not the technology. It is the human and organizational layer."

  • Dimon builds a management philosophy section entirely around how to get organizations to move at AI speed — and explicitly warns that workforce adaptation may be the binding constraint on AI's benefits.

The Changeis Lens — Strategic Takeaways for Varun

Changeis operates at the intersection of federal IT consulting, digital transformation, and organizational change management. Both CEO letters provide direct strategic input for how Changeis should position itself, frame client conversations, and pursue BD opportunities over the next 12–24 months.

Priority Positioning Shift

Stop leading with "AI adoption." Start leading with "organizational survival in an AI-accelerated world." Both Jassy and Dimon demonstrate that the hardest challenge is not the technology — it's flattening organizations, empowering small teams, retooling culture, and preparing workforces. That is Changeis's domain.

Strategic Takeaway 1 — Change Management Is the AI Bottleneck

Jassy calls it "almost like reexamining your faith." Dimon warns that AI deployment may move faster than workforce adaptation. Every federal agency Changeis serves is facing the same challenge: they are being pushed to adopt AI-enabled systems, but they lack the organizational capacity to absorb the change.

For BD Conversations

Lead with the organizational problem, not the AI solution

"Our clients at Amazon and JPMorgan are discovering that the binding constraint on AI ROI is not technology — it's organizational readiness. Changeis specializes in exactly that transition."

For Proposals

Frame change management as AI enablement

Restructure Changeis's change management offerings as prerequisite infrastructure for AI transformation — not as an add-on service. Cite Jassy and Dimon as validation that top operators agree.

Strategic Takeaway 2 — Workforce Reskilling Is a Federal Budget Priority

Dimon dedicates a full pillar of his American Dream Initiative to careers and skills, advocates for EITC expansion, and explicitly calls on government and business to co-invest in reskilling. Federal agencies are now facing the same question JPMC is: how do we redeploy the workforce AI displaces into higher-value roles?

Changeis Opportunity
  • Position workforce transformation services alongside AI modernization contracts — not as separate engagements.
  • Target CHCO (Chief Human Capital Officer) offices and workforce development programs at DHS, DOD, OPM, and civilian agencies.
  • Frame reskilling not as "training" but as "organizational redeployment for AI readiness" — consistent with Dimon's vocabulary.
  • Cite JPMC's explicit acknowledgment that government + business must co-invest in reskilling — federally-funded programs exist exactly for this.

Strategic Takeaway 3 — AWS and Federal Cloud Are Validated at the Highest Level

Jassy names CIA, NASA, and government entities among AWS's most significant enterprise wins. The letter validates what Changeis already knows: federal agencies are moving to cloud and AI infrastructure at scale. AWS's $142B run rate (85% of global IT spend still on-premises) signals a decade-long migration wave ahead.

BD Angle

Cloud + AI Migration as Prime Pipeline

The "85% of IT spend still on-premises" figure is Changeis's addressable market framing. Every percentage point of that migration is a potential engagement. Position as the organizational bridge — not just the technical one.

Proposal Angle

Bedrock / Agents as Reference Architecture

Amazon's agent ecosystem (Bedrock, Kiro, Transform, AgentCore) is increasingly appearing in federal procurement. Changeis can position its services around adoption and change management for these exact tools.

Strategic Takeaway 4 — National Security + AI = Emerging Federal Mega-Theme

Dimon's Security and Resiliency Initiative — $1.5 trillion over 10 years, focused on aerospace, defense, advanced manufacturing — aligns with what multiple federal budget cycles are prioritizing. AI for national security, supply chain resilience, critical infrastructure protection, and military readiness are all active federal spending areas.

Changeis Federal Positioning Opportunity
  • Develop a "AI + National Security Readiness" service line or at minimum a BD narrative that connects Changeis's digital transformation expertise to DOD, DHS, and IC modernization priorities.
  • Dimon's SRI validates the federal government's own stated priorities — use this alignment in proposal narratives and capability statements.
  • The JPMC model of "Special Advisory Services" — connecting clients to internal AI/cybersecurity/geopolitics expertise — is a template for Changeis to consider for its own service expansion.

Strategic Takeaway 5 — Small Team Model Has Direct Federal Consulting Relevance

Both Jassy and Dimon describe the same organizational fix: smaller, dedicated, empowered teams with clear missions — rather than large committees with shared accountability. Federal IT programs are notorious for the opposite: large PMOs, diffuse accountability, slow decision-making.

"When efforts are 1% of a lot of people's jobs, it will never get done. You need a team 100% dedicated to the mission."
Jamie Dimon · directly applicable to federal program management failures

Changeis can use this framing directly with federal CIOs and agency leaders: "Your AI transformation is failing for the same reason JPMorgan almost failed — too many people with partial ownership. We help you build dedicated, empowered delivery teams."
