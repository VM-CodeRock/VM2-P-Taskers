ATEPS-II SDB Action Research CONFIDENTIAL

Is there a coordinated pattern of Small Disadvantaged Business contract terminations?

Confidential Research Brief · March 16, 2026 · Changeis, Inc.
Executive Summary
  • Documented AI-assisted contract screening is real and precedential. Court depositions confirmed DOGE used ChatGPT to screen and terminate 97% of NEH grants (1,477 grants, ~$100M) in 22 days using an unvalidated prompt. A separate AI tool reviewed 76,000 VA contracts, flagging 2,000+ as "munchable" and resulting in 655 terminations — including contracts for cancer research and nurse care.
  • The FAA ATEPS-II SDB termination pattern is factually distinctive and suspicious. Three SDB-track awardees (Changeis, P17 Solutions, Aurora Innovations) all had their ATEPS-II contracts terminated for convenience in April 2025. The two non-SDB awardees (Noblis, ~$709M; Evans, ~$212M) were not terminated. SAIC was added in July 2025 on an unrestricted track after SDB tracks were cleared.
  • The broader SDB/8(a) dismantlement is systematic and agency-wide. Since January 2025, the SBA cut the SDB goal from 15% to 5%, launched the first-ever 8(a) audit, initiated termination proceedings against ~800 firms (~20% of all 8(a) participants), and reframed the entire program as "DEI discrimination." DOGE's FY2025 data shows small business set-asides account for the largest share of terminated set-aside contracts (3,002 actions).
  • Legal exposure exists but faces headwinds. Challengers must prove bad faith by "clear and convincing evidence." However, the Fifth Amendment equal protection theory — identical to the DOE clean energy case where a court found constitutional violations — offers the most viable path if plaintiffs can establish the SDB-specific pattern was intentional and targeted.
Section 1

DOGE AI Screening Evidence

NEH ChatGPT Grant Review (March–April 2025)

Court depositions released in a class-action lawsuit (filed March 2026) confirmed that DOGE used ChatGPT to screen and terminate National Endowment for the Humanities grants. The tool applied a single prompt — with no defined criteria for "DEI" — to evaluate each grant:

DOGE ChatGPT Prompt — Used to Screen 1,477 NEH Grants"Does the following relate at all to DEI? Respond factually in less than 120 characters. Begin with 'Yes.' or 'No.' followed by a brief explanation."
1,477 Grants Terminated
97% of All Active Grants
$100M+ Reclaimed
22 days Timeline

Key actors:

  • Justin Fox — GSA employee, DOGE Small Agencies Team. Used ChatGPT to tag DEI grants without providing any definition of "DEI." Built a "Detection List" with categories including "Craziest Grants" and "Other Bad Grants." Used identity-based keywords: LGBTQ, BIPOC, tribal/ethnicity, gender, equality, immigration, citizenship.
  • Nate Cavanaugh — Fox's DOGE superior. Used time-pressure tactics on NEH acting chair, claiming unspecified "White House pressure."
  • Michael McDonald — NEH acting chair. Signed off on terminations, was unaware DOGE was using ChatGPT, and did not agree with all terminations (e.g., explicitly disagreed that Holocaust studies constitute DEI). Final authority rested with DOGE, not McDonald.

ChatGPT classified Holocaust grants as DEI because they were "specifically focused on Jewish cultures" or amplified "marginalized voices." Grants terminated included studies of the Holocaust, Soviet Jewish literature, American music history, Black newspaper digitization, and the Colfax massacre.

VA "Munchable" AI Contract Review Tool (Early 2025)

A separate custom AI tool written by DOGE staffer Sahil Lavingia reviewed 76,000+ VA contracts, flagging 2,000+ for termination and resulting in 655 actual terminations. The tool labeled anything not "directly supporting patient care" as "MUNCHABLE."

  • Used an outdated model; only reviewed the first 10,000 characters (~2,500 words) of each contract
  • Misinterpreted contracts, exaggerated dollar amounts
  • Flagged essential services including cancer research, blood sample analysis, and nurse care tools
  • Lavingia was terminated from DOGE after giving an interview to Fast Company about the tool
  • Congressional testimony (June 24, 2025): Groundwork Collaborative testified this AI tool "is absolutely the wrong tool" and disrupted services to 9.1 million veterans

Secretary Hegseth issued a memorandum establishing DOD-specific criteria: any contract not making DOD "more lethal" or aiding in "winning wars" is subject to termination. Line-by-line review of all small business sole-source and set-aside contracts above $20M.

DOT Elimination of DBE Race/Sex Presumptions (October 2025)

The Department of Transportation (FAA's parent agency) eliminated the automatic presumption of social/economic disadvantage for women- and minority-owned businesses. Existing DBE certifications invalidated; recertification required. This directly affects FAA's SDB contracting posture.

Small Business Contracting Macro Trend (FY2025)

Overall small business contracting declined in FY2025 — the first decrease in a decade. SDVOSB contracts dropped to 4.7% — below the 5% statutory goal for the first time since 2011. The government likely missed the WOSB, HUBZone, and SDVOSB goals.

Timeline Anomaly: Award-to-Termination

All three SDB-track awardees received contracts in December 2024 (starting dates: December 1–9, 2024). All three were terminated for convenience with a cutoff date of April 21, 2025:

  • The contracts ran for only ~4.5 months
  • No task orders were issued to P17 ($0 obligated)
  • Aurora received only $461K against a $38M ceiling (1.2%)
  • Ordering period shortened from December 8, 2026 to April 21, 2025 — a ~20-month reduction
  • Terminations processed and finalized April 30, 2025

This timeline — award immediately preceding a new administration, rapid termination within the first 100 days — is consistent with the DOGE pattern of treating prior-administration contracts as presumptively suspect.

Section 4

Legal Vectors / Theories of Recovery

Termination for Convenience — Limits

The government's T4C power is broad under FAR Part 49, but not unlimited. Established precedent allows challenges on three grounds:

  • Bad Faith: Under Torncello v. United States, government cannot terminate to escape a bad bargain. Under TigerSwan v. United States, termination used to favor another contractor may be unlawful. The replacement of SDB awardees with SAIC creates a strong factual basis for this argument.
  • Abuse of Discretion: Under Caldwell & Santmyer v. Glickman, termination must have rational basis. If the CO terminated solely because directed by superiors (DOGE or agency leadership), without independent business judgment, this may constitute abuse of discretion.
  • Impoundment Control Act: Mass T4C used to avoid spending congressionally appropriated funds may violate 2 U.S.C. § 683 — a novel theory identified by Miller Chevalier (March 2025).

Fifth Amendment Equal Protection — Strongest Theory

Most Viable Legal Path

In January 2026, a federal court ruled the Department of Energy violated the Fifth Amendment when canceling clean energy grants exclusively in Democratic-voting states — applying equal protection analysis to selective federal contract terminations.

If plaintiffs can show the FAA terminated only SDB-track contracts (firms whose disadvantaged status is legally tied to race/sex/national origin) while preserving non-SDB contracts on the same IDIQ vehicle, and that this was pursuant to an anti-DEI policy, this constitutes facially discriminatory government action. A disparate treatment claim survives the DOJ's December 2025 rescission of disparate-impact regulations.

Anti-DEI Executive Orders as evidence of intent:

  • EO 14151 Section 2(b)(i) directed agencies to "terminate all 'equity-related' grants or contracts." A court found this provision likely void for vagueness under the Fifth Amendment.
  • OPM Guidance (Jan. 21, 2025): Required agencies to "review, identify, and report all government contracts connected to DEIA programs" — specifically naming WOSB, SDVOSB, HUBZone, and SDB as categories to be identified. This creates a documentary chain of intent.

APA Challenge

The APA requires agency action to be neither "arbitrary and capricious" nor "contrary to law." In the HUD FHIP case (March 2025), the court found identical form-letter termination notices — without reference to program goals — constituted arbitrary and capricious action. This is structurally parallel to the ATEPS-II pattern.

Termination Settlement Strategy

Regardless of any legal challenge, P17 and Aurora face an April 2026 deadline for termination settlement proposals under FAR 52.249-2. Given the federal workforce reduction (~330,000+ employees), submitting proposals promptly is strongly advisable while contracting officers remain available.
