ATEPS-II SDB Action Research

Is there a coordinated pattern of Small Disadvantaged Business contract terminations?

Confidential Research Brief · March 16, 2026 · Changeis, Inc.
Executive Summary
  • AI-assisted contract screening is documented and precedential. Court depositions confirmed DOGE used ChatGPT to screen and terminate 97% of NEH grants (~$100M) in 22 days using an unvalidated prompt with no defined DEI criteria. A separate DOGE AI tool reviewed 76,000 VA contracts, flagging 2,000+ as “munchable” and terminating 655 — including cancer research and nurse care contracts.
  • The FAA ATEPS-II SDB termination pattern is factually distinctive and suspicious. Three SDB-track awardees (Changeis, P17 Solutions, Aurora Innovations) all had ATEPS-II contracts terminated for convenience in April 2025 — within months of award. The two non-SDB awardees (Noblis ~$709M, Evans ~$212M) were not terminated. SAIC was added in July 2025 on an unrestricted track after SDB slots were vacated.
  • The broader SDB/8(a) dismantlement is systematic and agency-wide. SBA cut the SDB goal from 15% to 5%, launched the first-ever 8(a) audit, initiated termination proceedings against ~800 firms (~20% of all 8(a) participants), and reframed the program as “DEI discrimination.” DOT eliminated race/sex presumptions in its DBE program in October 2025.
  • Legal exposure exists but faces headwinds. Termination-for-convenience power is broad; challengers must prove bad faith by “clear and convincing evidence.” However, the Fifth Amendment equal protection theory — identical to the DOE clean energy case where a federal court found constitutional violations in January 2026 — offers the most viable path if plaintiffs establish the SDB-specific pattern was intentional.

Section 1

DOGE AI Screening Evidence

1.1   NEH ChatGPT Grant Review (March–April 2025)

Court depositions released in a class-action lawsuit (filed March 2026) confirm DOGE used ChatGPT to screen and terminate NEH grants using the following prompt:

The DOGE ChatGPT Prompt “Does the following relate at all to DEI? Respond factually in less than 120 characters. Begin with 'Yes.' or 'No.' followed by a brief explanation.”

No definition of “DEI” was provided to ChatGPT. The model classified Holocaust grants as DEI because they were “specifically focused on Jewish cultures” or amplified “marginalized voices.”

Key Actors

  • Justin Fox — GSA employee, DOGE Small Agencies Team. Used ChatGPT to tag DEI grants without providing any definition of “DEI.” Built a personal “Detection List” with categories including “Craziest Grants” and “Other Bad Grants.” Used identity-based keyword filters: LGBTQ, BIPOC, tribal/ethnicity, gender, equality, immigration.
  • Nate Cavanaugh — Fox’s DOGE superior. Used time-pressure tactics on NEH acting chair, claiming unspecified “White House pressure.”
  • Michael McDonald — NEH acting chair. Signed off on terminations; was unaware DOGE was using ChatGPT; did not agree with all terminations (explicitly disagreed that Holocaust studies constitute DEI). Final authority for cancellations rested with DOGE, not McDonald.

1.2   VA “Munchable” AI Contract Review Tool (Early 2025)

A separate AI tool written by DOGE staffer Sahil Lavingia reviewed 76,000+ VA contracts, flagging 2,000+ for termination as “MUNCHABLE” — anything not “directly supporting patient care.” The tool resulted in 655 actual terminations.

  • Used an outdated model; only reviewed the first 10,000 characters (~2,500 words) of each contract
  • Misinterpreted contracts, exaggerated dollar amounts, flagged cancer research, blood sample analysis, nurse care tools, and internet service contracts
  • Lavingia was terminated from DOGE after giving an interview to Fast Company about the tool
  • Congressional testimony (June 24, 2025): Groundwork Collaborative’s DiVito testified the AI tool “is absolutely the wrong tool” and disrupted services to 9.1 million veterans

Section 2

SDB Termination Patterns Across Agencies

2.1   SBA Systemic Dismantlement of 8(a)/SDB Program

The Trump SBA has pursued the most aggressive restructuring of the 8(a)/SDB contracting apparatus in the program’s nearly 50-year history:

  • Feb. 2025 SDB contracting goal cut from 15% to 5% (statutory minimum); ended approvals based on “unsubstantiated racial discrimination claims”
  • Jun. 2025 First-ever 8(a) audit launched; all high-dollar, limited-competition contracts reviewed going back 15 years
  • Jul. 2025 USAID’s 8(a) contracting authority rescinded after DOJ uncovered $550M bribery scheme
  • Oct. 2025 Suspended contractors over $253M in alleged fraud
  • Dec. 2025 Ordered all 4,300 active 8(a) contractors to submit 3 years of financial documents
  • Jan. 2026 Suspended 1,091 firms (25% of all 8(a) participants) for non-compliance; declared race-based presumption of social disadvantage unconstitutional
  • Feb. 2026 Terminated 154 Washington, D.C.-based 8(a) firms receiving $1.3B in contracts
  • Mar. 4, 2026 Initiated termination of 628 additional firms that refused to submit financial documents
Cumulative Impact

~800 firms (~20% of all 8(a) participants) have been suspended or faced termination proceedings. The program was rebranded internally as a “vehicle for DEI favoritism.”

2.2   DOD “Lethality” Test for 8(a) and Set-Aside Contracts

On January 16, 2026, Secretary Pete Hegseth issued a memorandum establishing DOD-specific criteria for 8(a) and small business set-aside terminations: any contract not making DOD “more lethal” or not aiding in “winning wars” is subject to termination. Line-by-line review of all small business sole-source and set-aside contracts above $20M.

2.3   DOT Elimination of DBE Race/Sex Presumptions (October 2025)

The Department of Transportation (FAA’s parent agency) issued an Interim Final Rule effective October 3, 2025, eliminating the automatic presumption of social/economic disadvantage for women- and minority-owned businesses in the DBE and ACDBE programs. Existing DBE certifications were invalidated; recertification required. Contract goals were temporarily suspended.

Driven by Mid-America Milling Co. v. U.S. Dep’t of Transp. (E.D. Ky., Sept. 2024). This regulatory context directly affects FAA’s SDB contracting posture.

2.4   HUD Fair Housing Contract Terminations

On February 27, 2025, HUD terminated 66 organizations’ FHIP grants (~$30M) using identical form letters citing EO 14158, stating grants “no longer effectuate program goals or agency priorities” without any specifics. This is structurally parallel to ATEPS-II: templated, non-specific termination applied across a legally protected program category.

2.5   Small Business Contracting Macro Trend (FY2025)

  • Overall small business contracting declined in FY2025 — the first decrease in a decade
  • Government likely missed the WOSB, HUBZone, and SDVOSB goals
  • SDVOSB contracts dropped to 4.7% — below the 5% statutory goal for the first time since 2011

Section 4

Legal Vectors / Theories of Recovery

4.1   Termination for Convenience — Bad Faith & Abuse of Discretion

The government’s T4C right is broad under FAR Part 49, but not unlimited. Terminations may be challenged where the contractor shows:

A. Bad Faith

TigerSwan v. United States (2013): Termination used to favor another contractor may be unlawful. The replacement of SDB awardees with SAIC (large business, added July 2025) and the continuation of non-SDB awards creates a strong factual basis for a “termination to benefit preferred contractors” argument.

B. Abuse of Discretion

Emerging theory (Miller Chevalier, 2025): If the contracting officer terminated solely because directed by superiors (DOGE or agency leadership), without independent business judgment, this may constitute abuse of discretion. The volume and speed of terminations makes it “possible, if not likely, that COs terminated contracts solely at the direction of their superiors.”

C. Impoundment Control Act Theory

Mass terminations-for-convenience used to avoid spending congressionally appropriated funds may constitute an impoundment in violation of 2 U.S.C. § 683 et seq. — a potentially novel basis identified by Miller Chevalier (March 2025).

4.2   Fifth Amendment Equal Protection

Strongest Theory

If plaintiffs can show the FAA terminated only SDB-track contracts (firms whose disadvantaged status is tied to race/sex/national origin) while preserving non-SDB contracts on the same IDIQ vehicle, and that this was done pursuant to a policy of eliminating “DEI” contracting, this constitutes facially discriminatory government action.

Parallel precedent: In January 2026, a federal court ruled DOE violated the Fifth Amendment when canceling clean energy grants exclusively in Democratic-voting states. The same equal protection framework applies to selective SDB terminations.

Documentary chain of intent: OPM guidance (Jan. 21, 2025) required agencies to “review, identify, and report” DEIA-related contracts, specifically naming SDB as a vendor type. Combined with ATEPS-II termination of only SDB-track awardees, this creates a documentary chain supporting intentional discrimination claims.

Headwind: DOJ rescinded disparate-impact Title VI regulations (Dec. 9, 2025). However, a constitutional Fifth Amendment claim based on disparate treatment (intentional discrimination) does not depend on disparate-impact regulations and survives.

4.3   APA Challenge

Several courts have applied APA “arbitrary and capricious” scrutiny to DOGE-directed terminations. In the HUD FHIP case (March 2025), the court found termination notices “fail to demonstrate a rational connection between any facts HUD may have found and the choice HUD made.” Note: FAA contract actions may be channeled through ODRA rather than district courts.

4.4   Termination Settlement Proposal Strategy

Imminent Deadline

Under FAR 52.249-2, termination settlement proposals must be filed within 1 year of termination notice. The April 2025 termination creates an April 2026 deadline — imminent. Given federal workforce reductions (~330,000+ employees), submitting proposals promptly is strongly advisable to capture costs while contracting officers remain available.
