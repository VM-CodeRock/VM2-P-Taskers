WH Federal Contracting EO Analysis

"Promoting Efficiency, Accountability, and Performance in Federal Contracting" — April 30, 2026
Prepared for Varun Malhotra, President — Changeis Inc.
Created: 2026-04-30
1 · Executive Summary

President Trump signed this Executive Order on April 30, 2026, mandating a government-wide shift away from cost-reimbursement and time-and-material contracts toward fixed-price, performance-based contracting. With $120 billion in cost-reimbursement consulting spend identified in FY2024 alone, this is a structural procurement reset — not an administrative tweak. For Changeis, the stakes are direct and immediate.

  • Fixed-price is now the default. All agencies must shift to fixed-price contracting as the presumptive vehicle. Any cost-reimbursement, T&M, or labor-hour contract now requires a written justification from the contracting officer — and for DHS contracts above $25M, agency head sign-off. This directly threatens the T&M structures common in Changeis's IT and management consulting work.
  • Your 10 largest non-fixed-price contracts at every agency will be reviewed within 90 days. Agency heads must attempt to restructure, renegotiate, or modify their top cost-reimbursement contracts toward fixed prices by July 29, 2026. Changeis's existing IDIQ task orders may be candidates for renegotiation pressure at DHS and DOT.
  • Performance-based metrics become the evaluation differentiator. The EO explicitly links profit to performance and frames past delivery rigor as the new competitive currency. Changeis must immediately position its CPARS record and outcome delivery history as proposal advantages.
  • FAR/DFARS amendments are coming within 120 days. OFPP must propose regulatory changes by late August 2026. The transition period creates dual-regime compliance risk — existing FAR clauses vs. new deviation-based text — requiring close solicitation-by-solicitation monitoring.
  • Small business and subcontracting dynamics shift. As agencies restructure T&M vehicles toward fixed price, teaming arrangements that previously absorbed uncertainty through flexible billing will need to be repriced with risk allocated clearly — affecting how Changeis structures proposals and subcontract agreements.
2 · What the EO Does (Plain English)

Section 1 — Purpose & Policy Statement

The EO identifies $120 billion in cost-reimbursement consulting contracts in FY2024 as emblematic of systemic waste. The Administration asserts that cost-reimbursement contracts provide "little incentive to control costs" and allow "poorly defined deliverables." The new policy: fixed-price contracts with performance-based incentives are the default. Cost-reimbursement is the exception, granted only in limited, senior-approved circumstances.

Key Definition "Fixed-price contracts" under this EO means contracts as defined in FAR Part 16 — including firm-fixed-price (FFP), fixed-price incentive (FPI), fixed-price with economic price adjustment — or contracts that "tie profit to performance-based metrics." This is broader than FFP only; it includes FPI vehicles that Changeis may already use.

Section 2(a) — Default to Fixed-Price

To the maximum extent consistent with law, all executive branch agencies must use fixed-price contracts. The "maximum extent practicable" qualifier gives contracting officers some discretion, but the presumption is now firmly reversed from prior practice.

Section 2(b) — Non-Fixed-Price Contract Approval Requirements

Any non-fixed-price contract (cost-reimbursement, T&M, labor-hour, or other) requires:

  • Written justification by the contracting officer to the agency head (all values)
  • Agency head written approval for contracts above these thresholds:
    • Department of War (DoD): $100 million
    • NASA: $35 million
    • DHS: $25 million
    • All other civilian agencies: $10 million
  • Agency heads may delegate approval to "appropriate non-career employees" — meaning politically appointed SES/PAS officials, not career civil servants
Changeis-Specific Alert Changeis operates predominantly at DHS, DOT, and VA — all subject to the $10M–$25M approval threshold. Any task order, contract modification, or new award structured as T&M or cost-reimbursement above these values will now require agency head sign-off. This adds approval latency and political scrutiny to vehicles previously handled at the contracting officer level.

Section 2(b)(iv) — Exemptions

The written justification and agency head approval requirements do NOT apply to:

  • Emergency, major disaster, or contingency operations (per FAR Part 2)
  • Research & development (R&D) and pre-production development for major systems (FAR Parts 34–35)

Engineering consulting and IT consulting do not qualify for these exemptions. Management consulting and training (NAICS 611430, 541611) are squarely within scope.

Section 2(c) — 90-Day Restructure Review

Within 90 days (by July 29, 2026), every agency head must review their 10 largest non-fixed-price contracts by dollar value and seek to modify, restructure, or renegotiate them to incorporate fixed prices and performance-based incentives "to the maximum extent practicable." This is an active solicitation for contract renegotiation — contractors on those vehicles will receive modification requests.

Section 2(d) — Semi-Annual Reporting to OMB

Agency heads must report to OMB semi-annually: the count, value, and written justifications for any non-fixed-price contracts. The first report is due by July 29, 2026 and must also identify additional opportunities to convert existing T&M/cost-plus vehicles. This creates an OMB-level audit trail on every exception, creating political incentive for agencies to minimize exceptions.

Section 3 — Implementation

  • Within 45 days (by June 14, 2026): OMB Director issues implementation guidance to agencies
  • Within 120 days (by August 28, 2026): OFPP Administrator must (a) propose FAR amendments to the FAR Council and (b) develop agency training programs on fixed-price contract formation and management, in coordination with Defense Acquisition University and the Federal Acquisition Institute

4 · Implications for Federal Contractors

Contract Type Transition Burden

Contractors who primarily operate on T&M and cost-reimbursement vehicles — common in consulting, IT services, and professional services — face the largest structural disruption. Fixed-price contracting requires contractors to absorb cost risk rather than passing it to the government. For labor-intensive consulting firms, this means accurately scoping labor hours, managing scope creep contractually, and building contingency into pricing without inflating the proposal to the point of non-competitiveness.

Performance-Based Evaluation Criteria

The EO's emphasis on tying profit to performance signals a coming shift in how source selections evaluate past performance. Agencies will likely increase the weight of past performance ratings (CPARS/PPIRS) and look for demonstrated experience managing fixed-price performance-based contracts. Contractors with strong CPARS ratings and documented outcome achievement will have a structural bid advantage.

Increased Compliance and Reporting Burden

The required written justifications for any non-fixed-price contract create new administrative friction for both agencies and contractors. Contractors may need to provide detailed technical justifications for why a T&M approach is appropriate — essentially co-authoring the CO's deviation justification. This requires legal and contracts staff capable of engaging at the policy level, not just the task order level.

Subcontractor and Teaming Flow-Down Risk

As prime contracts shift to fixed price, the risk allocation problem cascades through the subcontract chain. Prime contractors must either negotiate fixed-price subcontracts (passing risk to subs) or accept the risk themselves. For small business subcontractors on 8(a) or mentor-protégé arrangements, being asked to perform on fixed-price terms may strain capacity and financial viability.

Overhead Rate and Indirect Cost Exposure

For firms currently on cost-reimbursement contracts, DCAA-approved indirect cost rates, G&A allocations, and overhead structures become less relevant as instruments to charge the government — but remain auditable to validate that historical cost-plus billings were appropriate. Firms should not relax DCAA audit readiness simply because new contracts may be fixed-price.

Broader Context — Related EOs and FAR Actions This EO builds on a cluster of Trump Administration procurement reforms: EO 14275 (Apr 2025, FAR Overhaul/RFO), EO 14222 (Feb 2025, DOGE Cost Efficiency), EO 14398 (Mar 2026, DEI in contracting), and the January 2026 "Prioritizing the Warfighter" EO for defense. The cumulative effect is a coordinated compression of the administrative and cost-reimbursement contracting model that has been the backbone of federal professional services for decades.
5 · Specific Implications for Changeis Inc.

A — Active Proposals & Pursuits (DHS, DOT, VA)

Evaluation Criteria Changes

Agencies preparing RFPs in the next 30–90 days are already being instructed to default to fixed-price structures. Solicitations for Changeis's target NAICS (541611, 541512, 541330, 518210) will increasingly arrive as FFP or FPI — not T&M IDIQ task orders. Pricing volumes in proposals become more complex: Changeis must submit a firm price against a defined statement of work, rather than billing actuals against a ceiling.

  • CBP/ICE IT modernization pursuits: Technology implementation and systems integration work (NAICS 541512, 518210) may retain T&M eligibility under complexity arguments, but expect written CO justifications to accompany awards. Changeis should prepare to justify T&M in capture discussions.
  • USCG/FEMA advisory/management consulting (541611): Highest risk of hard conversion to fixed price. Emergency management readiness and planning work may qualify for exemption under FAR Part 2 emergency definitions, but routine advisory contracts will not.
  • DOT/FAA technical support: Aviation safety engineering and technical advisory work has a stronger case for complexity-based T&M justification, but Changeis should proactively build the narrative.
  • VA healthcare IT and program support: VA is subject to the $10M agency-head approval threshold. Any T&M task order above $10M at VA now requires political-level sign-off — build this into opportunity probability assessments.

Past Performance as Competitive Currency

As the EO elevates performance-based contracting, agencies will increasingly weight past experience managing fixed-price deliverables. Changeis should catalog every FFP task order in its history and ensure CPARS ratings are documented, challenged where inaccurate, and highlighted in capability statements and proposals.

B — Existing Contracts

90-Day Watch If Changeis holds any T&M or cost-reimbursement task orders among the 10 largest non-fixed-price contracts at DHS, DOT, or VA, it will receive a modification request or restructuring discussion by July 29, 2026. Begin identifying which vehicles are at risk now.
  • Modification risk: Agencies may send bilateral modification requests to convert cost-reimbursable task orders to fixed-price or FPI. Changeis has the right to negotiate — and potentially walk away from modifications that present unacceptable risk — but refusal could affect agency relationships and option exercise decisions.
  • New performance/reporting obligations: Even if your current contract type is not changed, agencies may add performance-based milestones and deliverable checkpoints to existing vehicles via modification, to satisfy the EO's "performance-based incentives" language.
  • Invoicing changes: Some agencies may request detailed deliverable-based invoicing (not time-tracking-based) even on existing T&M vehicles, in anticipation of eventual conversion. Update your invoicing templates and PM rhythms accordingly.

C — BD Posture

Emphasize in Proposals
  • Fixed-price delivery track record — all FFP past performance
  • Defined outcome methodology (not just staffing plans)
  • Performance-based milestones and deliverable frameworks
  • Risk management approach and scope control protocols
  • CPARS ratings (especially Exceptional/Very Good)
De-emphasize / Restructure
  • Labor category hourly rate tables as primary pricing vehicle
  • T&M ceiling-based BOEs as primary cost justification
  • Staffing plan-first proposal narratives
  • Overhead rate discussions as proxy for value
New Positioning Angles
  • "Outcome-first" contracting methodology — brand Changeis as fixed-price ready
  • Fixed-price feasibility analysis as a free pre-proposal service for agencies
  • Help COs write justifications for T&M exceptions where appropriate
  • Position as advisory support for agency implementation of this EO

D — Subcontractor / Teaming Considerations

New Flow-Down and Risk Allocation Requirements

As your primes shift toward fixed-price, subcontract structures must follow. Historically, T&M prime-to-sub arrangements allowed flexibility; fixed-price primes require well-scoped, fixed-price subcontracts or the prime absorbs all risk. For Changeis as a prime:

  • Review all current teaming agreements for contract-type assumptions and update them to address fixed-price risk allocation before the 90-day review window closes
  • Small business subs on 8(a) or SB set-aside vehicles may lack the financial capacity to take on fixed-price performance risk — assess each sub's financial stability
  • Build "scope creep" and "government-caused delay" protections into all subcontracts; these are critical on fixed-price vehicles where cost overruns are not recoverable
  • Certification risk: subs must certify compliance with DEI EO (EO 14398) and now also confirm their ability to perform on fixed-price terms

Teaming Selection Criteria

Prioritize teaming with partners that have demonstrated fixed-price execution capacity: documented FFP CPARS ratings, clear financial statements, and track records on defined-deliverable task orders. Partners whose value proposition is primarily "flexible T&M capacity" are a liability in a fixed-price world.

E — Compliance & Operations

Internal Processes to Initiate Immediately

  • CPARS review and response protocol: Conduct an internal audit of all CPARS ratings in the past 3 years. Challenge any inaccurate ratings immediately through the formal response process. Exceptional and Very Good ratings are now a financial asset.
  • Proposal pricing team capability: Fixed-price proposal development requires different skill sets than T&M ceiling analysis. Assess whether your pricing team needs support from a contracts advisor experienced in FFP risk modeling and FPI incentive structure design.
  • Contract-type monitoring dashboard: Create an internal tracker of all current vehicles by contract type, dollar value, and agency — to identify which are at risk of modification requests before July 29.
  • SOW/deliverable discipline: Begin shifting internal project management from time-tracking-based reporting to deliverable-based milestone reporting, even on existing T&M contracts. This positions Changeis as already "fixed-price ready" and makes conversions smoother.
  • Legal review of existing vehicles: Have contracts counsel review the modification clauses in your existing IDIQs to understand your rights and obligations if the government attempts to restructure them.
  • CO relationship engagement: Identify your key contracting officers at CBP, ICE, USCG, TSA, FEMA, FAA, and VA. Proactively engage them to understand which of your vehicles are on their agency's "top 10" non-fixed-price review list.
6 · Industry Reaction

Note: The EO was signed April 30, 2026. Direct named statements responding specifically to this EO are emerging as of analysis time; the following reflects available commentary from professional associations and legal counsel on the fixed-price trend and prior related EOs, which directly inform expected reactions to this order.

Professional Services Council (PSC)

"There's pressure on agencies to use more fixed price that would potentially take away some of the accounting requirements that come along with a cost-plus contract — but the question is whether agencies have the program management sophistication to write statements of work detailed enough to support truly fixed-price vehicles, especially in complex IT and advisory work." — PSC Member Commentary, Federal News Network Federal Drive (January 2026), reflecting ongoing industry tension over the fixed-price push

PSC has consistently advocated for contract-type flexibility based on risk and complexity rather than administrative uniformity. The association is expected to formally comment during the FAR rulemaking phase and will likely argue for robust T&M exemptions for advisory, research, and complex systems integration work.

Legal Counsel (Wiley Rein / Holland & Knight / Crowell & Moring)

"Implementation through class deviations creates near-term uncertainty regarding which provisions apply to current and upcoming procurements. Contractors should prepare for potential contractual variability but may benefit from reduced administrative burdens in the long term." — Wiley Rein LLP, "Decoding the FAR Overhaul" (2026), on the dual-regime compliance challenge during the RFO transition
"The formal rulemaking process implementing the revisions is set to take effect in 2026, which is when contractors will see the new provisions in solicitations and contracts… Contractors will need to closely monitor solicitation-specific updates, clause numbering, and content that might differ from Title 48 of the CFR, and elevated compliance risks during this transition period." — Jeremy D. Burkhart, Government Contracts Partner, Holland & Knight LLP (Crowell/Law360 GovCon Policy Watch, January 2026)
"The trend toward speed and commercial solutions over traditional cost-plus vehicles reflects a wartime footing framework — agencies are being directed to acquire capabilities quickly, even if that means compromising full-and-open competition procedures." — M. Yuan Zhou, Counsel, Crowell & Moring LLP (Law360 Government Contracts Policy Watch, January 2026)

FaegreDrinker / Bradley (Q1 2026 Government Contracts Review)

Bradley's April 2026 Q1 review noted that "the result of the class deviations is that contractors increasingly face dual regimes of governing regulations: deviation-based FAR texts versus codified FAR clauses in the Code of Federal Regulations" — a compliance complexity that will compound as fixed-price requirements layer onto the existing RFO transition. Source: FaegreDrinker Q1 2026 GovCon Review and Bradley BuildSmart Q1 2026 GovCon Law.

RSM US (Defense Contractor Accountability Analysis)

"Performance, reinvestment, governance and accountability are becoming integral measures of contractor reliability. As scrutiny moves beyond cost allowability and into capital strategy and incentive alignment, the definition of a responsible contractor is expanding." — RSM US, "Defense Contractor Accountability Under the New Executive Order" (March 2026)
