Research Plan & Approach

This white paper is built on a structured research approach across seven domains: (1) the current state and 3–5 year trajectory of exponential technologies — AI/ML, agentic AI, quantum computing, robotics, and digital twins; (2) documented implementations of agentic AI in federal agencies including GSA, DoD, and the State Department; (3) FAA-specific modernization programs including NextGen, SWIM, and emerging digital twin initiatives; (4) the full NSEMS scope across its three mission pillars — Safety, Execution, and Management — and how each functional work area maps to exponential technology transformation; (5) federal acquisition strategy innovation, including multiple-award IDIQ precedents such as SE2020, OASIS+, and CIO-SP4; (6) case studies from aviation, defense, and commercial infrastructure management; and (7) thought leadership on agentic organizational design and the future of work. The goal: arm the FAA with the intellectual framework to evolve both its requirements and its acquisition strategy for NSEMS.

Section 01

Executive Summary

The NAS Enterprise Management System (NSEMS) represents the FAA's next-generation framework for managing the vast physical and technological infrastructure of the National Airspace System. The scope Changeis sees reflected in solicitation 693KA9-26-NSEMS — spanning system safety engineering, configuration management, lifecycle support, logistics, and program management — remains not only relevant but increasingly critical as U.S. airspace absorbs new entrants, higher traffic volumes, and accelerating complexity.

But how that work gets done is undergoing a fundamental transformation.

Exponential technologies — artificial intelligence, agentic automation, quantum optimization, digital twins, IoT-enabled predictive maintenance, and autonomous robotics — are compressing what once required large human teams and multi-year timelines into outcomes that can be delivered faster, cheaper, and with greater accuracy. The transition is already underway: as of 2026, the FAA is actively investing in AI-assisted software development, digital twin capabilities for Traffic Flow Management, and cloud migration of NAS systems.[1,2] The Department of Defense has formally declared an "AI-first" posture and launched Enterprise Agents programs to automate federal workflows at scale.[3] Gartner projects that 40% of enterprise applications will feature task-specific AI agents by end of 2026, up from under 5% in 2025.[4]

40% of enterprise applications will feature task-specific AI agents by end of 2026 — up from less than 5% in 2025. — Gartner, 2026

This white paper advances three core arguments for the FAA's consideration as it shapes the NSEMS acquisition strategy:

  1. The scope is right; the delivery model must evolve. Every NSEMS functional work area — from configuration management to help desk operations — can be transformed by exponential technologies within the 10-year contract horizon. Delivery expectations, performance metrics, and staffing models should be written to accommodate this transformation, not resist it.
  2. The agentic revolution is not speculative — it is operational. AI agents are handling Tier 1–2 support resolution, autonomously managing configuration databases, and orchestrating supply chain reorder cycles in production environments today. NSEMS performance standards should anticipate agentic baselines within 3–5 years of award.
  3. Single-award contracts cannot deliver innovation velocity. The technology underlying NSEMS scope will evolve more than once during a 10-year performance period. A single-award structure locks the FAA into one vendor's technology roadmap and inhibits the competitive dynamics that drive innovation. A multiple-award IDIQ — structured with innovation task orders and technology transition mechanisms — is the acquisition architecture that matches the mission's future needs.

The recommendations in this paper are optimistic and pragmatic. The future of NAS infrastructure management is not a threat to the workforce or the mission — it is an opportunity to deliver more capability with greater precision, at lower cost, with fewer human errors, and with continuous improvement built into the contract structure itself.

Section 02

The Exponential Technology Landscape

Exponential technologies derive their name from a defining characteristic: they do not improve linearly. They double in capability, halve in cost, and expand in accessibility at compounding rates that consistently outpace institutional planning horizons. The NAS infrastructure contract awarded in 2026 will operate in a technological environment that looks fundamentally different in 2030 — and unrecognizable by 2036. Understanding this trajectory is prerequisite to designing an acquisition strategy that will remain fit for purpose.

Artificial Intelligence and Generative AI

AI has moved from proof-of-concept to operational infrastructure at scale. The shift most consequential for government programs is the transition from generative AI — which answers questions — to agentic AI, which executes tasks. This distinction matters enormously for NSEMS: generative tools improve analyst productivity; agentic systems change the organizational operating model entirely.

The White House National Policy Framework for AI, released March 20, 2026, signals federal-level commitment to AI adoption across agencies.[5] The GSA's USAi platform — a shared environment for experimenting with AI models across federal agencies — launched in August 2025, with FedRAMP 20x accelerating AI authorization timelines.[6] Nearly 90% of federal agencies are already using or intending to use AI as of early 2026.[7]

Quantum Computing: Supply Chain and Optimization

Quantum computing is crossing from laboratory to commercial relevance in the domain most directly applicable to NSEMS: complex optimization problems. The World Economic Forum's 2025 report on quantum technologies identifies supply chain routing, inventory optimization, and production scheduling as the immediate commercial applications for hybrid quantum-classical algorithms.[8] A port terminal operator demonstrated that layering quantum optimization into truck-to-crane appointment processes — running every few minutes across two shifts — delivered measurable efficiency gains that classical computing could not achieve at that latency.[8]

For NAS supply chain management — where thousands of parts across hundreds of facilities must be positioned against flight-safety maintenance schedules — quantum optimization represents a step-change in what is achievable. The U.S. National Quantum Initiative is funding $1 billion annually in quantum R&D, with the second Trump administration identifying quantum supply chain resilience as a national priority.[9]

Digital Twins: The NAS Already Has One

Digital twin technology is not a future aspiration for the FAA — it is a present reality. NASA Ames has developed a Digital Twin Simulator of the NAS that operates in parallel with the live system, continuously receiving real-world data from SWIM to support "what-if" scenario analysis.[10] Integrated Systems Solutions (ISS) was awarded a FAA contract in 2024 to develop a digital twin capability specifically for Traffic Flow Management (TFM) operations, enabling simulation of weather impact scenarios and policy changes without disrupting live operations.[11] The FAA's NAS Enterprise Architecture division is pursuing a broader Digital Twin Concept for the NAS as an active digital engineering initiative.[2]

Key Insight: Digital Twin Maturity

The infrastructure for a NAS-wide digital twin already exists in SWIM — the data-sharing backbone that makes operational data available system-wide. The NSEMS contractor who can leverage SWIM data to build asset-level digital twins for NAS physical infrastructure will deliver lifecycle management capabilities that current human-dependent processes cannot match.

Section 03

The Agentic Revolution: Autonomous AI in Federal Operations

The single most transformative near-term technology for NSEMS mission delivery is not quantum computing or robotics — it is agentic AI. Understanding precisely what this means, and how it is already reshaping government operations, is essential for FAA acquisition leadership.

What Agentic AI Is — and Isn't

Generative AI (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) assists human workers: it summarizes documents, drafts text, answers questions. It makes workers faster. Agentic AI is fundamentally different: it sets goals, plans action sequences, executes across multiple systems, verifies outcomes, and closes the loop — without requiring human prompting at each step. It is not a faster assistant. It is a digital coworker that owns outcomes.[12]

StateTech Magazine captured it precisely in February 2026: "Agentic AI is that, plus the ability to use tools, to actually have memory and context and reasoning. These agentic AIs are like digital co-employees, digital coworkers."[12] For a federal infrastructure program like NSEMS, this distinction has profound implications: agentic systems can manage Tier 1–2 IT support tickets, monitor supply chain reorder levels, track configuration item changes, and generate program management status reports — all without human intervention in the routine execution loop.

Federal Adoption: It Is Already Happening

Federal agencies are not waiting to experiment with agentic AI. The Department of Defense's January 2026 AI Acceleration Strategy launched seven Pace-Setting Projects, including "Enterprise Agents" — an explicit program to build "a playbook for AI agent development and deployment for enterprise workflows."[3] The DoD's Chief Digital and Artificial Intelligence Office (CDAO) is tasked with enabling these projects across all military departments, combatant commands, and defense agencies.[3]

The GSA launched USAi — the government's first shared platform for experimenting with AI models — in August 2025, and FedRAMP 20x is eliminating agency sponsorship requirements that previously blocked AI cloud services from entering the authorization pipeline.[6] The State Department released its Enterprise Data and AI Strategy for 2026, aiming to equip diplomats with AI tools for real-time decision making.[7]

"Agentic AI can reduce manual work, speed up citizen services and inspections, and free staff to focus on higher-value decisions." — StateTech Magazine, February 2026

What This Means for NAS Infrastructure Management

Apply the agentic model to NSEMS scope and the transformation becomes concrete:

  • Help Desk / Service Management: An agentic ITSM system understands user intent from natural language, decides the resolution path based on context, executes actions across connected systems (provisioning, configuration, access), and closes tickets — without human routing at each step. Gartner projects that by 2028, 33% of enterprise software applications will include agentic AI, up from less than 1% in 2024.[13]
  • Configuration Management: AI agents can continuously monitor configuration item databases, flag unauthorized changes, initiate compliance workflows, and update configuration baselines — replacing what currently requires significant human configuration management analyst hours.
  • Program Management Reporting: Agentic systems can gather data from multiple systems, synthesize status against performance metrics, identify schedule risks, and draft program reviews — compressing weeks of manual PM activity into hours.
  • Supply Chain Monitoring: AI agents can watch demand signals, trigger reorder processes, validate vendor compliance, and escalate anomalies — all without waiting for human review cycles that introduce latency into safety-critical parts pipelines.
Federal Precedent: MTA Rail Inspection

Google Public Sector partnered with the New York City Metropolitan Transportation Authority to deploy agentic AI for rail defect detection — using AI to understand where defects may exist, generate inspection tickets, and train inspectors faster. The same model translates directly to FAA facility inspection and NAS equipment monitoring under NSEMS scope.

The Human Role Evolves — It Does Not Disappear

A critical point for FAA acquisition planning: exponential technologies do not eliminate the need for skilled human professionals — they change the nature of the work. As GDIT's former FAA official Mike Hawthorne stated in January 2026: "We're not talking about replacing air traffic controllers with AI. What we're talking about is making the systems and the platforms that they use better so that they can do their jobs better."[1] The same principle applies across NSEMS scope. AI agents handle routine execution; humans supervise, govern, and manage exceptions. The workforce becomes smaller and more skilled — not eliminated.

Section 05

The Case for Multiple Award: Innovation Requires Competition

The most consequential acquisition decision the FAA will make for NSEMS is not which vendor to select — it is how many vendors to select. The current NISC contract — a single award to SAIC — represents a delivery model calibrated for a more stable technology environment. That environment no longer exists.

Why Single-Award Contracts Cannot Keep Pace

A single-award contract for a 10-year NSEMS performance period would mean that the exponential technology transformations described in Sections 2–4 would reach the FAA only if SAIC (or any single prime) chooses to invest in them, and only if the existing contract structure has explicit technology insertion mechanisms with meaningful incentives. The Federal Acquisition Regulation recognizes this limitation: "competition helps the Government achieve the lowest price, motivates contractor performance, and promotes innovation."[14]

Single-award IDIQs work well "when one firm has unique capabilities or when the scope is narrow."[15] NSEMS is neither narrow nor one-firm-dependent. Its 13+ functional work areas touch supply chain, systems engineering, safety engineering, configuration management, facilities lifecycle, and IT service management — a breadth that benefits structurally from a diverse vendor pool with specialized capabilities across each domain.

$74.8B flowed through GWAC and multiple-award IDIQ vehicles in FY2024, representing 9.5% of total federal contract awards — a trend that continues to grow. — GovDash Federal IDIQ Analysis, February 2026

FAA Precedent: The SE2020 Multiple-Award Portfolio

The FAA has direct institutional experience with large-scale multiple-award contracting for NAS technical services. In 2010, the FAA awarded seven multiple-award contracts under the Systems Engineering 2020 (SE2020) portfolio — with a total award baseline of $1.2 billion — to support NextGen and non-NextGen initiatives.[14] The OIG's 2018 audit noted that SE2020's multiple-award structure was explicitly designed to "enable the Government to obtain high-quality supplies and services and take advantage of the latest available technological changes." The lesson from SE2020 is not that multiple-award is wrong — it is that it requires strong program management oversight. That oversight challenge is itself addressable through the kinds of AI-assisted PM tools described in Section 4.

The Innovation Task Order Model

The ideal structure for NSEMS innovation is not simply "award to multiple vendors and compete each task order." It is a deliberate architecture that includes:

  • 1
    Innovation Corridor Task Orders — Dedicated task order categories that explicitly invite emerging technology solutions. Vendors compete not on lowest price but on technology maturity, integration plan, and measurable outcome commitments. The winning approach gets a pilot; pilots that succeed transition to full delivery.
  • 2
    Technology Transition Milestones — Contract performance periods that include explicit technology refresh gates (e.g., at years 3, 5, and 8). At each gate, the delivery approach is re-evaluated, and vendors may propose updated tools — incentivizing continuous investment in the contract rather than locking in year-one solutions for a decade.
  • 3
    Outcome-Based Performance Standards — Rather than specifying inputs (labor categories, FTEs, hours), performance standards should define outcomes: system availability rates, parts-on-hand accuracy percentages, mean time to resolution for service desk tickets. This creates space for vendors to achieve outcomes through technology rather than headcount.
  • 4
    Small Business and Innovative Company Access — Multiple award structures allow specialized small businesses and emerging technology firms to compete alongside large primes. The FAA's own FTI-2 acquisition study acknowledged that multiple-award approaches "provide greater value to the FAA" through broader competition — even when management overhead is higher.[16]
DoD Analogy: Modular Open Systems Architecture

The Department of Defense's AI Acceleration Strategy explicitly emphasizes Modular Open Systems Architecture (MOSA) — designing system architectures that "enable component replacement at commercial velocity to maintain overmatch." The DoD wants to swap in best-in-class AI and technology tools without being dependent on prime contractor support for each change. NSEMS acquisition design should adopt the same philosophy: modularity, replaceability, and vendor-agnostic performance standards.

Section 07

Recommendations for the FAA

Changeis offers the following specific, actionable recommendations for the FAA as it shapes the NSEMS acquisition strategy through the RFI process and beyond.

  • 1
    Structure NSEMS as a Multiple-Award IDIQ Award the base contract to a pool of 3–7 qualified vendors spanning the breadth of functional work areas. Task orders compete among IDIQ holders, maintaining pricing pressure and ensuring that no single vendor's technology limitations constrain mission delivery. Align with FAR preference for multiple-award and the DoD's MOSA philosophy. The management overhead is real but manageable — and far lower than the opportunity cost of a decade locked into a single vendor's roadmap.
  • 2
    Write Performance Standards Around Outcomes, Not Inputs Avoid specifying labor categories and FTE counts as primary performance metrics. Define outcomes: system availability percentages, mean time to resolution, parts fill rates, configuration compliance percentages. Outcome-based standards give vendors the contractual freedom to achieve results through technology — and create incentives to invest in it.
  • 3
    Create an Innovation Task Order Pathway Designate a category of task orders specifically for technology innovation pilots. Evaluation criteria for innovation task orders should weight technology maturity level, integration risk, and quantified outcome projections — not lowest price. Successful pilots auto-transition to full delivery. Failed pilots inform the next innovation cycle without penalizing the vendor pool.
  • 4
    Include Mandatory Technology Transition Plan Requirements Require all IDIQ holders to submit and maintain Technology Transition Plans — living documents updated annually — describing how they will incorporate emerging technologies into their delivery approach over the contract period. Tie annual award fee evaluations (where applicable) to technology adoption milestones, not just service level compliance.
  • 5
    Establish Human-in-the-Loop Governance Requirements Agentic AI systems deployed on NSEMS scope must operate within clearly defined governance frameworks: scope limitations, escalation triggers, audit trails, and human override mechanisms. The FAA should specify governance requirements in the PWS — not mandate specific tools — so vendors can use best-in-class governance platforms while ensuring safety-critical operations retain human supervisory control.
  • 6
    Leverage SWIM as the Data Foundation The System Wide Information Management (SWIM) infrastructure — already mature and expanding as of 2025 — is the ideal data backbone for the exponential technology tools described in this paper. RFI respondents should be asked how they would leverage SWIM data to support digital twin development, predictive analytics, and agentic workflow automation. SWIM-fluency should be an evaluation criterion.
  • 7
    Align NSEMS with the FAA's Three-Year ATC Modernization Trajectory The Trump administration's plan to overhaul the air traffic control system within three years — replacing telecommunications infrastructure, radars, and building new centers — creates a dynamic requirements environment for NAS infrastructure management. NSEMS must be structured to absorb rapid scope changes as the broader modernization program evolves, which further argues for multiple-award flexibility over single-vendor lock-in.
Acquisition Risk: The Single-Award Lock-In Scenario

If NSEMS is awarded as a single IDIQ to one prime, the FAA should anticipate: (a) technology insertion driven by the prime's commercial investment cycle, not the government's mission needs; (b) limited ability to onboard emerging technology firms with specialized AI, quantum, or digital twin capabilities; (c) contract modification negotiations every time the government wants to change the technology delivery model; and (d) a performance baseline calibrated to the prime's capacity rather than what best-in-class market competition could deliver. The SE2020 OIG audit found that a single program office's "lack of specific policy to manage multiple-award contracts" led to missed benefits — the solution is better program management, not single-award concentration of risk.

Section 08

Conclusion: The Future Is Already Here

The technologies described in this white paper are not emerging — they are operational. Agentic AI is in production across enterprise environments. Digital twins of the NAS exist today. Quantum-classical hybrid algorithms are optimizing supply chains at commercial ports right now. Predictive maintenance is reducing unplanned downtime in aviation, energy, and logistics at measurable, audited rates.

What is not yet operational is a federal acquisition structure for NAS infrastructure management that is designed to capture these capabilities at the velocity they will become available. NSEMS represents the FAA's opportunity to close that gap — to write a contract that delivers the mission not just as it exists today, but as it will exist in 2028, 2030, and 2035.

Changeis's message to the FAA is optimistic: the technologies are proven, the government appetite is there (evidenced by the White House AI framework, DoD Enterprise Agents, and GSA USAi), and the NAS data infrastructure through SWIM provides the foundation for everything described here. The acquisition question is simply one of architecture: will NSEMS be designed for the world it will operate in, or the world it is replacing?

A multiple-award IDIQ with outcome-based performance standards, innovation task order pathways, and mandatory technology transition planning is the answer. It reflects how the most sophisticated federal agencies — DoD, GSA, and forward-looking mission agencies — are structuring large-scale IT and technical services contracts in 2026. And it gives the FAA the one thing a single-award contract cannot: the ability to continuously bring in the best capabilities the market has to offer, as those capabilities evolve, for the full life of the program.
