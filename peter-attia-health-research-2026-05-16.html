Executive Summary — 5 Key Takeaways

  • Exercise is the single highest-leverage longevity intervention. VO2 max is the best predictor of lifespan of any measurable metric; going from the bottom 25th percentile to above-average cuts all-cause mortality risk by 60-70% over a decade — more impact than eliminating smoking or treating diabetes (peterattiamd.com).
  • Zone 2 cardio (3-4 hrs/week) is the aerobic foundation. Sustained moderate-intensity exercise at a conversational pace builds mitochondrial density, improves fat oxidation, and creates the base that makes all other training more effective (peterattiamd.com).
  • Strength and muscle mass are the most actionable insurance against aging. Muscle loss accelerates in the 50s and 60s; Attia recommends treating strength training as a retirement savings account — build the reserve now. Grip strength is a reliable proxy for longevity (Hone Health).
  • Know your ApoB and Lp(a) early. These two lipid biomarkers — not standard LDL cholesterol — are the best predictors of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease. Attia targets ApoB <65 mg/dL for his own patients. One measurement of Lp(a) is sufficient since it is largely genetic and unchanging (Fast Life Hacks).
  • Sleep is not optional — it is the foundation everything else rests on. Short sleep dramatically increases insulin resistance, impairs cognitive performance, and raises Alzheimer's risk. Attia targets 8 hours in bed nightly, a consistent schedule, and a bedroom at 65°F (Mattress Miracle).
01 Core Longevity Frameworks

Attia's framework, articulated in Outlive (2023), rejects treating chronic disease reactively after it appears. Instead, it applies a "Medicine 3.0" approach: proactive, data-driven prevention beginning decades earlier. His thesis is that the four chronic diseases responsible for most premature death — cardiovascular disease, cancer, metabolic dysfunction, and neurodegeneration — share common root causes that exercise, sleep, nutrition, and early biomarker tracking can substantially delay.

He frames longevity in two dimensions: lifespan (how long you live) and healthspan (how well you live). The goal is compressing morbidity — staying functional and independent close to the end of life rather than spending a decade in decline.

The Four Exercise Pillars

Attia identifies four physical pillars needed to train for the "Centenarian Decathlon" — a personal list of 10 functional activities you want to be able to perform at age 100 (Forbes). Examples from his own list include hiking 1.5 miles on a hilly trail, picking a child off the floor, balancing on one leg for 30 seconds, climbing four flights of stairs in three minutes, and carrying two 5-lb grocery bags five blocks.

Pillar 1

Zone 2 Cardio

  • Moderate intensity: conversational pace, but challenging
  • Heart rate: approximately 65-75% of max HR
  • Target: 3-4 hours per week minimum; Attia personally does 4 hrs
  • Best modalities: cycling, brisk walking (15% incline at 3-3.4 mph), rowing, swimming
  • Benefits: mitochondrial biogenesis, fat oxidation, metabolic flexibility, insulin sensitivity
  • MAF formula for HR: 180 minus your age
Pillar 2

VO2 Max Training (Zone 5)

  • 1-2 sessions per week, 30-60 minutes total
  • Protocol: 4 minutes at maximum sustainable pace, 4 minutes full recovery ×4-6 rounds
  • Any modality that drives large muscle groups: rowing, cycling, running
  • VO2 max is the single greatest predictor of lifespan of any measurable number
  • Going from "low" to "above average" fitness = ~70% mortality reduction over a decade
Pillar 3

Strength Training

  • 3 sessions/week of 45-60 minutes targeting all major muscle groups
  • Focus: grip strength, concentric/eccentric loading, pulling motions (rows, pull-ups), hip hinging (deadlifts, kettlebell swings)
  • Male benchmark: carry half bodyweight in each hand for 1 minute
  • Explosive work (sprints, skipping) preserves fast-twitch muscle fiber
  • Blood flow restriction (BFR) training for lower-load hypertrophy
Pillar 4

Stability

  • 10 min/day before every other workout; ~1 hr/week dedicated sessions
  • Methods: Dynamic Neuromuscular Stabilization (DNS), toe yoga, core breathing drills
  • Single-leg balance: 30 seconds eyes open; 15 seconds eyes closed (goal)
  • Stability first — 6 months of stability work before heavy strength loading
  • Reduces fall risk and protects spine, hips, and shoulders

Sleep: The Non-Negotiable Foundation

Attia describes sleep as the "base that holds everything up" — not a third pillar alongside diet and exercise, but the foundation beneath them. His protocol from Outlive:

  • Duration: 8 hours in bed nightly (typically 10pm-6am); target 7.5 hours actual sleep
  • Consistency: Same bedtime and wake time every day including weekends — your circadian rhythm does not know it is Saturday
  • Temperature: Bedroom at 65°F (18°C); cool shower or sauna before bed lowers core body temperature to aid sleep onset
  • Food timing: No food within 3 hours of bedtime — one of Attia's single highest-impact interventions
  • Alcohol: Zero biochemical benefit at any dose; suppresses REM sleep. If consumed, finish by 6-7pm
  • Sleep apnea: Dramatically underdiagnosed; Attia recommends STOP-BANG screening for all patients
  • Caffeine: Cut off at noon (minimum 6 hours before sleep)
  • Blue light: Minimize screen exposure in the 2 hours before bed

Metabolic Health Framework

Attia defines good metabolic health as efficient glucose regulation, healthy insulin sensitivity, and low visceral adiposity. His five tools for managing blood glucose (peterattiamd.com):

  1. Nutrition (primary lever)
  2. Exercise (most powerful non-pharmacologic tool for insulin sensitivity)
  3. Stress management (cortisol drives glucose dysregulation)
  4. Sleep (two weeks of 4-hr sleep causes ~50% reduction in glucose disposal)
  5. Medication/supplements (last resort, not first)

Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) is recommended even for non-diabetics to understand personal carbohydrate tolerance and the downstream effects of food, sleep, and exercise choices (peterattiamd.com).

03 Supplement & Nutrition Protocols

Attia's supplement philosophy is conservative: most supplements are a supplement to — not a replacement for — food quality, sleep, and exercise. His framework evaluates supplements by evidence quality, individual need, and cost-risk tradeoff. He is explicit that he does not endorse most of the supplement industry (peterattiamd.com AMA #69).

Nutrition Framework ("3 Levers")

Attia explicitly rejects a single universal diet. Instead, he proposes pulling three levers in combination based on individual metabolic status and goals (peterattiamd.com):

  • Lever 1 — Dietary Restriction (DR): Remove specific food categories. The more calorically dense the restriction, the more effective. Attia recommends eliminating fructose-sweetened beverages, ultra-processed foods, refined carbohydrates, and trans fats. Eating "around the outer rim of the grocery store."
  • Lever 2 — Time Restriction (TR): Eating window of 8-10 hours (e.g., noon to 8pm). Attia eats his first meal in the afternoon on most days. Finish eating 3 hours before bed.
  • Lever 3 — Caloric Restriction (CR): The most effective but hardest to sustain; requires tracking every calorie. Extended fasting (3-5 days) is Attia's most powerful lever when all three are combined.

Non-negotiable: Protein is the macronutrient Attia is least flexible on. Target: 1.6 g/kg/day minimum; optimal: 1 g/lb bodyweight/day (2.2 g/kg) for active individuals. This is the single most important nutritional variable for preserving muscle mass with age (Hone Health / Outlive).

Alcohol: Attia considers alcohol a net negative at any dose. It is a carcinogen, impairs sleep architecture, and has no proven health benefit when studies are rigorously analyzed. Practical limit if consumed: <7 drinks/week, <2/day, and finish by 6-7pm (Penguin Books / Outlive).

CGM use: Attia recommends periodic use of a continuous glucose monitor even for non-diabetics to understand personal carbohydrate tolerance, identify glucose-spiking foods, and observe the downstream effects of sleep and stress on blood sugar.
