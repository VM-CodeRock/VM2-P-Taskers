1. Executive Summary & Compensation Philosophy

Strategic Objective

Design a compensation framework that (a) attracts and retains C-suite talent competitive with the $50M–$250M federal professional services peer set, (b) directly links variable pay to Changeis growth initiatives—NSEMS, FHWA, DOT/DHS acquisitions, and the Innovation Lab—and (c) maintains full FAR 31 compliance and DCAA audit readiness.

Changeis operates at the inflection point between small business set-aside status and mid-tier competitor: NAICS 541511/541512/541513/541519/541611/541330/541690/518210 positions the firm squarely in the federal IT consulting and professional engineering services arena. The firm's active pipeline—FAA OIG, DSCA IT IDIQ, FAA APEX, and the emerging NSEMS/FHWA pursuits—represents a credible path to $100M+ revenue within a 3-year strategic window.

The recommended compensation philosophy rests on four pillars:

  1. Market-Competitive Base: Anchor base salaries at the 50th–60th percentile of the $50M–$150M federal IT/professional services peer group to manage fixed-cost structure while remaining competitive.
  2. Performance-Leveraged Variable: Annual STI (short-term incentive) targets at 40–60% of base for CEO/Founder, tiered down to 20–30% for VP-level, paid on achievement of verified growth KPIs.
  3. Long-Term Value Creation: Phantom equity or profits-interest LTIP for C-suite and key program leaders, vesting over 3–5 years with liquidity triggers tied to M&A, recapitalization, or revenue milestones.
  4. FAR Compliance First: All base + bonus + deferred compensation allocable to contracts must remain within the 2025 OFPP benchmark cap of $671,000. Total actual pay above this level is permissible but funded from profit distributions rather than contract overhead/G&A.

All five KPIs above must be formalized in a board-approved incentive plan document prior to January 1 of each performance year. Auditors may request the plan documentation; maintain a compensation committee resolution file.

5. Equity & LTIP Recommendations — Private GovCon Design

5a. Recommended Instrument: Phantom Equity with Performance-Vesting

For a private federal contractor organized as a corporation or LLC, phantom equity (phantom stock / phantom unit plan) is the most practical LTIP instrument. It delivers value creation alignment without triggering FAR 31.205-6(i) concerns about stock-price-based compensation, provided the phantom units are valued on a formula basis (EBITDA multiple) rather than market price movements.

Phantom Equity vs. Profits Interest — Decision Matrix for Changeis

Phantom Equity (Recommended for key hires, CFO, CRO, VP level): Simpler to administer; no K-1 complexity for recipients; payout taxed as ordinary income (W-2); company gets §162 deduction. Allocable to contracts if structured as deferred compensation per CAS 415 / FAR 31.205-6(k).

Profits Interest (Recommended for CEO/Founder co-investment and senior partners): More tax-efficient for recipients (capital gains treatment at exit); requires LLC/partnership structure or restructuring. At a $300K exit gain: profits interest yields ~$228,600 after tax vs. $181,950 for phantom equity—a $46,650 advantage to the recipient. However, requires book-up of partnership capital accounts and K-1 filing.

8. Tax & Regulatory Considerations

8a. IRC §409A — Deferred Compensation

Any phantom equity or deferred bonus arrangement that does not satisfy the §409A short-term deferral rule must comply with §409A's substantive requirements (fixed payment schedule, no acceleration, no deferral elections). Non-compliance imposes a 20% additional tax + interest on the employee (not the company). The short-term deferral exemption (payment within 2.5 months after year of vesting) is the most practical escape valve for annual cash LTIP awards.

8c. Key Safe Harbors and Protective Provisions

  • Reasonableness Documentation: Maintain at least two independent compensation surveys (Heidrick & Struggles, BDO Private Company, Radford/Mercer, or WTW) and document position matching methodology annually. FAR 31.205-6(b) reasonableness anchors to 50th percentile; document board rationale for any deviations above.
  • Owner Distributions vs. Compensation: For Varun as founder/owner, clearly separate owner/shareholder profit distributions (not an allowable cost) from CEO compensation (allowable within cap). DCAA auditors scrutinize this distinction in closely-held companies.
  • Pre-Established Plans: All bonus programs must be documented before the start of the performance period — not ratified retroactively. Keep a timestamped file of each year's incentive plan approval.
  • CAS 416 Coverage: If Changeis crosses the CAS-covered threshold (~$2M in covered contracts), pension/deferred comp cost practices must be disclosed and followed consistently under CAS 412/413/415.
