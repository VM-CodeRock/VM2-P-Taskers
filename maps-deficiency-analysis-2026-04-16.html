Claim 1 — Q&A Backlog: Over 1,000 Questions, Under 200 Answered

CLAIM 1: Q&A Volume Imbalance VALIDATED
Turner's Assertion

As of April 14, more than 1,000 questions had reportedly been submitted to the contracting office, while fewer than 200 had been answered. Turner argues the backlog is not merely administrative — it forces offerors to make assumptions on material proposal issues, creating unfair competition and protest risk.

VM2 Validation

This claim is validated and credible. The Q&A window opened March 30, 2026 (draft 006 release) and closes April 17 — a 17-day window for a $50B contract with 5 domains, multiple business size categories, and a highly complex self-scoring evaluation. A 1,000+ question volume is entirely consistent with industry behavior on large IDIQ vehicles of this scale (CIO-SP4, OASIS+, and SeaPort-e generated comparable volumes).

The Army's Amendment 0002 (April 9, 2026) addressed some issues but did not resolve all scoring clarity questions. Industry trackers (Trident Proposals, Baker Tilly, OST Global) have not reported any Q&A publication as of April 16, 2026. The April 17 questions deadline is tomorrow — and if no answers have been released, the window effectively closes before responses are available.

Regulatory/Legal Basis
FAR 15.304(d): "All factors and significant subfactors that will affect contract award and their relative importance shall be stated clearly in the solicitation." Questions about scoring methodology and evaluation criteria are material — an agency that refuses to answer creates a factual basis for ambiguity protests.
GAO Precedent (Nelnet Diversified Solutions): "An ambiguity exists when two or more reasonable interpretations of the terms or specifications of the solicitation are possible." A 1,000-question backlog across scoring elements is strong evidence of widespread ambiguity.
Army Response Probability

The Army is likely to release at least a partial Q&A batch before or on April 17. Whether this resolves the 1,000+ backlog meaningfully is doubtful. The more consequential risk is that a large body of questions remains unanswered at submission, which creates protest grounds for any losing offeror. The Army has clear incentive to address this — protests on a $50B vehicle are extremely costly in time and administrative resources.

Risk to Changeis if Unresolved

Changeis submitted 8 government questions (documented in the Apr 14 analysis) covering domain exclusivity, NAICS alignment, ELB revenue threshold, CO signature process, and ISO 9001 entity coverage. If these remain unanswered at submission, Changeis must proceed with reasonable assumptions — which it has already documented in its prior analysis. The documented assumptions become a protective record if the assumptions are later challenged.

Claim 2 — Solicitation Clarity: Undefined Documentation Standards

CLAIM 2: Indeterminate Documentation Requirements VALIDATED
Turner's Assertion

The Army stated in Q&A that it does not intend to provide a complete list of supporting documentation required to substantiate scorecard points. Turner argues this prevents offerors from competing on a level playing field — when documentation standards are unstated, offerors are competing on guesswork about evidentiary expectations, not capability.

VM2 Validation

This claim is validated and represents a significant protest risk vector. MAPS is a scorecard-based self-evaluation proposal. The entire evaluation architecture rests on offerors claiming points and the government conducting a "Verification Review" (Phase 2 in the 4-phase evaluation). If the government does not disclose what documentation it will use to verify claims, it creates a situation where:

  • High scorers who used reasonable documentation may be downgraded without recourse
  • Verification consistency across thousands of proposals cannot be guaranteed
  • Post-award protests become inevitable when losing offerors discover others used different documentation and received different treatment
Regulatory/Legal Basis
FAR 15.305(a): Agencies "shall evaluate competitive proposals and then assess their relative qualities solely on the factors and subfactors specified in the solicitation." If verification criteria are not specified, evaluation against unstated criteria violates this provision.
GAO (MAXIMUS Federal Services): GAO sustained a protest where an RFP clause was ambiguous because it did "not reasonably articulate" a key timing requirement. The same principle applies here: if a material verification standard is not articulated, the basis for downward score adjustments is legally vulnerable.
GAO (CACI, Inc.-Federal): GAO upheld an evaluation where the solicitation clearly communicated what was expected ("clear, coherent, and prepared in sufficient detail for effective evaluation"). The MAPS RFP falls short of this standard in its verification documentation guidance.
Impact on MAPS Self-Score Architecture

The Trident Proposals analysis confirms MAPS has a 4-phase evaluation: Phase 1 (initial ranking by self-score), Phase 2 (Verification Review with downward adjustments), Phase 3 (Preliminary Assessment), Phase 4 (Final Review/Award). The verification phase is where the documentation gap bites. Offerors who claim maximum points for HR metrics, CPARS quality, NAICS alignment, and QP dollar value will have those claims reviewed — but against standards they were never told. This is structurally unfair and legally vulnerable.

Risk to Changeis

This is a HIGH-impact issue for Changeis/ArchZen because:

  • HR Vacancy Rate and Time-to-Fill data (153,000+ points at stake) needs specific documentation — what format? What time period? What methodology?
  • QP dollar value substantiation — what counts as verification of the $2.5M minimum?
  • CPARS quality claims — can offerors supplement missing CPARs with PPQs? What format is required?

Changeis's protective action: Document every data source and methodology used for scoring claims. Retain original HR records, government-signed QP forms, and FPDS printouts. Build a verification package alongside the proposal, not after. If downward adjustments are made post-submission without explanation, a protest based on undisclosed evaluation criteria is viable.

Claim 3 — 13 CFR 125.2(g): Small Business Subcontractor Past Performance

CLAIM 3: SBA Regulatory Conflict — 13 CFR 125.2(g) VALIDATED — DIRECTLY FAVORABLE TO CHANGEIS/ARCHZEN
Turner's Assertion

The Army stated in Q&A that subcontractors and subcontractor past performance will not be evaluated for MAPS IDIQ proposal responses. Turner argues this directly conflicts with 13 CFR 125.2(g), which mandates that agencies must consider first-tier subcontractor capabilities and past performance for small business primes when the prime does not independently demonstrate the necessary capabilities.

Exact Regulatory Text — 13 CFR 125.2(g)
"When an offer of a small business prime contractor includes a proposed team of small business subcontractors and specifically identifies the first-tier subcontractor(s) in the proposal, the head of the agency must consider the capabilities, past performance, and experience of each first tier subcontractor that is part of the team as the capabilities, past performance, and experience of the small business prime contractor if the capabilities, past performance, and experience of the small business prime does not independently demonstrate capabilities and past performance necessary for award." — 13 CFR § 125.2(g) (Cornell Law School Legal Information Institute; amended as recently as December 2024)
VM2 Legal Analysis

This is the strongest and most legally unambiguous claim in the Turner letter. The regulation uses mandatory language ("the head of the agency MUST consider"). It is not discretionary. It was added to the SBA regulations on November 16, 2020 and most recently updated December 17, 2024, making it current law.

The Army's Q&A response excluding subcontractor past performance from MAPS evaluation is directly contrary to this regulation. The only escape route for the Army is to argue that (1) small business primes independently demonstrate sufficient capability, or (2) the regulation does not apply to IDIQ vehicles. Both arguments are legally weak given the broad scope of MAPS and the explicit regulatory text.

Court of Federal Claims — SH Synergy, LLC and VCH Partners, LLC v. United States
"If a prime contractor lacks the experience necessary for award, this provision [13 C.F.R. § 125.2(g)] requires the procuring agency to treat the experiences of first-tier subcontractors as if they belonged to the prime contractor." Further: "Section 125.8(e) requires the agency to evaluate the individual performance of a mentor-protégé JV's protégé member, and Section 125.2(g) requires that the agency allow prime contractors to submit projects completely performed by subcontractors." — U.S. Court of Federal Claims, SH Synergy, LLC and VCH Partners, LLC v. The United States (cited in Turner letter endnotes)
GAO Precedent — Blue Rose Consulting Group, Inc.

In Blue Rose Consulting Group, GAO considered a protest regarding whether 13 CFR 125.2(g) allows an agency to prohibit offerors from relying on first-tier subcontractors to meet solicitation requirements. GAO dismissed for procedural reasons (parallel litigation), not because the issue lacked merit. Turner correctly characterizes this as confirmation that the interaction between solicitation drafting and 13 CFR 125.2(g) is a live and active protest issue.

Identical Issue — CIO-SP4 Precedent (2021)

This is not the first time this issue has appeared on a large IDIQ. PilieroMazza's 2021 analysis of a CIO-SP4 modification identified the same conflict when NITAAC removed subcontractor experience from the RFP: "This restriction makes no allowance for SBA's new November 16, 2020, regulations, which require that the head of the agency must consider the capabilities, past performance, and experience of each first-tier subcontractor." The CIO-SP4 procurement was disrupted by protests on related grounds. MAPS faces the same exposure.

Direct Impact on ArchZen (MPJV)

This is the most directly actionable finding in this entire analysis for Changeis/ArchZen. ArchZen JV is a Mentor-Protégé Joint Venture (MPJV). The regulation explicitly covers MPJVs (per the SH Synergy decision). Under 13 CFR 125.2(g):

  • ArchZen may propose Kaizen Federal Systems (its JV partner/subcontractor) as a specifically identified first-tier subcontractor
  • The Army MUST consider Kaizen's past performance as ArchZen's own if ArchZen does not independently demonstrate sufficient capability
  • This potentially unlocks additional QPs for Domain 5 (Foundational IT) where Kaizen's HACS and SeaPort experience is relevant
  • If the Army's evaluation excludes this mandated consideration, any downward adjustment is protestable
Action for ArchZen Proposals

Include 13 CFR 125.2(g) language explicitly in ArchZen's Volume I cover letter and Volume III narrative. Specifically identify Kaizen as a first-tier subcontractor in the proposal. State that ArchZen's team relies on Kaizen's past performance per 13 CFR 125.2(g) for Domain 5 QP coverage where ArchZen lacks independent demonstrated capability. This creates a documented, regulation-backed basis for including Kaizen QPs and makes any Army exclusion of this experience a protestable action. Raise this as an additional government question — ask the CO explicitly: "Does 13 CFR 125.2(g) apply to MAPS small business proposals?" The Army's response will be on record.

Historical Context — Industry Precedents

CIO-SP4 (NIH NITAAC) — 2021 Subcontractor Issue

In June 2021, PilieroMazza identified an identical 13 CFR 125.2(g) conflict in the CIO-SP4 RFP. NITAAC had removed subcontractor experience from evaluation factors in a modification. The firm published a formal legal analysis arguing the modification "directly contradicts this mandate [SBA §125.2(g)] as it provides no exception for a FAR 9.601(2) arrangement where the subcontractor is a small business." CIO-SP4 experienced multiple protests and significant delays before final awards were made. MAPS faces the same structural risk from the same regulatory conflict.

OASIS+ (GSA) — SB Teaming & Documentation Issues

GSA's OASIS+ procurement also faced industry concerns about documentation clarity and subcontractor evaluation. GSA responded with multiple amendments clarifying documentation requirements. The OASIS+ experience validates that agencies that clarify these issues proactively reduce protest exposure.

Turner's Prior MAPS Engagement Track Record

His LinkedIn analysis of draft 4 vs. draft 5 shows sustained technical engagement. He is not a protest attorney — he is a BD advisor whose clients have real stakes in the procurement. His open letter is the maximum pre-submission pressure he can apply before a formal GAO protest becomes the only recourse for clients who receive unfavorable awards. The Army knows this.
