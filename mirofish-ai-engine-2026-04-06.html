MiroFish AI Engine

Open-Source Swarm Intelligence Prediction Engine — Applied to Federal IT Consulting
April 6, 2026  ·  Changeis / VM2
Executive Summary

MiroFish is a v0.1.2 open-source AI engine that ingests real-world documents and builds a simulated digital society populated by thousands of autonomous AI agents — each with a distinct persona, persistent memory, and behavioral logic. Those agents interact freely across dual social-media-style platforms; the engine then synthesizes what emerged into a structured prediction report. The concept is best understood as SimCity for forecasting: instead of numerical regression, you get emergent social dynamics.

Released in December 2025 by Guo Hangjiang (a senior undergraduate in China), the project topped GitHub Global Trending in March 2026, reached 35,000+ stars and 4,600 forks, and attracted 30 million yuan (~$4.1M) in seed investment from Shanda Group within 24 hours. The simulation engine underneath — OASIS by CAMEL-AI — scales to one million agents and is backed by peer-reviewed research.

For Changeis, MiroFish represents an early-stage but strategically significant capability: policy impact simulation, stakeholder behavior modeling, and multi-variable scenario planning — all use cases that map directly to FAA safety rule rollouts, DHS policy analysis, DOT infrastructure planning, and DOD logistics stress-testing. This brief maps those opportunities, assesses maturity risks, and provides concrete next steps.

What MiroFish Is
Core concept: Feed any document (news event, policy draft, financial report, or even a novel) into MiroFish as "seed material." The engine builds a knowledge graph from it, spawns hundreds to thousands of AI agent personas, runs them through a simulated social environment, and produces a prediction report — plus a live, queryable digital world you can interrogate.

Traditional forecasting models compute statistics from historical data. MiroFish instead models the social process by which outcomes emerge — opinion dynamics, coalition formation, herd behavior, information cascades, and stakeholder reactions. This makes it fundamentally different from ML regression or time-series models: it's a behavioral simulation engine.

How It Works — The 5-Step Pipeline
1
Knowledge Graph Construction Upload "seed material" — a policy document, news article, financial report, or even a novel. MiroFish uses GraphRAG to parse it and build a structured knowledge graph: who the key players are, how they're connected, what pressures and institutions are involved. This graph becomes the "reality" of the simulation world.
2
Environment Setup & Agent Creation An "Environment Configuration Agent" auto-generates simulation parameters. Each spawned agent gets a unique personality, background, stance on the topic, long-term memory (via Zep Cloud), and behavioral logic governing how they interact. Personas can represent regulators, contractors, the public, media, analysts, or any stakeholder type.
3
Dual-Platform Parallel Simulation Agents interact simultaneously across two social-media-style platforms (Twitter-like and Reddit-like), powered by OASIS. They post, comment, debate, follow, and persuade — forming opinions and coalitions. The system continuously tracks the prediction question and dynamically updates each agent's memory as events unfold.
4
Report Generation A dedicated ReportAgent analyzes post-simulation patterns: opinion drift, coalition formation, polarization dynamics, influencer effects, and information cascades. It produces a structured, human-readable prediction report with narrative and data.
5
Deep Interaction & What-If Scenarios The report is not the final product. You can chat with any agent in the simulated world, interrogate the ReportAgent, inject new variables mid-run, and re-run scenarios. "What if we change X?" becomes a live experiment, not a static analysis.

Important Caveats
  • Not a crystal ball. No published benchmarks compare MiroFish predictions against actual real-world outcomes. Outputs represent plausible emergent scenarios, not probability estimates. Always frame as "scenario exploration," not "prediction."
  • LLM agent bias. The OASIS research paper documents that LLM agents are more susceptible to herd behavior than real humans — simulated crowds polarize faster. Diverse persona weighting and multiple simulation runs are essential for defensible outputs.
  • MacOS-first. Windows compatibility is still being tested per the project README. Linux/Docker deployment recommended for production use.
  • Early ecosystem. Only 2 contributors listed on GitHub. The project depends on Zep Cloud (proprietary SaaS for agent memory) and an external LLM API. A fully air-gapped federal deployment requires replacing both with open-source alternatives (e.g., open-source Zep, Ollama).
  • Demo evidence only. The two documented use cases (Wuhan University public opinion, Dream of the Red Chamber novel prediction) are illustrative. Financial and policy simulation applications are announced but not yet benchmarked publicly.
