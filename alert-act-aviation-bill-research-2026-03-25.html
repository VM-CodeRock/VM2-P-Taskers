02 · Background

The DCA Collision & Legislative Response

On January 29, 2025, American Airlines Regional Flight 5342 (a Bombardier CRJ-700) and a U.S. Army Sikorsky UH-60 Black Hawk collided on final approach into DCA, killing all 67 people on board both aircraft. The NTSB completed a year-long investigation and issued its final report on February 17, 2026, identifying 50 safety recommendations spanning collision avoidance technology, helicopter route design, ATC training, safety culture, and civil-military coordination.

The House response — the ALERT Act — was introduced three days later. It was led jointly by the House Transportation and Infrastructure Committee and the House Armed Services Committee, reflecting the bill's dual civil/military scope. A companion Senate bill — the ROTOR Act (Rotorcraft Operations Transparency and Oversight Reform Act) — passed the Senate unanimously in December 2025 and focuses more narrowly on mandating ADS-B In equipage.

Legislative Status (as of March 25, 2026)
  • H.R. 7613 introduced in House on February 20, 2026
  • Referred to House Subcommittee on Aviation on February 21, 2026
  • 84 cosponsors as of March 10, 2026 — strong bipartisan momentum
  • Committee markup expected imminently; House floor vote targeted for spring 2026
  • Senate ROTOR Act already passed; House-Senate reconciliation likely before final enactment
  • Possible outcomes: standalone passage, merger with ROTOR Act, or inclusion in FY2026 appropriations
03 · Bill Summary

Key Provisions — Section by Section

The ALERT Act is organized into two titles: Title I (Civil Aviation, 23 sections) and Title II (Department of Defense, 3 sections). Every section directly addresses a specific NTSB recommendation.

Title I: Civil Aviation Matters

Section Provision Key Mandate / Deadline
Sec. 101 ACAS-Xa Inhibit Altitude Evaluation FAA evaluates feasibility of lowering collision-avoidance alert thresholds to improve alerting throughout flight envelope; considers human factors, ATC impact, pilot training
Sec. 102 ACAS-Xa Upgrade Rulemaking New Aviation Rulemaking Committee (ARC) within 1 year; NPRM within 18 months of ARC report; Final rule 18 months after NPRM — mandating ACAS-Xa on commercial aircraft
Sec. 103 ACAS-Xr for Rotorcraft FAA to finalize performance standards for helicopter collision-avoidance system; ARC established within 30 days; same NPRM/final rule timeline as Sec. 102
Sec. 104 ADS-B In / Collision Mitigation Negotiated rulemaking within 45 days of enactment; requires ADS-B In on turbine aircraft with ADS-B Out and civil aircraft in Class B/C airspace; compliance deadline: December 31, 2031
Sec. 105 Controller Time-on-Position FAA/NATCA MOU review of ATC supervisor time-on-position practices at DCA and high mixed-traffic facilities
Sec. 106 Controller Training Working Group Established within 180 days; reviews threat/error management (TEM), visual separation, simulation technology; report to Congress ≤ 1 year; FAA NPRM ≤ 90 days after; Final rule ≤ 180 days
Sec. 107 Safety Risk Assessment Tool FAA to contract with an FFRDC within 180 days to develop real-time ATC risk identification, mitigation, and decision-support tool; consultations with ATCs, human factors experts, stakeholders
Sec. 108 DCA Operational Rates Assessment of DCA arrival rates; rulemaking to update IFR slot allocation in 30-minute periods
Sec. 109 Time-Based Flow Management TBFM operational at Potomac TRACON and associated towers within 1 year of enactment
Sec. 110 ATC Facility Level Classification FAA/NATCA National Validation Team reviews classification criteria; reassesses DCA and high-complexity/mixed-traffic facilities; elevates levels where appropriate
Sec. 111 DCA Shared Radio Frequency Working group assesses safety risks/benefits of shared frequency when helicopter and local ATC positions are combined at DCA; report and implementation within 1.5 years
Sec. 112 Anti-Blocking Technology FAA assesses feasibility of technology to alert ATCs to blocked/simultaneous radio transmissions; report to Congress with implementation plan
Sec. 113 Conflict Alert System Task Force Task force develops framework to improve ATC conflict alert salience and meaningfulness; 2-year implementation plan; semi-annual briefings to Congress
Sec. 114 Post-Accident Drug & Alcohol Testing FAA reviews and revises ATO initial response protocols; develops annual training standards; audits facility testing capabilities
Sec. 115–118 Helicopter Route Safety Annual review/certification of helicopter routes; DCA-area route conflicts evaluated; vertical separation criteria revised; helicopter routes integrated into Terminal Procedures Publications
Sec. 119–120 Close Proximity Encounters Working group defines close proximity encounters; ASIAS data sharing mandated; annual Congressional reporting on loss-of-separation events
Sec. 121 Safety Culture Review DOT IG audits FAA ATO safety culture and SMS; report with recommendations to Congress; Secretary implementation plan within 120 days
Sec. 122–123 ATC Position Documentation & MIT Review Review and revise rules for ATC position combinations; review miles-in-trail procedures at all Class B/C facilities

04 · Market Implications

Implications for FAA Contractors

The ALERT Act is fundamentally a work generator for FAA support contractors. Each mandate creates program offices, rulemaking committees, study contracts, and implementation projects. The bill's 180-day, 1-year, and 18-month deadlines create an immediate contracting pipeline.

Contract Creation Triggers from the ALERT Act
  • FFRDC Contract (Sec. 107): Direct contract within 180 days to build a real-time safety risk assessment tool for ATC — strong systems engineering and human factors scope
  • Aviation Rulemaking Committees (Sec. 102, 103, 104): New ARCs require FAA program management, facilitation, technical analysis, and subject matter expertise support
  • Controller Training Working Group (Sec. 106): 180-day stand-up, 1-year report — training design, needs assessment, simulation tech evaluation
  • DCA Operations Assessment (Sec. 108, 109, 110): Airspace analysis, data analytics, TBFM implementation — directly relevant to Changeis ATO support
  • Safety Culture Audit (Sec. 121): DOT IG-led SMS review with contractor support — change management, organizational transformation
  • Annual Helicopter Route Reviews (Sec. 115): Recurring annual program — route analysis, safety data reporting, stakeholder documentation
  • Close Proximity Encounter Monitoring (Sec. 120): Data analytics, ASIAS integration, annual Congressional reporting support

The companion FAA reorganization (January 2026) — which created a new Office of Airspace Modernization and Advanced Aviation Technologies office — adds further procurement surface. Combined with the FAA's ongoing IT modernization competition (seeking a 10-year, challenge-based prime integrator), the ALERT Act lands in the middle of the most active FAA modernization period in two decades.

Key BD Themes to Emphasize

1. Incumbent Advantage

Changeis already supports AJT, AJI, AJM, AJR, AJV, AJW, and AJF — the exact directorates executing ALERT Act mandates. No ramp-up time, no learning curve.

2. Safety Culture + Training

Sec. 106 (training reform) and Sec. 121 (SMS/culture audit) play directly to Changeis's AJI safety training support and organizational transformation experience.

3. Data Analytics at Scale

Close proximity encounter monitoring (Sec. 120) and safety risk tool development (Sec. 107) require exactly the kind of NAS data analytics, ASIAS integration, and air traffic tracking system analysis Changeis performs under ATAPS II.

4. WOSB Set-Aside Positioning

As a WOSB with SDB status, Changeis has preferred positioning for any set-aside task orders or small business reserve requirements embedded in ALERT Act-driven procurement vehicles.

5. Rulemaking & ARC Support

Three of the bill's most time-sensitive mandates (Sec. 102, 103, 104) require Aviation Rulemaking Committees to stand up and produce reports within 1 year. Changeis's program management and technical analysis capabilities are purpose-built for this work.

6. DCA Operations Expertise

Multiple ALERT Act sections (108, 109, 110, 111, 116, 123) focus specifically on DCA airspace — where Changeis has direct ATO operational support experience across air traffic flow, system operations, and facility management.

Risks and Watch Items

  • Legislative timeline uncertainty: The ALERT Act was introduced but not yet passed. A House-Senate reconciliation process with the ROTOR Act could delay enactment into late 2026. BD positioning should begin now, but contract actions will lag enactment by 6–18 months.
  • FFRDC lock-out on Sec. 107: The Safety Risk Assessment Tool contract is directed to an FFRDC (likely MITRE, VOLPE, or RAND). Changeis should position as a subcontractor or follow-on implementation partner.
  • ADS-B In compliance rulemaking: The negotiated rulemaking for ADS-B In (Sec. 104) involves aviation industry stakeholders and may be contentious. Labor groups criticized the ALERT Act's allowance of portable ADS-B devices — this could slow or reshape the rulemaking. Monitor for shifts in technology scope.
  • FAA reorganization alignment: The new Office of Airspace Modernization and Advanced Aviation Technologies office (created January 2026) may shift program office structures. Changeis should map ALERT Act mandates to both old and new organizational owners.
  • Competition from large primes: Booz Allen, Deloitte, GDIT, and Peraton are all active in the FAA space. Changeis's WOSB status and incumbent positions are its primary shields; maintain customer intimacy and relationship depth.
