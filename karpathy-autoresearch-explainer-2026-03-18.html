2. What Is Autoresearch? (Plain Language Explanation)

Think of the standard way a researcher or engineer improves something: they have an idea, they try it, they measure whether it worked, they keep the change if it helped or discard it if it didn't, and they start again with a new idea. This cycle — hypothesis, experiment, evaluate, iterate — is the fundamental engine of improvement in almost every knowledge-based field.

It is also almost entirely mechanical once you have a clear goal and a way to measure progress.

Autoresearch hands that entire cycle to an AI agent.

How it works in practice:

  1. The human writes a plain-English instruction file (program.md) describing what to optimize and what constraints to respect
  2. The AI agent reads existing code and forms a hypothesis about what might improve it
  3. The agent edits the code and runs a timed experiment (5 minutes per run)
  4. The agent measures the result against a single objective metric
  5. If the result improved, the change is saved. If not, the change is discarded
  6. The agent starts again with a new hypothesis, informed by what it just learned
  7. This runs continuously — overnight, unattended, without human supervision

The human's role shifts entirely. You stop writing the code. You start writing the research strategy — the goals, the constraints, the definition of "better." The AI handles everything else.

In Karpathy's demonstration, the agent ran approximately 100 experiments while he slept and had logged 700 experiments across two days. A human researcher doing the same work manually might complete 8–10 cycles per day, spending most of that time simply waiting for results rather than thinking.

The codebase is 630 lines of Python. It fits in a single AI context window by design. It runs on a single GPU. The MIT license means anyone can use it, fork it, or build on it without restriction.

As Data Science Dojo's analysis noted: "The agent wasn't doing anything a careful human researcher couldn't have done. It was just running experiments continuously, without the cognitive fatigue or context-switching that pulls a researcher's attention away from the task."

4. The Principle Behind the Tool: The Karpathy Loop

Analyst Janakiram MSV coined the term "the Karpathy Loop" to describe the generalizable pattern this project demonstrates. It has three components:

  1. An agent with access to a modifiable asset (code, a document, a process, a template)
  2. A single, objectively measurable metric the agent is trying to improve
  3. A fixed time limit per experiment, keeping cycles fast and failures cheap

What makes this powerful is what it implies about where human judgment belongs. In the traditional research or optimization cycle, humans spend most of their time on mechanical execution — waiting, running tests, making small adjustments. The Karpathy Loop inverts this: humans spend their time defining the objective and reviewing results. The AI handles everything in between.

LinkedIn contributor Yariv Adan identified five conditions that make a process a good candidate for this kind of autonomous loop:
- Objective scoring: A measurable score that distinguishes better from worse without human judgment
- Fast, cheap iterations: Cycles measured in minutes, not months
- Bounded environment: A clearly defined scope for what the agent can and cannot modify
- Low cost of failure: A bad iteration wastes minutes, not relationships or compliance standing
- Traceability: The agent leaves a clear record of decisions and changes

Notice that these conditions describe many business processes — not just AI training.

5. Beyond Machine Learning: The Generalization That Matters for Business

Karpathy himself made the critical observation: "It's worth considering whether your problem falls into this category too."

The loop he built is specific to ML training. But the pattern — autonomous agent, modifiable asset, objective metric, fixed time budget, commit or revert — applies far beyond machine learning. As Yariv Adan's LinkedIn analysis noted, early applications of agentic loops are emerging in:

Marketing: Agents generating ad variations, running A/B tests against live audiences, iterating toward a target click-through or conversion rate — autonomously, overnight, without a team meeting to review 37 variants manually.

Sales: Loops running cold outreach experiments, testing subject lines, email lengths, and call-to-action phrasing against reply rate data — improving over time without a human configuring each test.

Software engineering: Agents generating code changes, running acceptance tests, keeping what passes and discarding what doesn't — continuous improvement without manual code review for every micro-change.

Product development: An agent modifying product copy, pricing page structure, or onboarding flow against a conversion metric, iterating overnight and surfacing only what actually moved the number.

Operations: Any process that can be parameterized and scored — routing rules, scheduling algorithms, pricing logic — becomes a candidate for autonomous overnight optimization.

The YouTube breakdown of autoresearch (Greg's analysis, March 2026) identified a concrete set of business models that become viable on top of this pattern: always-on A/B testing agencies, niche agent-in-a-box products for specific industries, autonomous process optimization as a service.

The pattern is simple enough to state in one sentence: Any process where you can define "better," measure it automatically, and run experiments cheaply is a candidate for autonomous overnight improvement.

6. Implications for Changeis: Government Contracting Applications

Changeis operates in a domain — government contracting — that is defined by three simultaneous pressures: strict compliance requirements, high-stakes document production under tight deadlines, and the need to improve win rates across a portfolio of bids. The Karpathy Loop maps onto each of these with meaningful specificity.

Proposal Development: Iterative Optimization Against Evaluation Criteria

Federal proposals are evaluated against explicit criteria, often published in detail within the RFP. This is one of the cleaner scoring environments in business — the government tells you exactly what they're looking for and how they'll weight it.

The Karpathy Loop applied here: an AI agent with access to a draft proposal, a scoring rubric derived from the RFP evaluation criteria, and the ability to modify sections within defined constraints. The agent proposes edits, scores the result against the rubric, keeps improvements, and iterates. A proposal manager sets strategy and reviews the morning output rather than manually drafting every pass.

This is not hypothetical. As Inventive AI's analysis of AI in government proposals documents, AI tools are already being used to extract RFP requirements, check compliance matrices, and maintain consistency across sections. The Karpathy Loop goes further: it makes that optimization autonomous and iterative rather than one-time and human-driven.

Specific applications:
- Automated compliance matrix generation and gap-checking against RFP requirements
- Iterative technical approach refinement scored against published evaluation criteria
- Past performance narrative optimization — testing which accomplishments and framing score highest against stated criteria
- Price-to-win model iteration against known agency budget signals
- Executive summary optimization against readability and alignment metrics

Compliance Checking: Continuous Monitoring as an Autonomous Loop

Government contractors operate under layered compliance requirements — FAR clauses, cybersecurity frameworks (CMMC), agency-specific reporting requirements, and contract-level obligations. Compliance gaps are expensive: they generate findings, require remediation, and can affect award decisions.

The Karpathy Loop applied here: an AI agent with access to compliance documentation, a checklist of requirements, and the ability to identify gaps and propose remediation. The metric is coverage — how many requirements are fully addressed. The agent runs continuously, flagging new gaps as requirements change or new contracts are added to the portfolio.

This converts compliance from a periodic audit function (expensive, human-intensive, retrospective) to a continuous monitoring function (systematic, agent-driven, prospective).

Process Optimization: Internal Operations as an Experimental System

Government contracting involves significant recurring process work: capture management, opportunity analysis, team staffing, subcontractor vetting, contract data reporting, deliverable production. Much of this work is systematic enough to be parameterized and measured.

Capture pipeline optimization: An agent analyzing historical bid decisions against win/loss outcomes, testing different capture strategies, identifying which combination of factors (team composition, incumbency advantage, small business set-asides, agency familiarity) predicts wins — and surfacing recommendations automatically.

Deliverable quality iteration: For contract deliverables with defined quality criteria, an agent that reviews drafts, scores against criteria, and proposes improvements — shifting the human role from first-draft writer to strategic reviewer.

Resource allocation modeling: An agent iterating staffing configurations for complex bids, optimizing for cost-to-win ratios, key personnel availability, and labor category compliance — finding solutions a human analyst might not explore in the time available.

The key requirement in each case is the same: a clear, automatically measurable definition of "better." Defining that metric with care is now the primary intellectual work. Executing against it can be increasingly automated.

7. The Bigger Picture: What This Moment Represents

Autoresearch is a working demonstration of something the AI research community has been theorizing about for years: recursive self-improvement, the ability of an AI system to evaluate its own outputs, generate modifications, and improve without human supervision.

The current version is narrow and safe — it improves training code for small language models, operating within strict constraints, with a locked evaluation function that prevents the agent from gaming its own metric. But the architecture it demonstrates points toward systems that could do the same for increasingly complex domains.

Karpathy's framing on X is direct: "LLM labs will do this. It's the final boss battle." His prediction is that frontier AI labs will eventually deploy agent swarms to optimize their own training pipelines at scale, with humans involved only at the periphery — reviewing strategic direction rather than executing experimental cycles.

The Fortune analysis noted the comparison to AutoML (automated machine learning, used by Google and Microsoft for years), but Karpathy's distinction is important: AutoML uses random variations and evolutionary algorithms. Autoresearch uses an AI agent that reads research papers, understands prior experiment results, and forms hypotheses — a qualitatively different capability.

What this signals for enterprise AI strategy:

According to Gartner's 2026 projections cited by CloudKeeper, 40% of enterprise applications are expected to embed task-specific AI agents by 2026, up from low single-digit adoption just a few years ago. The OneReach enterprise AI analysis frames the strategic imperative clearly: "By 2026, winners will not be those who deploy agents fastest, but those who reimagine their enterprise as agent-native from the ground up."

The shift is not about adopting specific tools. It is about redesigning how decisions get made and where human judgment is applied:

  • Before: Humans execute and decide; AI assists
  • After: AI executes and proposes; humans define strategy and review results

This is a fundamentally different operating model, not an incremental improvement.

8. What a CEO Should Watch For

The autoresearch project itself is a research tool for ML engineers. CEOs do not need to run it. But the pattern it demonstrates — and the signals it sends about where AI is going — are directly relevant to strategic planning.

Watch for: The Metric Definition Advantage

The companies that win with autonomous AI loops will be those that get good at defining measurable objectives. This is a strategic capability, not a technical one. The quality of the metric determines the quality of what the agent optimizes for. Poorly defined metrics produce agents that optimize for the wrong things brilliantly.

For Changeis: Define win criteria, compliance standards, and quality benchmarks with enough precision that an agent can score against them automatically. That work has value independent of any specific AI tool.

Watch for: The Overnight Advantage

Agents don't sleep. A firm that deploys autonomous improvement loops on proposal development, compliance monitoring, or process optimization gains compounding advantage over time — each overnight run builds on the previous night's improvements. The gap between firms that deploy these loops and those that don't will widen continuously.

Watch for: The Competitive Shift in Government Contracting

Other firms will apply these tools to proposal development. The Deltek analysis of AI in government proposals documents that AI adoption in this space is accelerating. When autonomous optimization becomes standard practice among large primes, the manual proposal process becomes a structural disadvantage.

Watch for: The Governance and Compliance Obligation

NIST launched its AI Agent Standards Initiative in February 2026, signaling that autonomous AI agents are now within the federal governance and compliance domain. For government contractors, this creates both a compliance requirement (security controls around agent authentication, activity logging, and scope limitation) and an opportunity (demonstrating mature AI governance as a differentiator in bids).

Watch for: The Talent Model Shift

Junior staff at firms that deploy autonomous improvement loops spend less time on mechanical work and more time on strategic judgment — reviewing agent outputs, refining objectives, identifying where the loop is missing nuance. This changes what hiring looks like and what skills actually create value. Firms that don't adapt will find themselves paying premium salaries for work that could be automated.

Watch for: The "Program the Research Org" Mindset

Karpathy's phrase "programming the research org in Markdown" describes something real and transferable. The durable artifact from an overnight agent run is not the specific changes made — it is the instruction document that produced them. Organizations that invest in building well-structured, precise instruction documents for their agents build a compounding institutional asset. The quality of your program.md — the document defining your agent's goals and constraints — determines how fast your organization improves.

9. Summary: The Decision That Matters

Autoresearch is a proof of concept. The 41,000 GitHub stars it earned in days are not enthusiasm for a training script — they are recognition that the pattern it demonstrates is generalized and applicable, and that it works.

The question for any CEO is not whether autonomous improvement loops will become standard in business. The Fortune analysis, the Gartner projections, the NIST regulatory attention, and the speed of adoption all point in one direction. The question is whether your organization defines its metrics, builds its instruction documents, and deploys its first loops before or after your competitors do.

For Changeis specifically, the opportunity is concrete:
- Proposal development is already document-intensive, criteria-driven, and measurable against publicly available rubrics
- Compliance monitoring is already systematic and parameterizable
- The government is itself accelerating AI adoption, creating both regulatory requirements and competitive dynamics that favor early movers

The right first step is not to find the right tool. It is to pick one process with a clear, measurable objective and build the equivalent of a program.md for it — a precise document that defines what "better" means and what constraints the agent must respect. That document is worth building regardless of which tool runs against it.
