Executive Summary

AADYAH Space North America is raising USD 1M as a convertible note to bridge the working capital gap during its acquisition of ABM Inc (Santa Ana, CA), a 30-year-old AS9100D precision machining company with $6.8M FY2025 revenue, $11M backlog, and active programs on the F-35, JASSM, and B2 Bomber. The parent entity — AADYAH Aerospace Private Limited (Bengaluru, India) — is an established space robotics company with 50 engineers, 15 customers in 9 countries, TRL-9 flight-proven products, and backing from Seraphim Space (Mission 13 accelerator) and Keiretsu Forum angel investors.

The strategic thesis is compelling: use a FOCI-compliant acquisition of a profitable US precision machining shop to unlock Buy American / ITAR access for AADYAH's space robotics technology, without the 3–5 year organic entry timeline. ABM's existing relationships with Parker Aerospace, Meggitt, and Lockheed/JASSM supply chain provide immediate revenue continuity. The deal is structured at a reasonable 0.78x revenue multiple (EV $5.3M on $6.8M revenue).

Key risks include: heavy customer concentration (Parker Aerospace = 60% of revenue), FOCI compliance complexity with a majority Indian parent, aggressive revenue projections ($6.8M → $59M by 2030 represents an 8.7x in 4 years), an unnamed GM/CEO hire that is critical to execution, and the structural subordination inherent in a convertible note from an early-stage holding entity.

Recommendation

DEEPER LOOK — The deal has genuine strategic merit and real underlying assets (ABM is a profitable, certified, program-bearing company). However, before committing capital: (1) verify FOCI/proxy board compliance plan with US legal counsel, (2) get audited financials on ABM Inc, (3) confirm the GM/CEO hire has been identified, and (4) model realistic downside scenarios on Parker contract concentration risk. The $59M 2030 target is highly ambitious and should not anchor valuation expectations.

Company Overview & Investment Thesis

AADYAH Aerospace Private Limited (est. 2016, Bengaluru, India) designs and builds motion control, guidance & navigation, avionics, and power subsystems for all spacecraft types — launch vehicles, satellites, landers, and rovers. The company has delivered 15 subsystems, is flight-proven on 2 orbital launches, and holds TRL-9 products (Satellite Propulsion Driver, Satellite Propulsion ECU). Its customer base spans 9 countries and 15 clients across the global space sector.

AADYAH Space North America Inc is the newly incorporated US holding entity. Its primary strategic action is acquiring 60% of ABM Inc (Q2 2026) for USD 3.18M, with earn-out payments reaching 100% ownership by 2028 at a total EV of USD 5.3M.

ABM Inc (est. 1996, Santa Ana, CA) is a 27-person, AS9100D-certified precision machining company. It operates a 12,000 sq ft facility with 5-axis and 4-axis CNC mills (Doosan, Mori Seiki). Its programs include the F-35 Joint Strike Fighter, JASSM Missile Program, B2 Bomber, Airbus A350, and Rolls Royce components. Primary customers: Parker Aerospace (60%), Meggitt (20%), Ontic/JASSM (20%). The Parker contract alone generates ~$4M/year and runs through Feb 2028.

The Core Strategic Logic
  • AADYAH's India-based space IP needs US-domiciled manufacturing for US federal/DoD contracts ("Buy American" rules)
  • Organic US entry takes 3–5 years for certifications, clearances, and supply chain credibility
  • ABM provides instant AS9100D qualification (since 2013), program access (F-35/JASSM/B2), and California NewSpace geography (SpaceX, Rocket Lab, Raytheon within 60 miles)
  • India engineering + US manufacturing = claimed 3x cost advantage vs. Western-only competitors
  • FOCI-compliant proxy board (led by Dr. Andrew Palowitch, ex-NRO) enables ITAR-cleared classified DoD programs

Problem Statement

The global space supply chain suffers from: siloed vendors with poor integration support, 12–18 month lead times, no co-design capability, and painful customization. Only 6 countries have design-to-launch expertise. The US (35% of global space market) requires US-domiciled entities for federal contracts, creating a structural barrier for Indian space tech companies.

Solution

A US-India corridor space supply chain: AADYAH provides engineering depth (ISRO pedigree, 30+ years IP), ABM provides US manufacturing presence, certifications, and DoD program access. The combined entity offers co-design, customization, and faster iteration cycles at lower cost than pure-Western alternatives.

Porter's Five Forces Analysis
1. Competitive Rivalry
MEDIUM — Manageable

The precision aerospace machining market is fragmented with hundreds of small-to-mid-size shops in California. However, ABM's specific program certifications (F-35, JASSM, B2) create high switching costs for customers. Program relationships take 10+ years to establish. New entrants cannot easily displace ABM on existing programs. Rivalry intensifies as DoD programs attract more bidders. AADYAH's India integration angle creates a unique cost-engineering position few US-only shops can match.

2. Threat of New Entrants
LOW — High Barriers

Entry barriers in AS9100D-certified aerospace machining are extremely high: 2–5 years to obtain certifications, significant capex (5-axis CNC equipment costs $500K–$2M+ per machine), ITAR registration requirements, and years of track record needed to become an approved vendor for prime contractors. DoD programs like F-35 have additional supplier qualification processes that can take 3–5 years. The FOCI/proxy board compliance framework for classified DoD work is a particularly high barrier. ABM's 30-year track record and $11M backlog represent durable moats.

3. Bargaining Power of Suppliers
LOW-MEDIUM — Manageable

Raw material suppliers (aluminum, titanium, Inconel) are commodity markets. CNC equipment suppliers (Doosan, Mori Seiki, Mazak) are competitive. The primary supplier risk is engineering talent — aerospace machinists with 5-axis CNC experience and security clearances are in short supply in Southern California. The new GM/CEO hire is also a single point of dependency. AADYAH mitigates some supplier risk by vertically integrating ISRO-pedigreed engineering, but the US talent pool for cleared aerospace manufacturing is constrained and competitive.

4. Bargaining Power of Buyers
HIGH — Critical Risk

Parker Aerospace (60% of ABM revenue, ~$4M/year) has significant leverage. A single contract non-renewal in Feb 2028 would cut ABM revenue nearly in half. Meggitt (20%) and Ontic/JASSM (20%) provide some diversification, but the overall buyer concentration is extreme. Prime contractors like Lockheed Martin and Northrop Grumman also exert pricing pressure on their supply chains. AADYAH's plan to diversify through internal component machining and new DoD programs partially addresses this, but the 2026–2027 window before diversification is a vulnerability period. This is the highest risk factor in the deal.

5. Threat of Substitutes
LOW — Program Lock-In

For existing programs (F-35, JASSM, B2), substitution is extremely difficult. Defense programs use approved supplier lists; swapping a precision machining supplier requires re-qualification, which is costly and time-consuming for the prime contractor. For new business development, digital manufacturing technologies (additive manufacturing, automated precision machining) could theoretically substitute traditional CNC machining on certain components — but space-grade and defense-grade parts require tolerances and material properties that currently favor traditional precision CNC. Over the 2028–2030 horizon, additive manufacturing for structural aerospace components may increase, but this is a gradual risk rather than an immediate substitute threat.

Investment Recommendation
DEEPER LOOK

AADYAH North America presents a strategically interesting but operationally dependent investment opportunity. The underlying ABM Inc asset is real, profitable, and program-certified — it is not a pre-revenue startup. The strategic thesis (India engineering IP + US manufacturing presence = US DoD access at 3x cost advantage) is coherent and the timing (US-India iCET defense cooperation framework, Buy American tailwinds) is favorable.

However, the gap between ABM's current $6.8M revenue and the 2030 $59M target requires a chain of assumptions that each need to hold: (1) Parker contract renewal in Feb 2028, (2) FOCI/proxy board successfully approved by DCSA, (3) the unnamed GM/CEO hire is strong, (4) AADYAH India's Series A closes (providing parent financial strength), and (5) ITAR-cleared classified programs are actually won. Any one of these failing materially changes the return profile.

The $1M convertible note is a small check in absolute terms, which makes the due diligence burden asymmetric — the cost to investigate properly is proportional to the check size. We recommend proceeding to a deeper diligence phase before committing.

5 Key Questions Before Next Step

  1. GM/CEO hire status: Has the ABM General Manager been identified? Can we meet them? This is the single most important execution dependency — an unnamed hire should not close a $1M round.
  2. FOCI compliance plan: Has a US national security law firm (e.g., Covington & Burling, Jenner & Block) been retained to file the FOCI mitigation plan with DCSA? What is the timeline for the proxy board to be approved for classified work? This is a legal/regulatory risk, not a commercial one.
  3. ABM audited financials: Can we see 3 years of audited P&L, balance sheet, and cash flow statements for ABM Inc? Who performed the $5.3M enterprise valuation, and what was the methodology? Is there a Quality of Earnings report?
  4. Parker Aerospace contract: What is the status of renewal negotiations for the Parker contract (expires Feb 2028)? Is Parker aware of the ownership change? Do the contract terms allow for change-of-control notification or consent?
  5. Convertible note terms: What is the conversion cap, discount rate, interest rate, and maturity? What triggers conversion — a priced equity round at AADYAH NA level, or a liquidity event? How does this note rank vis-à-vis the ABM acquisition debt?
