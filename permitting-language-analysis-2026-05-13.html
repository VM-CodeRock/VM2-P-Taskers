LAN26214 — Permitting Language Analysis

BNATCS Environmental Waiver Authority & AIC Recommended Posture
Created: 2026-05-13  ·  Due: COB 2026-05-13
Executive Summary
Recommended Posture Support with Comments
LAN26214 is a Senate bill introduced by Chairman Ted Cruz (Senate Commerce Committee) that grants the FAA Administrator sweeping authority to waive major environmental and permitting statutes — including NEPA, ESA, Clean Air Act, and Clean Water Act — solely to advance the Brand New Air Traffic Control System (BNATCS). AIC should support the language publicly while flagging three targeted technical improvements.
  • What it does: Authorizes the FAA Administrator to waive 10 categories of federal environmental and permitting law exclusively for BNATCS projects, limits judicial review to constitutional challenges filed within 60 days in the D.C. Circuit, preempts state/tribal permitting conflicts, and sunsets on October 1, 2029 (with a carve-out for committed funding).
  • Why it matters now: Administrator Bedford has told Congress that without permitting relief, BNATCS will take "many more years" to complete. The AIC — specifically formed to support BNATCS implementation — is being asked directly by Cruz's Senate Commerce Committee staff to publicly endorse this language and provide technical assistance. This is a high-visibility moment for the coalition with the Administration's top aviation priority.
  • Recommended AIC position: Issue a written statement of support, offer technical assistance on three areas (tribal preemption language, noise statute scope, and judicial review exclusivity), and assign two board members to brief Bedford and Duffy directly. This locks in AIC as a credible ally without creating legal or reputational risk for member firms.
The Issue — What Is LAN26214?

LAN26214 is a Senate Legislative Counsel draft (119th Congress, 2d Session) introduced by Sen. Ted Cruz (R-TX), Chairman of the Senate Commerce, Science, and Transportation Committee. It is not yet assigned a bill number — the "LAN" prefix is a Senate Legislative Counsel designation indicating it is in draft/review status and being circulated to stakeholders before formal introduction and committee referral.

The bill adds a new § 40133 to Title 49 of the U.S. Code (chapter 401, FAA general powers), titled "Brand New Air Traffic Control System." Its operative provisions are as follows:

Waiver Authority (§ 40133(b))

The Administrator may waive compliance with the following statutes, on a project-by-project basis, by publishing a waiver in the Federal Register:

StatuteWhat It ProtectsWhy This Matters for BNATCS
NEPA (42 U.S.C. 4321) Environmental review / EIS requirements New ATC facilities, radar sites, and 6 new centers require NEPA review; litigation can add 2–5 years per site
Endangered Species Act (16 U.S.C. 1531) Habitat protection for listed species Radar siting near protected habitat; Bedford cited a North Carolina radar delayed by a bird's nest
National Historic Preservation Act (Title 54, Div. A, Sub. III) Section 106 tribal/historic site review Many FAA facilities near Native American sites requiring consultation under § 106
Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. 7401) Air quality permits for construction Construction of 6 new ATC centers requires permits in multiple non-attainment zones
AIM Act / HFCs (42 U.S.C. 7675) Hydrofluorocarbon phase-down HVAC systems in new ATC facilities; affects procurement timelines
Migratory Bird Treaty Act (16 U.S.C. 703) Migratory bird protection Radar tower construction can disrupt nesting/migration routes
49 U.S.C. § 303 Section 4(f) — parks, refuges, historic sites Real property acquisition near parks/refuges for new ATC facilities
Clean Water Act (33 U.S.C. 1251, §§ 402/404) Water discharge / wetland fill permits New center construction may require wetland delineation and 404 permits
40 U.S.C. § 3312(c)–(e) Historic preservation in federal buildings Renovation of legacy ATC facilities; GSA-style architectural review requirements
Aircraft Noise Statutes Noise impact requirements Airspace redesign / PBN procedures may alter noise exposure — this provision enables that redesign without community litigation

Key Procedural Features

  • Federal Register trigger: Waivers take effect immediately upon publication — no waiting period, no comment period required.
  • Judicial review strictly limited: Only constitutional challenges may be filed. Statutory and regulatory claims are jurisdictionally barred. All claims must be filed within 60 days and go directly to the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals (bypassing district courts).
  • State/tribal preemption: States, localities, tribes, and other governmental entities are barred from applying or enforcing the listed statutes against BNATCS projects.
  • Sunset clause: Authority terminates October 1, 2029, except for projects with committed FAA funding as of that date.
  • Rule of construction: No other law can modify this section unless it explicitly cites this provision — a "super-preemption" clause designed to prevent accidental statutory conflict.
Plain-Language Bottom Line This bill gives the FAA Administrator a master key to bypass virtually all environmental permitting for BNATCS for approximately 3.5 years (through October 2029). It is the single most aggressive permitting relief measure ever proposed for a civilian aviation project. The scope is intentionally broad — reflecting Congress's judgment that the current permitting regime is fundamentally incompatible with a 3-year modernization timeline.
Political Implications

Who Is Driving This and Why Now

This bill originates from Sen. Ted Cruz's Senate Commerce Committee staff (Andrew Miller, Policy Director for Aviation and Space), working directly with Gene Hayman (CEO, Stratify Aerospace) and Scott Brenner at the Aviation Innovation Coalition. The timing is deliberate: the BNATCS program is operational (Peraton selected as Integrator in December 2025; $12.5 billion down payment in the One Big Beautiful Bill), but the next phase — building 6 new ATC centers, replacing 618 radars, redesigning airspace — will run directly into environmental review bottlenecks. The Senate Commerce Committee wants this language embedded in an upcoming vehicle before recess.

Legislative Vehicle and Timing

The bill has not yet been formally introduced (no Senate number assigned). Gene Hayman's email to AIC board members (May 12) indicates Congress wants the coalition's endorsement quickly — suggesting the language may be attached to a markup or reconciliation vehicle imminently. The FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 (P.L. 118-63) was already signed, so this is likely a standalone bill or an attachment to a spending or reconciliation package rather than a reauthorization vehicle.

Key Political Risk for Changeis/AIC The aircraft noise waiver provision (§ 40133(b)(1)(J)) is the most politically explosive element. Airspace redesign near major metro areas has generated intense community opposition (see: FAA NextGen litigation in NY, DC, Phoenix, Chicago). Supporting this provision publicly could draw criticism from affected communities. AIC should note this in its technical comments while endorsing the bill's core framework.
FAA / DOT Alignment Check

Administrator Bedford's Position

In December 2025 testimony before the House T&I Subcommittee on Aviation, Administrator Bedford stated: "President Trump presented a bold vision for a brand-new air traffic control system within the next three years and our work to do that is already underway." He has briefed the Senate Commerce Committee directly and stated that "without permitting relief the BNATCS project would take many more years to complete." The language in LAN26214 is a direct legislative response to Bedford's testimony. Supporting it is supporting Bedford's stated program priorities.

Bedford has also emphasized safety urgency (post-DCA crash), accelerated hiring (2,026 controllers in FY25), and a compressed 3-year modernization timeline. LAN26214 removes the legal barriers that would prevent that timeline from being achievable. Bedford needs this or equivalent legislation to succeed.

Secretary Duffy's Position

BNATCS is Secretary Duffy's signature initiative since his May 8, 2025 announcement. Duffy has positioned it as "a once-in-a-generation opportunity" and an "economic and national security necessity." The $12.5 billion One Big Beautiful Bill funding, the Peraton Integrator contract (December 2025), and the 3-year timeline are all Duffy-led. LAN26214 is the permitting companion to that funding — without it, the program is resource-rich but legally constrained. Supporting LAN26214 is operationally synonymous with supporting Duffy's core DOT agenda.

Trump Administration EO Context

The Trump Administration has issued multiple executive orders on permitting reform, NEPA streamlining, and infrastructure acceleration. LAN26214 is consistent with — and arguably required by — the Administration's stated permitting reform goals. Opposing or hedging on this language would position AIC against both the Administration's executive priorities and its specific legislative agenda. There is no political benefit to a qualified opposition posture from Changeis's perspective.

Alignment Assessment LAN26214 is directly aligned with Bedford's testimony, Duffy's program agenda, and the Trump administration's permitting reform EOs. Supporting it carries minimal political risk with the Administration and maximum relationship value with the FAA and DOT leadership teams.
Recommended AIC Board Response
AIC Board Posture Support with Technical Comments
Publicly endorse the bill's framework and intent; offer three targeted technical improvements. Do not oppose, abstain, or offer qualified support premised on major structural changes. Gene Hayman's characterization is correct: this is a non-partisan infrastructure safety issue that AIC was specifically formed to support.

Suggested Letter Language (for Gene Hayman to send on behalf of AIC Board)

Opening / Policy Statement
"The Aviation Innovation Coalition strongly supports the permitting reform provisions in LAN26214. The current environmental review framework was not designed for a time-sensitive, safety-driven modernization program of this scope. Administrator Bedford has been direct with Congress: without permitting relief, the BNATCS timeline is not achievable. We endorse the FAA Administrator's waiver authority as proposed and urge the Committee to advance this language at the earliest opportunity."
Technical Comment 1 — Tribal Consultation Language
"We recommend clarifying subsection (c) (Preemption) to distinguish between tribal sovereign immunity rights — which flow from treaty obligations and may carry constitutional dimensions — and purely statutory tribal consultation requirements under NHPA § 106. A narrowing construction that preserves good-faith consultation processes (without creating litigation delay) would improve the bill's durability against constitutional challenge."
Technical Comment 2 — Noise Statute Scope
"Subsection (b)(1)(J) — waiving 'any provision of law relating to aircraft noise, as determined by the Administrator' — is written as an open-ended Administrator determination. We recommend specifying that this applies to airspace redesign and performance-based navigation procedures under subsection (a)(2)(D), to reduce ambiguity and limit constitutional due process challenges from affected communities."
Technical Comment 3 — Federal Register Process
"We support waiver effectiveness upon Federal Register publication but recommend adding a requirement for a brief administrative record (even a 1–2 page determination) to support the constitutional review standard in subsection (b)(3)(A). This strengthens the government's position in any D.C. Circuit constitutional challenge by demonstrating reasoned decision-making."

Key Talking Points for Board Members

  • Safety is the North Star: BNATCS was born from the DCA crash. Every month of delay is a safety risk. Permitting reform accelerates safety — opposing it prioritizes process over lives.
  • Scope is appropriately limited: This is not a blanket NEPA repeal. It applies exclusively to BNATCS and sunsets in 2029. The judicial review provision (constitutional challenges in 60 days, D.C. Circuit) still provides meaningful oversight.
  • The industry asked for this: Aviation stakeholders have long cited permitting delays as a barrier to infrastructure investment. This is the government responding to what AIC and its predecessor coalitions have requested for years.
  • AIC's founding purpose: The AIC website states its mission is to "help define the future architecture for the National Airspace System" in support of BNATCS. Endorsing the legislative tool that enables BNATCS is the most direct expression of that mission.
  • Non-partisan frame: Infrastructure modernization, aviation safety, and ATC reliability are non-partisan. NATCA (labor), A4A (airlines), ACI-NA (airports), and AAAE have all supported BNATCS investment. This is an all-of-industry issue.

Risks & Counterarguments to Anticipate

What Opponents Will Argue

CounterargumentAIC Response
"This sets a precedent for eliminating environmental review for all infrastructure." BNATCS-specific, time-limited (sunsets 2029), and the bill's rule of construction expressly limits the waiver authority to BNATCS. This is not general NEPA repeal.
"Tribal consultation rights are treaty obligations that cannot be waived by statute." This is a legitimate constitutional concern — it is one of AIC's three technical comments. Narrowing the tribal preemption language reduces this risk without changing the bill's core effect.
"Airspace redesign will harm noise-impacted communities without any process." The noise provision is the most contestable. AIC should argue that airspace redesign is a safety improvement that historically has been blocked by targeted litigation from wealthy airport communities, not affected low-income communities.
"The 60-day filing window is too short for communities to mount a constitutional challenge." 60 days is consistent with other administrative law filing windows (e.g., APA review). Constitutional challenges do not require discovery; 60 days is facially reasonable.
"Peraton has no civilian aviation experience — this waiver just speeds a flawed program." AIC's position is on permitting reform, not contractor selection. These are separate debates. BNATCS scope and Integrator qualifications are a distinct policy question.
"Environmental review requirements exist to protect communities — bypassing them is discriminatory." Argue that the current permitting regime creates environmental injustice by delaying safety improvements that protect all communities equally. The DCA crash was an environmental justice failure — inadequate ATC infrastructure kills passengers and communities near airports.
Changeis-Specific Risk Changeis works with the FAA and DOT on existing and future contracts. Supporting this legislation publicly carries essentially zero downside risk with the Administration. The only risk is being seen as insufficiently supportive — i.e., hedging when the Administration's top priority is on the line. Full public support for the core framework, with constructive technical comments, is the optimal position.
