1. Executive Summary
  • The strategic direction is sound; the technical claims need calibration. Q-NAS correctly identifies a real and urgent convergence of quantum-enabled optimization and post-quantum cryptography (PQC) in air traffic control, and its framing around the "Three C's" (Complexity, Congestion, Cybersecurity) aligns with FAA's own language in the PQC RFI 697DCK-26-RFI-PQC and the BNATCS program. However, several quantitative claims ("in seconds," "10–15% CO2 reduction," "15–20% taxi time reduction") lack citations and overstate current NISQ-era hardware capabilities.
  • PQC is the strongest and most immediately actionable angle. NIST finalized FIPS 203/204/205 in August 2024; FAA issued a formal RFI for PQC migration of NAS/ATC systems in March 2026, naming BNATCS, SWIM, ERAM, TBO, Data Comm, and STARS. Peraton (BNATCS prime) is a natural teaming target — Kathryn Maxwell relationship is directly relevant here.
  • Changeis is well-positioned as an integrator, not a hardware vendor. The value chain gap between quantum hardware R&D (D-Wave, IBM, Google) and FAA program execution is exactly where Changeis can insert: program management, integration architecture, cyber/PQC migration support, digital twin modeling, and RFI response drafting.
  • Three immediate bid plays emerge: (1) PQC sub-role under a BNATCS/FENS integrator, aligned with faa-atlas (AWS partnership); (2) FAA SBIR FY26 Phase I on hybrid quantum-classical ATM optimization proof-of-concept; (3) Probity3+AVMGT+Changeis teaming MOU for a strategic quantum aviation studies contract or OTA.
  • The paper needs six substantive edits before publication — garbled sentences, missing citations, no NISQ-era limitations discussion, no FAA-specific program names, no DO-178C/certification implications, and no clear "ask" of the FAA audience. Changeis can offer these edits as a value-add to solidify the teaming relationship with Reid and David.
  • Today's 12 PM CCC lunch with Brian Pickerall and Christopher Hill is a natural touchpoint to soft-brief Q-NAS framing and gauge FAA appetite for quantum pilot studies — particularly surface management optimization at ORD/JFK/ATL where TFDM deployment creates a real integration hook.
2. Paper Validity Assessment

Each major technical claim is assessed against current peer-reviewed literature and program status as of May 2026.

Claim Rating Assessment & Evidence
Quantum annealing solves combinatorial ATC problems "in seconds" Mixed Technically directionally accurate but contextually misleading for 2026 operational use. D-Wave Advantage (5,000+ qubit annealer) does solve QUBO problems in microseconds, but problem sizes that map to real NAS sectors (hundreds of aircraft, thousands of constraints) require decomposition into sub-problems of ~100–200 binary variables — which is far smaller than a realistic sector. A 2025 PMC review of NISQ-era optimization confirms: "achieving a definitive quantum advantage over classical methods for practical problems remains unresolved." A December 2024 arXiv QAOA paper on the Tail Assignment Problem found QA stable up to ~14 routes but not competitive with classical branch-and-price for larger instances. The paper should say "sub-second on decomposed sub-problems in current hardware; full-sector capability projected within 5–7 years."
QAOA for flight path optimization — research/peer-reviewed status Mixed QAOA is actively researched but not operationally proven for ATC-scale problems. The December 2024 TAP paper (arXiv:2412.12773) applied QAOA to real-world airline scheduling instances and found it "capable of distinguishing optimal from suboptimal solutions" on small instances (up to 14 routes) but showing "a significant increase in TTS [time-to-solution] as problem size grows" — scaling worse than classical branch-and-price. The April 2025 quantum airline scheduling paper corroborates this. QAOA is at TRL 3–4 for ATC applications. The paper overstates by not distinguishing demo scale from operational scale.
10–15% CO2 reduction per flight hour in congested sectors Weak No source is cited in the paper. Comparable figures from FAA/EUROCONTROL TBO and 4D-trajectory studies suggest 2–8% fuel savings from performance-based navigation improvements in high-density corridors — not 10–15%. NASA's ARAT tool (ML-based rerouting, Mosaic ATM/NASA 2024) delivers "measurable fuel savings" without quantifying 10–15%. The EUROCONTROL Network 4DT CONOPS projects 3–5% fuel reduction from network-wide 4D trajectory optimization. The 10–15% figure conflates potential efficiency gains across all optimization layers; it needs disaggregation and a citation.
15–20% taxi time reduction from quantum surface optimization Weak No source is cited. Actual ASDE-X-enabled surface management trials at JFK (MITRE, 2011) demonstrated improved taxi-out prediction accuracy but reported nominal taxi time in the 7–15 minute range with incremental improvements — not 15–20% reduction. The BQP quantum ATC overview cites up to 15% delays reduction but attributes it to the OneSky UAM pilot in Japan — not peer-reviewed, not applicable to legacy hub airports. The estimate is plausible in theory (quantum annealing is well-suited to sequencing) but has zero empirical backing from real airports. Must be qualified as a theoretical estimate.
PQC for satellite-based navigation/communication — NIST/FAA status Strong This is the paper's most well-grounded claim. NIST finalized FIPS 203 (ML-KEM), FIPS 204 (ML-DSA), and FIPS 205 (SLH-DSA) on August 13, 2024. FAA issued a formal RFI (697DCK-26-RFI-PQC, March 2026) explicitly citing BNATCS, SWIM, TBO, Data Comm, ADS-B, and WAAS as PQC migration targets. The FAA's language mirrors the paper exactly: "PQC not as a compliance exercise, but as a foundational enabler of modernization." The "Harvest Now, Decrypt Later" threat is specifically named. The paper correctly identifies urgency but does not cite any of these sources, missing an opportunity to demonstrate credibility to an FAA audience.
Roadmap phases — realistic given current quantum hardware (NISQ era)? Mixed The three phases (0–2 yr pilots; 1–3 yr digital twins; 3+ yr live quantum TFM) are conceptually reasonable but lack specificity. Phase 1 PQC audits are immediately executable — FAA has already scoped this. Phase 2 "hybrid digital twin" work is plausible given platforms like AWS Braket and Azure Quantum; IBM's quantum aerospace report (2024) validates digital twin use cases. Phase 3 live quantum TFM by year 3 is over-optimistic; IBM and Google's fault-tolerant quantum roadmaps target commercially relevant problem sizes no earlier than 2028–2030. No budget figures, milestones, decision gates, or program office owners are named. A serious paper for an FAA audience needs to reference specific budget lines (F&E accounts) and BNATCS implementation windows.

5. What the Paper Gets Right
  • The "Three C's" framing (Complexity, Congestion, Cybersecurity) is strategically sharp and maps directly to FAA's own language in the BNATCS fact sheet and PQC RFI. This is a compelling organizing framework that will resonate with an FAA policy audience.
  • The combinatorial explosion problem statement is technically accurate. Classical ATC systems do face exponential scaling challenges as UAM, UAS, and commercial space operations multiply. The QUBO formulation is the correct technical paradigm and is consistent with published quantum ATC research.
  • PQC urgency is correctly timed and well-framed. The paper's warning about "Harvest Now, Decrypt Later" threats to ADS-B and GPS-based navigation is validated by the FAA's own March 2026 RFI, which uses identical language. This section will immediately resonate with FAA cybersecurity leadership.
  • The "avoiding technological debt" argument in Section 5 is the strongest strategic point in the paper. The hybrid adoption argument — quantum handling optimization sub-routines within classical systems — is technically sound, matches IBM's stated aerospace strategy, and gives FAA a risk-acceptable entry path without requiring a full rip-and-replace.
  • Commercial space + UAM integration framing is timely. FAA's BNATCS plan explicitly addresses these "new entrants." The paper's framing of Mach 25 spacecraft and drone volume creating routing conflicts is operationally accurate and not widely discussed in quantum aviation literature.
6. What the Paper Needs Before Publication

Specific line-level issues and structural gaps that should be addressed before distributing to FAA contacts:

  • Garbled Text — Line 4 (Section 1.2)
    "Current Air Traffic Control (ATC) systems rely on [blank] to manage aircraft separation" — the technology being relied on (radar, STARS, ERAM) is missing. Also: "computers struggle with 'combinatorial explosions'" — missing subject (classical computers? legacy systems?).
  • Garbled Text — Line 6 (Section 2.0)
    "T Flow Management: uantum annealing..." — should be "Traffic Flow Management: Quantum annealing..." Capital letters dropped, likely a copy-paste formatting artifact.
  • Garbled Text — Line 8 (Section 2.0)
    "ptimization of 4D trajectories inimiz holding patternsirectly reduc CO2 emissions by what some estimate 10-15%" — multiple words missing. Should read: "Optimization of 4D trajectories minimizes holding patterns and directly reduces CO2 emissions by an estimated 10–15% [CITATION NEEDED] per flight hour in congested sectors."
  • Garbled Text — Lines 13–14 (Section 2.1)
    "continually add ground vehicles (emergency and maintenance)thousands of dronesUrban Air Mobility (UAM) vehicles into low-altitude..." — missing "As we" at the start, missing commas and articles throughout. Needs full rewrite.
  • Garbled Text — Line 14
    "NextGen was built that fly at similar speeds" — missing subject phrase, likely "NextGen was built for aircraft that fly at similar speeds."
  • Missing Citations — Every Quantitative Claim
    "10–15% CO2 reduction," "15–20% taxi time reduction," "solve in seconds," and "billions annually" in delays have zero in-text citations. For an FAA audience, these claims without sources will undermine credibility immediately. Recommend citing: FAA Performance Report, EUROCONTROL 4DT CONOPS, D-Wave published benchmarks, and BTS airline delay cost data.
  • Missing — NISQ-Era Hardware Limitations
    The paper presents quantum computing as ready-to-deploy today. There is zero discussion of decoherence, gate error rates, qubit connectivity constraints, or the current limit of ~100–200 binary variables for QUBO problems on available hardware. An FAA technical reviewer will reject any paper that omits this — it signals the authors are not technically credible. Add a "Current Hardware Status" section or callout box acknowledging NISQ constraints and hybrid approaches.
  • Missing — FAA Program Names
    The paper references NextGen generically but does not name BNATCS, SWIM, ERAM, TBO/4D-TBO, TFDM, STARS, or the PQC RFI — all of which are directly in scope. An FAA reader will immediately wonder whether the authors are current on the agency's actual modernization portfolio. These names should appear in Sections 2.1, 4.0, and 5.0.
  • Missing — Cybersecurity Certification Implications
    Any quantum-enabled or PQC-enabled component in the NAS must be certified under DO-178C (software), DO-278A (CNS/ATM systems), and NIST SP 800-53. The paper does not mention FAA certification pathways at all — a significant gap for a technical audience. The FAA PQC RFI explicitly cites DO-178C/DO-254/DAL compliance as requirements for all PQC vendors.
  • Missing — Clear "Ask" of FAA
    The conclusion calls for "early adoption" but never specifies what action FAA should take: fund a pilot study? Issue an RFI? Convene a working group? Join a coalition? Papers without a clear call-to-action are filed and forgotten. The paper needs a specific, bounded ask — e.g., "FAA should establish a Quantum-Enabled NAS Pilot Program under the Airspace Modernization Office (Reauthorization Sec. 207) with $5M Phase I funding for hybrid quantum-classical surface management demonstration at three high-density airports."
  • Missing — Roadmap Budget, Milestones, Decision Gates
    The three-phase roadmap in Section 4.0 has no funding estimates, no milestones, no decision criteria for progressing from Phase 1 to Phase 2, and no program office ownership. For an FAA audience, this reads as aspirational rather than executable. Add at minimum: rough order of magnitude costs, "go/no-go" criteria at each phase transition, and the relevant FAA office or ATO directorate responsible.
7. Recommended FAA-Specific Examples to Add

Per David Rhodes' request for more FAA-specific content, these examples would significantly strengthen the paper's credibility with an FAA/ATO audience:

NextGen Pain Point: TFM-S Weather Delays

The Traffic Flow Management System (TFMS) — built on 1960s-era technology per the BNATCS Plan (2025) — cannot dynamically re-optimize the NAS-wide flow during major weather events. During convective weather in the summer of 2024, the FAA reported equipment-related delays at ~300% above the 2010–2024 average. A quantum annealer running QUBO-formulated re-routing could serve as a real-time adjunct to TFMS during ground delay programs.

Surface Management: ORD/JFK/ATL

TFDM is being deployed at high-density airports to replace paper flight strips. The surface sequencing problem at ORD (300+ daily operations, 8 runways) involves thousands of taxi routing combinations — precisely the combinatorial problem quantum annealing excels at. Citing the specific airport and the TFDM rollout schedule gives the paper an immediately testable, bounded use case that a real FAA program manager can act on.

BNATCS + PQC: Specific Hook

The FAA PQC RFI 697DCK-26-RFI-PQC (March 2026) explicitly states: "FAA therefore views PQC not as a compliance exercise, but as a foundational enabler of modernization — one that must be embedded into every vendor solution, every system upgrade, and every step of BNATCS." Citing this directly in the white paper would demonstrate that Q-NAS is not ahead of the curve — it is in lockstep with the FAA's own stated strategy.

AAM Integration Use Case

FAA's AAM Implementation Plan v1.0 identifies low-altitude airspace corridors (below 1,200 ft AGL in urban areas) as the most computationally demanding integration challenge. UAM vehicles flying at 60–150 mph in dense urban corridors create conflict geometries classical separation logic cannot handle in real-time. A quantum-enabled Urban Air Mobility Traffic Management (UATM) use case — potentially piloted in one of the five AAM corridors under study — would give the paper a concrete, fundable proof of concept.

NASA + Rigetti / IBM ATM Precedent

NASA's Ames Research Center and Quantum Artificial Intelligence Laboratory (QuAIL) have published studies on quantum optimization for gate scheduling and ramp control. IBM's 2024 quantum aerospace report identifies ATM as a top-3 near-term application area. Citing specific NASA/IBM prior work gives the paper academic grounding and positions Q-NAS as a logical next step in an established research arc rather than a novel speculation.

FAA Reauthorization 2024 — Airspace Modernization Office

Section 207 of the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 established a new Airspace Modernization Office within FAA responsible for NAS modernization, advanced technology integration, and coordination of emerging entrants. This office is the natural organizational home for a Q-NAS pilot program and should be named explicitly as the recommended program champion.
