Scope & Objectives: The audit (Project ID FI2026023) assessed whether FAA (1) has selected and implemented the required high-impact baseline security controls for its 45 high-impact NAS systems, and (2) is mitigating potential vulnerabilities for those systems. Audit fieldwork ran from October 2024 through January 2026. The OIG reviewed System Security Plans (SSPs), interviewed FAA officials, and analyzed data from both FAA's internal SMART tool and DOT's official CSAM system of record.

Why This Audit Matters Now: The 2021 predecessor audit (OIG IT2021033) found FAA had recategorized 45 ATO systems from low/moderate to high-impact but was not holding system owners accountable for remediating baseline control gaps. This 2026 audit is the follow-up accountability check — 4.5 years after reclassification — and finds persistent, material gaps. The systems support four NAS operational capabilities: automation, communication, navigation & weather, and surveillance. A successful cyberattack on any of these systems could have severe or catastrophic effects on U.S. air travel.

FAA Response Posture: FAA concurred with all four recommendations and committed to full implementation by December 31, 2026. FAA attributed gaps to technical challenges, legacy system constraints, funding limitations, and limited operational resources.

Top 7 Specific Findings (with Verbatim Report Language)

Finding 1
15 of 45 Systems Still Operating Under Outdated NIST SP 800-53 Rev 4

FAA has made progress — 30 of 45 systems selected high baseline controls under Rev 5 — but 15 systems remain on the older standard, which requires only 343 controls vs. the 370 required under Rev 5.

"We found 30 of the 45 systems had selected high baseline controls under NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 — NIST's most recent applicable standards — which requires the selection of 370 high security controls. However, 15 systems had security controls selected under the outdated NIST SP 800-53 Rev 4, which required 343 high baseline security controls. As a result, these systems continue to be vulnerable because they are not meeting current Federal requirements under NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 and NIST SP 800-37 Rev 2." — OIG Report FI2026023, p. 6

By capability: Automation — 14 systems total, 15 documented under Rev 4; Communication — 11 systems, 9 under Rev 4; Navigation & Weather — 3 systems, 2 under Rev 4; Surveillance — 17 systems, 14 under Rev 4. (Note: Some systems span multiple categories per SSP documentation graphic.)

Finding 2
1,836 Required Controls Not Fully Implemented (11.3% of Total)

Of 16,245 total required controls across the 45 systems, 1,836 remain unimplemented — comprising 717 documented as "not implemented" (entirely absent or untested) and 1,119 documented as "planned" (scheduled but not yet in place).

"According to the required system documentation we reviewed, FAA had not fully implemented 1,836 selected controls (11.3 percent) of the 16,245 required controls for the 45 systems, including 717 that were documented as not implemented and 1,119 that were documented as planned. 'Not implemented' denotes that the specified security control is entirely absent, has not been tested, or only exists in a form that fails to satisfy the required [standards]." — OIG Report FI2026023, p. 7–8

This gap means that controls covering penetration testing, supply chain protection, and access management — high-priority safeguards for air traffic systems — are absent or untested on NAS-critical infrastructure.

Finding 3
84% of Systems Have Outdated System Security Plan (SSP) Documentation

For 38 of the 45 systems, FAA's SSP documentation does not accurately reflect current security posture. NIST 800-37 Rev 2 requires system owners to update documentation as controls are implemented — but FAA left them as "planned" or "not implemented" even where implementations had occurred.

"For 38 of the 45 (84 percent) high-impact systems, FAA did not update documentation for the required high baseline security controls. Instead, FAA documented some of the controls in the SSPs as planned or not implemented... Nevertheless, not addressing the need for selecting, implementing, and sufficiently documenting all required high baseline security controls for these high-impact systems may affect FAA's ability to maintain and protect these critical systems." — OIG Report FI2026023, p. 8–9
Finding 4
FAA Uses SMART Instead of DOT's Official CSAM System of Record for Vulnerability Tracking

FAA tracks vulnerabilities in its own internal Security Management & Assessment Reporting Tool (SMART) rather than the DOT-wide Cyber Security Assessment & Management (CSAM) system, which is the departmental system of record required by both DOT and FAA policy.

"FAA is not tracking and mitigating vulnerabilities within DOT's system of record, as required. As a result, FAA is not being fully transparent with the Department in identifying its vulnerabilities... FAA officials use an internal tracking system, Security Management & Assessment Reporting Tool (SMART), as the primary source for documenting, tracking, and mitigating potential vulnerabilities for FAA systems. According to FAA, the Agency's deficiencies of tracking and documenting vulnerabilities within CSAM are due to lack of funding, technical issues with upgrading legacy systems, and limited operational resources." — OIG Report FI2026023, p. 10
Finding 5
Vulnerabilities Not Tracked in CSAM Span All Four NAS Operational Capability Groups

The audit found untracked vulnerabilities across all capability groups: all systems supporting Automation, Communication, and Navigation & Weather had vulnerabilities not reported in CSAM. For Surveillance, 14 of 17 systems had untracked vulnerabilities.

"We identified potential high-impact baseline security control assessment vulnerabilities that were not being tracked in CSAM. For example, we found that all systems supporting Automation, Communication, and Navigation and Weather capabilities had vulnerabilities that were not reported, tracked, and mitigated. For the Surveillance capability, 14 of the 17 systems had vulnerabilities that were not reported, tracked, and mitigated." — OIG Report FI2026023, p. 11
Finding 6
FAA's Manual SMART-to-CSAM Transfer Causes Significant Delays in Departmental Transparency

Because CSAM cannot pull data directly from SMART, FAA must manually re-enter vulnerability data — a process FAA itself acknowledges causes "significant delay." As of May 2025, FAA stated it was working with the Department to automate the transfer.

"FAA officials further acknowledged that their use of SMART has caused a significant delay in the reporting of vulnerabilities within CSAM due to having to input vulnerabilities within CSAM manually. In May 2025, FAA officials stated that they are working with the Department to automate a process for transferring vulnerabilities from SMART to CSAM." — OIG Report FI2026023, p. 10–11

This lack of transparency means DOT cannot identify common threats across FAA systems or provide comprehensive IT weakness tracking and reporting.

Finding 7
Gaps Attributed to Technical Challenges and Funding — Not Negligence Alone

FAA's root cause framing: legacy system complexity (many NAS systems are decades old), technical constraints in applying modern security standards to operational infrastructure, funding limitations, and workforce/resource constraints in the ATO Cybersecurity Group. This positions any remediation as a resource-intensive modernization challenge, not a simple policy fix.

"According to FAA, these gaps exist in part because of technical and other challenges with FAA's systems. Until these gaps are filled, these systems may be vulnerable to cyberattacks that could cause severe or catastrophic effects on the NAS." — OIG Report FI2026023, Highlights / p. 9

All 4 Recommendations — With FAA Responses

# Recommendation FAA Response Target Date
1 Identify all NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 high baseline security controls not yet selected and implemented; conduct a security impact analysis to document potential security ramifications; develop Plans of Action & Milestones (POA&Ms) for unimplemented controls across the 45 systems. Concur Dec 31, 2026
2 Identify and update System Security Plan (SSP) documentation that contains outdated NIST SP 800-53 Rev 4 security controls; update all SSP documentation and appendices to reflect the current selection and implementation status of security controls for all 45 systems. Concur Dec 31, 2026

FAA Response Posture: Full Concurrence "Based on our review of the draft report, we concur with the four recommendations as written and plan to implement them fully by December 31, 2026." — FAA response to OIG. This is a notably cooperative response, though the Dec 31, 2026 deadline for all four recs is an 8-month implementation window. OIG has designated all four as "Actions Required."

The "45 Systems" Question — Answered

Direct Answer to Varun's Question Yes — the "45 systems" in this 2026 report are the exact same 45 systems flagged in the 2021 predecessor audit. They are FAA Air Traffic Organization (ATO) systems that were recategorized from low/moderate-impact to high-impact between 2017 and December 2020. The recategorization triggered a requirement for more stringent NIST SP 800-53 "high baseline" security controls. The 2026 audit is a follow-up accountability check 4–5 years later.

What are these 45 systems? The specific names are listed in Exhibit C of the full report, but that exhibit is redacted in the public version (SSI). From the public text, we know they span four NAS operational capability groups:

Key context: FAA ATO began revalidating NAS systems after prior OIG and GAO audits documented that FAA had never rated any NAS critical infrastructure system as high-impact. The recategorization of 45 systems was a direct response to those audits, completed by December 2020. These systems were previously all categorized as low or moderate impact — despite providing safety-critical services like air traffic control and communications.

Linkage Analysis — Prior Reports & Audit Chain

This report is the third in a multi-year OIG/GAO audit chain targeting FAA NAS cybersecurity. Understanding the chain is critical to positioning Changeis on what comes next.

2020 — GAO
GAO-21-8: FAA Has Not Addressed Significant Cybersecurity Risks to Air Traffic Control Systems
GAO found FAA had not applied high-impact categorization to any NAS system. Recommended FAA develop a comprehensive cybersecurity risk management program. This audit catalyzed FAA's recategorization effort. GAO-21-8 ↗
Aug 2, 2021 — OIG
OIG Report No. IT2021033: FAA Is Taking Steps to Properly Categorize High-Impact Information Systems but Security Risks Remain
The direct predecessor. Found FAA had recategorized 45 ATO systems by Dec 2020, but: (1) used an outdated 2013 NAS Requirement Document and may be missing other high-impact systems; (2) lacked formalized policies for selecting and implementing high security controls; (3) had not completed the required gap analysis; (4) had not implemented enterprise security initiatives. Six recommendations issued, all concurred by FAA. OIG Library Item 38494 ↗
Sep 2024 — OIG Announcement
OIG Audit Announcement Memo: Cybersecurity Review of FAA's High-Impact Systems (Project No. 24T3004T000)
OIG formally announced this follow-up audit, noting that the 45 systems from 2021 "provide safety-critical services — such as for air traffic control — and in the event of system failure, would have adverse impact to FAA's mission." Fieldwork began September 2024. Announcement Memo ↗
Sep 23, 2024 — GAO
GAO-24-107001: Air Traffic Control — FAA Actions Are Urgently Needed to Modernize Aging Systems
Parallel GAO report examined all 138 FAA ATC systems. Found 51 (37%) "unsustainable" and 54 (39%) "potentially unsustainable." 15 systems had no modernization plan. Of 17 especially concerning systems, investments for 13 won't complete for at least 6 years. Seven recommendations issued. This report covers a different scope (all 138 ATC systems; sustainability/modernization focus) vs. the OIG's 45 high-impact security controls focus — but they are complementary and cite each other's findings. GAO-24-107001 ↗
Apr 1, 2026 — OIG (THIS REPORT)
OIG Report No. FI2026023: FAA Does Not Effectively Secure Its High-Impact Systems Supporting the National Airspace System
Same 45 systems. Same core problem. Persistent gaps. After 4+ years of "working on it," FAA still has: 15/45 systems on outdated NIST standards, 1,836 unimplemented controls, 38/45 systems with outdated SSP documentation, and a broken vulnerability tracking chain (SMART ≠ CSAM). Four new recommendations — all concurred. Implementation target: Dec 31, 2026.

Bottom Line on Persistence The core finding — FAA cannot fully secure its most critical NAS systems — is unchanged from 2021. Progress has been made (30/45 on Rev 5; majority of controls now selected) but at 4+ years post-recategorization and 11.3% of controls still unimplemented, the pace is inadequate for systems where a successful cyberattack could ground U.S. aviation. FAA's Dec 2026 deadline represents the agency's fourth commitment to close these gaps (2021 recommendations, 2024 audit launch, 2026 concurrence).
